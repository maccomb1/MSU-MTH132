    Section 3.1 Motion in Physics

    Objectives

    • Translate between physical, geometric, numerical, and algebraic meanings of the derivative.
      🔗
    • Interpret motion quantities \(s(t)\text{,}\) \(v(t)\text{,}\) \(a(t)\text{,}\) and \(j(t)\) using derivatives.
      🔗
    • Use graphs and tables to estimate rates of change and interpret what they mean in context.
      🔗
    🔗
    Mathematics solves problems partly with technical tools, but also by translating between different levels of meaning. Many problems begin in a physical or geometric setting. We often translate them into a numerical or algebraic setting where our tools apply, then translate the result back to the original context.
    🔗
    Our key concept so far has been the derivative. For a function \(y=f(x)\text{,}\) the derivative has several complementary meanings.
    🔗
    • Physical: \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=f'(x)\) is the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\text{.}\) For a specific input \(a\text{,}\) \(f'(a)\) describes how fast \(f(x)\) changes near \(x=a\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Geometric: \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line to the graph \(y=f(x)\) at \((a,f(a))\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Numerical: we approximate the derivative using a difference quotient
      \begin{equation*} f'(a)\cong \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
      where \(h\) is small. As \(\Delta x=h\to 0\text{,}\) the average rate of change approaches the instantaneous rate of change.
      🔗
    • Algebraic: we compute derivatives from formulas using derivative rules and known basic derivatives. Occasionally we return to the definition
      \begin{equation*} f'(a)=\displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}. \end{equation*}
      🔗

    Subsection The Basics of Motion

    Many applications of derivatives involve motion. In this section, all quantities are functions of time \(t\text{.}\) Here is a basic set of definitions:
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.1. Position, Velocity, Acceleration, and Jerk.

    Let \(s(t)\) denote the position of an object at time \(t\text{,}\) measured relative to a fixed reference point. The following quantities describe different aspects of the object’s motion:
    🔗
    • Position \(s(t)\text{:}\) the distance past a reference point (for example, in feet) at time \(t\) seconds.
      🔗
    • Velocity \(v(t)=\displaystyle \frac{ds}{dt}=s'(t)\text{:}\) the rate of change of position, measured in feet per second. Velocity may be negative if the object is moving in the negative direction. The speed of the object is the absolute value of velocity, \(|v(t)|\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Acceleration \(a(t)=\displaystyle \frac{dv}{dt}=\frac{d^2 s}{dt^2}=s''(t)\text{:}\) the rate of change of velocity, measured in feet per second squared. A useful benchmark is one gee, approximately \(32\) ft/sec\(^2\text{,}\) which is the acceleration due to gravity near Earth’s surface.
      🔗
    • Jerk \(j(t)=\displaystyle \frac{da}{dt}=\frac{d^3 s}{dt^3}=s'''(t)\text{:}\) the rate of change of acceleration, measured in feet per second cubed.
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.2.

    A ball is thrown straight up from a height of 6 feet with an initial velocity of 64 ft/sec. Its vertical position (height) at time \(t\) seconds is modeled by
    \begin{equation*} s(t)=-16t^2+64t+6\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (a)
    Find formulas for the ball’s velocity \(v(t)\) and acceleration \(a(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiating gives
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle v(t) = s'(t) = -32t + 64 \text{ ft/sec,} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) = -32 \text{ ft/sec}^2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    When is does the ball have a velocity of zero? What is special about that moment in terms of the ball’s height?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The ball has zero velocity when
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 0 = v(t) = -32t + 64. \end{equation*}
    Solving gives \(t=2\) seconds. At that time, the ball reaches its maximum height before falling back down.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    When does the ball hit the ground? What are its velocity and acceleration right before it strikes the ground?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The ball hits the ground when \(s(t)=0\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 0 = -16t^2 + 64t + 6. \end{equation*}
    Or equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 8t^2 - 32t - 3 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solving this quadratic equation (using the quadratic formula) gives
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{32 \pm \sqrt{(-32)^2 - 4 \cdot 8 \cdot (-3)}}{2 \cdot 8} = 2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Since time must be positive, we take the larger solution: \(t = 2 + \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}\) seconds (approximately 4.1 seconds).
    🔗
    The velocity just before impact is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle v\left(2 + \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}\right) \amp =\amp -32\left(2 + \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}\right) + 64\\ \amp =\amp -8\sqrt{70} \text{ ft/sec} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (approximately -67 ft/sec, indicating downward motion).
    🔗
    The acceleration is constant:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle a(t) = -32 \text{ ft/sec}^2 \end{equation*}
    at all times, including right before the ball strikes the ground.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.3.

    The graph of \(s(t)\) in Example 3.1.2 is a downward-opening parabola in \(t\text{,}\) representing height over time. It is not the full two-dimensional flight path of the ball, since horizontal motion is ignored in this model.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.4.

    Consider the velocity graph of a particle moving along a line given below.
    🔗
    Use this to answer the following questions:
    🔗
    (a)
    When is the particle moving at a constant speed? Explain how you know.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The particle is moving at a constant speed from time \(t=2\) to \(t=4\text{.}\) In interval notation this is \([2,4]\text{.}\) This is because the velocity graph is flat (horizontal) during this interval, indicating that the velocity is not changing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    When is the particle changing direction? Explain how you know.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The particle is changing direction at time \(t=5\text{.}\) This is because the velocity graph crosses the \(t\)-axis at this time, indicating that the velocity changes from positive to negative. A change in the sign of velocity indicates a change in direction of motion.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    What is the acceleration of the particle at time \(t=1\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The acceleration at time \(t=1\) is the slope of the velocity graph at that time. From the graph, we see that from \(t=0\) to \(t=2\text{,}\) the velocity decreases linearly from 4 to 1. The slope of this line segment is
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{1-4}{2-0} = \frac{-3}{2} = -1.5. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the acceleration at time \(t=1\) is \(-1.5\) units per second squared.
