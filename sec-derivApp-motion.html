    Section 3.1 Motion in Physics

    Objectives

    • Calculate velocity, acceleration, and jerk using derivatives. Include units and interpret results in real-world context.
      🔗
    • Move fluently between multiple representations of motion (formula, table, and graph), including estimating derivatives from graphs or tables and interpreting slopes as rates of change.
      🔗
    • Determine when an object is moving in the positive or negative direction by analyzing the sign of the velocity, and identify times when the object is at rest (\(v(t)=0\)).
      🔗
    • Use acceleration to describe how velocity is changing, and compute acceleration values either by differentiating \(v(t)\) or by finding slopes on a velocity graph.
      🔗
    • Decide when an object is speeding up or slowing down by comparing the signs of velocity and acceleration, and express the answer in interval notation.
      🔗
    • Distinguish between displacement and total distance traveled on a time interval and compute both quantities.
      🔗
    • Interpret and solve motion problems that require setting up and solving equations or inequalities (for example, finding when height is above a given value, when velocity is positive, or when speed exceeds a threshold).
      🔗
    🔗

    Warning 3.1.1. Prerequisite Review.

    In this section there are several types of problems that require us to be able to solve inequalities. Problems such as determining when an object is moving in the positive direction, or speeding up, or when it has a negative acceleration.
    🔗
    If you feel a bit out of practice with inequalities, it may be helpful to revisit those ideas via the Precalculus Review: Inequalities 0.4 section before proceeding.
    🔗
    🔗
    Mathematics solves problems partly with technical tools, but also by translating between different levels of meaning. Many problems begin in a physical or geometric setting. We often translate them into a numerical or algebraic setting where our tools apply, then translate the result back to the original context.
    🔗
    Our key concept so far has been the derivative. For a function \(y=f(x)\text{,}\) the derivative has several complementary meanings.
    🔗
    • Physical: \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=f'(x)\) is the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\text{.}\) For a specific input \(a\text{,}\) \(f'(a)\) describes how fast \(f(x)\) changes near \(x=a\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Geometric: \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line to the graph \(y=f(x)\) at \((a,f(a))\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Numerical: we approximate the derivative using a difference quotient
      \begin{equation*} f'(a)\cong \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
      where \(h\) is small. As \(\Delta x=h\to 0\text{,}\) the average rate of change approaches the instantaneous rate of change.
      🔗
    • Algebraic: we compute derivatives from formulas using derivative rules and known basic derivatives. Occasionally we return to the definition
      \begin{equation*} f'(a)=\displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}. \end{equation*}
      🔗

    Subsection The Basics of Motion

    Many applications of derivatives involve motion. In this section, all quantities are functions of time \(t\text{.}\) Here is a basic set of definitions:
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.2. Position, Velocity, Acceleration, and Jerk.

    Let \(s(t)\) denote the position of an object at time \(t\text{,}\) measured relative to a fixed reference point. The following quantities describe different aspects of the object’s motion:
    🔗
    • Position \(s(t)\text{:}\) the distance past a reference point (for example, in feet) at time \(t\) seconds.
      🔗
    • Velocity \(v(t)=\displaystyle \frac{ds}{dt}=s'(t)\text{:}\) the rate of change of position, measured in feet per second. Velocity may be negative if the object is moving in the negative direction. The speed of the object is the absolute value of velocity, \(|v(t)|\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Acceleration \(a(t)=\displaystyle \frac{dv}{dt}=\frac{d^2 s}{dt^2}=s''(t)\text{:}\) the rate of change of velocity, measured in feet per second squared. A useful benchmark is one gee, approximately \(32\) ft/sec\(^2\text{,}\) which is the acceleration due to gravity near Earth’s surface.
      🔗
    • Jerk \(j(t)=\displaystyle \frac{da}{dt}=\frac{d^3 s}{dt^3}=s'''(t)\text{:}\) the rate of change of acceleration, measured in feet per second cubed.
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.3.

    A ball is thrown straight up from a height of 6 feet with an initial velocity of 64 ft/sec. Its vertical position (height) at time \(t\) seconds is modeled by
    \begin{equation*} s(t)=-16t^2+64t+6\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (a)
    Find formulas for the ball’s velocity \(v(t)\) and acceleration \(a(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiating gives
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle v(t) = s'(t) = -32t + 64 \text{ ft/sec,} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) = -32 \text{ ft/sec}^2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    When is does the ball have a velocity of zero? What is special about that moment in terms of the ball’s height?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The ball has zero velocity when
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 0 = v(t) = -32t + 64. \end{equation*}
    Solving gives \(t=2\) seconds. At that time, the ball reaches its maximum height before falling back down.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    When does the ball hit the ground? What are its velocity and acceleration right before it strikes the ground?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The ball hits the ground when \(s(t)=0\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 0 = -16t^2 + 64t + 6. \end{equation*}
    Or equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 8t^2 - 32t - 3 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solving this quadratic equation (using the quadratic formula) gives
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{32 \pm \sqrt{(-32)^2 - 4 \cdot 8 \cdot (-3)}}{2 \cdot 8} = 2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Since time must be positive, we take the larger solution: \(t = 2 + \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}\) seconds (approximately 4.1 seconds).
    🔗
    The velocity just before impact is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle v\left(2 + \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}\right) \amp =\amp -32\left(2 + \frac{\sqrt{70}}{4}\right) + 64\\ \amp =\amp -8\sqrt{70} \text{ ft/sec} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (approximately -67 ft/sec, indicating downward motion).
    🔗
    The acceleration is constant:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle a(t) = -32 \text{ ft/sec}^2 \end{equation*}
    at all times, including right before the ball strikes the ground.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.4.

    The graph of \(s(t)\) in Example 3.1.3 is a downward-opening parabola in \(t\text{,}\) representing height over time. It is not the full two-dimensional flight path of the ball, since horizontal motion is ignored in this model.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.5.

    Consider the velocity graph of a particle moving along a line given below.
    🔗
    Use this to answer the following questions:
    🔗
    (a)
    When is the particle moving at a constant speed? Explain how you know.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The particle is moving at a constant speed from time \(t=2\) to \(t=4\text{.}\) In interval notation this is \([2,4]\text{.}\) This is because the velocity graph is flat (horizontal) during this interval, indicating that the velocity is not changing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    When is the particle changing direction? Explain how you know.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The particle is changing direction at time \(t=5\text{.}\) This is because the velocity graph crosses the \(t\)-axis at this time, indicating that the velocity changes from positive to negative. A change in the sign of velocity indicates a change in direction of motion.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    What is the acceleration of the particle at time \(t=1\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The acceleration at time \(t=1\) is the slope of the velocity graph at that time. From the graph, we see that from \(t=0\) to \(t=2\text{,}\) the velocity decreases linearly from 4 to 1. The slope of this line segment is
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{1-4}{2-0} = \frac{-3}{2} = -1.5. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the acceleration at time \(t=1\) is \(-1.5\) units per second squared.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we have reviewed some of the basic concepts of motion, let’s move on to some more advanced topics.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Speeding Up and Slowing Down

    Before we proceed, it is important to emphasize the difference between velocity and speed. An object is said to be speeding up when its speed is increasing, and slowing down when its speed is decreasing. Let’s consider the following example to illustrate this concept before we get our main rule for this section.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.7.

    Consider the following situations. In each case, determine whether the particle is speeding up or slowing down.
    🔗
    (a)
    The velocity of a particle is initially \(5\) m/sec and it increases to \(9\) m/sec.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since speed is the absolute value of velocity, the speed changes from \(|5|=5\) m/sec to \(|9|=9\) m/sec. Because the speed is increasing, the particle is speeding up.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    The velocity of a particle is initially \(9\) m/sec and it decreases to \(5\) m/sec.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since speed is the absolute value of velocity, the speed changes from \(|9|=9\) m/sec to \(|5|=5\) m/sec. Because the speed is decreasing, the particle is slowing down.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    The velocity of a particle is initially \(-5\) m/sec and it decreases to \(-9\) m/sec.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since speed is the absolute value of velocity, the speed changes from \(|-5|=5\) m/sec to \(|-9|=9\) m/sec. Because the speed is increasing, the particle is speeding up.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (d)
    The velocity of a particle is initially \(-9\) m/sec and it increases to \(-5\) m/sec.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since speed is the absolute value of velocity, the speed changes from \(|-9|=9\) m/sec to \(|-5|=5\) m/sec. Because the speed is decreasing, the particle is slowing down.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.8.

    In summary it is not enough to know whether the velocity is increasing or decreasing to determine if the object is speeding up or slowing down. One must also consider the sign of the velocity.
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s summarize this information as a theorem:
    🔗

    Theorem 3.1.9. Speeding Up and Slowing Down.

    • An object is speeding up when its velocity and acceleration have the same sign (either both positive or both negative).
      🔗
    • An object is slowing down when its velocity and acceleration have opposite signs (one is positive and the other is negative).
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Justification.

    If the velocity is positive then it is the same as speed. Thus if the acceleration is also positive then the velocity is increasing which means the speed is increasing.
    🔗
    If the velocity is negative then the speed is the opposite of velocity. Thus if the acceleration is also negative then the velocity is decreasing which means the speed is increasing.
    🔗
    The other two cases are similar.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.10.

    Suppose an object has velocity \(v(t)=t^2-4t\text{.}\) Determine when the object is speeding up and when it is slowing down. Express your answers in interval notation.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First let’s find the sign of the velocity. We have
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle v(t) = t^2 - 4t = t(t-4). \end{equation*}
    Using the Test Point Method for Inequalities , we find that the velocity is zero at \(t=0\) and \(t=4\text{.}\) These points divide the number line into three intervals: \((-\infty,0)\text{,}\) \((0,4)\text{,}\) and \((4,\infty)\text{.}\) Choosing test points from each interval, we find:
    • For \(t=-1\) in \((-\infty,0)\text{,}\) \(v(-1)=5\text{,}\) so velocity is positive on this interval.
      🔗
    • For \(t=2\) in \((0,4)\text{,}\) \(v(2)=-4\text{,}\) so velocity is negative on this interval.
      🔗
    • For \(t=5\) in \((4,\infty)\text{,}\) \(v(5)=5\text{,}\) so velocity is positive on this interval.
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Next, we find the acceleration by differentiating the velocity:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle a(t) = v'(t) = 2t - 4. \end{equation*}
    Setting the acceleration equal to zero gives \(2t - 4 = 0\text{,}\) or \(t=2\text{.}\) This divides the number line into two intervals: \((-\infty,2)\) and \((2,\infty)\text{.}\) Choosing test points from each interval, we find:
    • For \(t=0\) in \((-\infty,2)\text{,}\) \(a(0)=-4\text{,}\) so acceleration is negative on this interval.
      🔗
    • For \(t=3\) in \((2,\infty)\text{,}\) \(a(3)=2\text{,}\) so acceleration is positive on this interval.
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now we can determine when the object is speeding up and when it is slowing down by comparing the signs of velocity and acceleration:
    🔗
    🔗
    • On \((-\infty,0)\text{,}\) velocity is positive and acceleration is negative, so the object is slowing down.
      🔗
    • On \((0,2)\text{,}\) velocity is negative and acceleration is negative, so the object is speeding up.
      🔗
    • On \((2,4)\text{,}\) velocity is negative and acceleration is positive, so the object is slowing down.
      🔗
    • On \((4,\infty)\text{,}\) velocity is positive and acceleration is positive, so the object is speeding up.
      🔗
    🔗
    Therefore, the object is speeding up on \((0,2)\cup(4,\infty)\) and slowing down on \((-\infty,0)\cup(2,4)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    This completes our discussion of speeding up and slowing down. We will now move on to discuss total distance traveled and how it differs from displacement, the final new concept in this section.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Displacement vs Distance Traveled

    Many students are probably already familiar with the concept of displacement from your favorite physics class:
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.14. Displacement.

    The displacement of an object over the time interval \([a,b]\) is how much the object’s position changes during that time.
    🔗
    Displacement is given by
    \begin{equation*} \text{Displacement} = s(b) - s(a), \end{equation*}
    where \(s(t)\) is the position function of the object.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.15.

    Building off of Example 3.1.3: Recall the vertical position of the ball is given by
    \begin{equation*} s(t)=-16t^2+64t+6\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Find the displacement of the ball from \(t=1\) seconds to \(t=3\) seconds using the equation above.
    🔗
    Then verify your answer on the graph below.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the displacement, we evaluate the position function at the endpoints of the interval:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle s(1) \amp =\amp -16(1)^2 + 64(1) + 6 = 54 \text{ ft,}\\[0.6em] \displaystyle s(3) \amp =\amp -16(3)^2 + 64(3) + 6 = 54 \text{ ft.} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the displacement is
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \text{Displacement} = s(3) - s(1) = 54 - 54 = 0 \text{ ft.} \end{equation*}
    This means that the ball ends up at the same height it started from during this time interval.
    🔗
    Looking at the graph, we can see that at \(t=1\) seconds, the ball is at a height of 54 feet. It then rises to a maximum height of 70 feet at \(t=2\) seconds before falling back down to 54 feet at \(t=3\) seconds. Thus, the ball returns to its original height, confirming our calculation of zero displacement.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    No compare this with the total distance traveled by the ball during the same time interval. Notice that it would be incorrect to say the ball traveled a distance of zero feet, since it did move up and then back down. But perhaps we are getting ahead of ourselves. Let’s first define total distance traveled then we can come up with a reliable method to calculate it.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.17. Total Distance Traveled.

    The total distance traveled by an object over the time interval \([a,b]\) is the total length of the path the object takes during that time.
    🔗
    🔗
    Before we try to come up with a formula for total distance traveled, let’s look back to our ball example to get some intuition.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.18.

    Again, building off of Example 3.1.3: Recall the vertical position of the ball is given by
    \begin{equation*} s(t)=-16t^2+64t+6\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (a)
    Find the total distance traveled by the ball from \(t=1\) seconds to \(t=3\) seconds by using the graph below.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    From the graph, we see that at \(t=1\) seconds, the ball is at a height of 54 feet. It then rises to a maximum height of 70 feet at \(t=2\) seconds. The distance traveled during this ascent is \(|70-54|=|16| = 16\) feet. Notice that we use absolute values here since distance traveled is always non-negative and we don’t care about direction.
    🔗
    Next, the ball falls from a maximum height of 70 feet at \(t=2\) seconds back down to 54 feet at \(t=3\) seconds. The distance traveled during this descent is \(|54-70|=|-16| = 16\) feet.
    🔗
    Therefore, the total distance traveled by the ball from \(t=1\) seconds to \(t=3\) seconds is the sum of the distances during the ascent and descent:
    \begin{equation*} \text{Total Distance Traveled} = 16 \text{ ft} + 16 \text{ ft} = 32 \text{ ft.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Find the total distance traveled by the ball from \(t=1\) seconds to \(t=3\) seconds by only using the height equation \(s(t)=-16t^2+64t+6\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the total distance traveled using only the height equation, we first need to determine when the ball changes direction. This occurs when the velocity is zero. We already found the velocity function in Example 3.1.3:
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = s'(t) = -32t + 64 \end{equation*}
    Setting \(v(t)=0\text{,}\) we solve:
    \begin{equation*} 0 = -32t + 64 \Rightarrow t = 2 \end{equation*}
    So the ball changes direction at \(t=2\) seconds.
    🔗
    The total distance traveled is the sum of the distances traveled during each segment of motion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{Total Distance Traveled} = |s(2)-s(1)| + |s(3)-s(2)| \end{equation*}
    We compute each value:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} s(1) \amp =\amp -16(1)^2 + 64(1) + 6 = -16 + 64 + 6 = 54 \\ s(2) \amp = \amp -16(2)^2 + 64(2) + 6 = -64 + 128 + 6 = 70 \\ s(3) \amp = \amp -16(3)^2 + 64(3) + 6 = -144 + 192 + 6 = 54 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \text{Total Distance Traveled} \amp=\amp |70-54| + |54-70|\\ \amp =\amp 16 + 16 = 32 \text{ feet} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.20.

    The key take away from this example is that in order to compute total distance traveled, we must know where all the turning points are (when \(v(t)=0\)) and then sum the distances traveled over each piece.
    🔗
    🔗
    Now we are ready to summarize our method for finding total distance traveled.
    🔗

    Strategy 3.1.21. Method for Finding Total Distance Traveled.

    To find the total distance traveled by an object over the time interval \([a,b]\text{:}\)
    🔗
    1. Find all times turning points, that is all values of \(t\) in \((a,b)\) where the velocity is zero: \(v(t)=0\text{.}\) These times divide the interval \([a,b]\) into pieces.
      🔗
    2. For each piece of the interval, compute the distance traveled by taking the absolute value of the displacement .
      🔗
    3. Sum the distances from each piece to get the total distance traveled over \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.22.

    An object moves along a line with position given by \(s(t)=3\sin(\pi t)\text{.}\) Find the total distance traveled over the time interval \([0,2]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we find the velocity by differentiating the position function:
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = s'(t) = 3\pi \cos(\pi t). \end{equation*}
    Setting \(v(t)=0\text{,}\) we solve:
    \begin{equation*} 0 = 3\pi \cos(\pi t) \Rightarrow \cos(\pi t) = 0. \end{equation*}
    Using the unit circle, the solutions to \(\cos(\pi t) = 0\) in the interval \([0,2]\) are \(t=\tfrac{1}{2}\) and \(t=\tfrac{3}{2}\text{.}\) These points divide the interval \([0,2]\) into three pieces: \([0,\tfrac{1}{2}]\text{,}\) \([\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{3}{2}]\text{,}\) and \([\tfrac{3}{2},2]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, we compute the distance traveled on each piece:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} |s(\tfrac{1}{2}) - s(0)| \amp=\amp |3\sin(\tfrac{\pi}{2}) - 3\sin(0)| = |3 - 0| = 3 \\ |s(\tfrac{3}{2}) - s(\tfrac{1}{2})| \amp=\amp |3\sin(\tfrac{3\pi}{2}) - 3\sin(\tfrac{\pi}{2})| = |-3 - 3| = 6 \\ |s(2) - s(\tfrac{3}{2})| \amp=\amp |3\sin(2\pi) - 3\sin(\tfrac{3\pi}{2})| = |0 - (-3)| = 3 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Finally, we sum the distances from each piece to find the total distance traveled over \([0,2]\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{Total Distance Traveled} = 3 + 6 + 3 = 12. \end{equation*}

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    True/False Questions.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false. Justify your answers.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(v(t)=s'(t)\) is positive on \((a,b)\text{,}\) then \(s(t)\) is increasing on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. If the velocity is positive, the position function is increasing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    For \(s(t)=-5t^2+15t+6\text{,}\) the ball reaches its maximum height at \(t=2\) seconds because \(v(2)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The velocity function is \(v(t)=s'(t)=-10t+15\text{.}\) Setting \(v(t)=0\text{,}\) we solve:
    \begin{equation*} 0 = -10t + 15 \Rightarrow t = 1.5. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the ball reaches its maximum height at \(t=1.5\) seconds, not \(t=2\) seconds.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(v(t)=0\) at a time \(t=5\text{,}\) then the object must change direction at \(t=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. When the velocity is zero, the object is momentarily at rest. However, this does not necessarily mean that the object changes direction at that time. For example, if the velocity changes from positive to zero and then back to positive, the object does not change direction.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(v(t)\) is decreasing on an interval, then the object is slowing down on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. If the velocity is decreasing, it means that the acceleration is negative. However, whether the object is slowing down or speeding up depends on the sign of the velocity as well. If the velocity is positive and decreasing, the object is slowing down. But if the velocity is negative and decreasing, the object is actually speeding up.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    If \(a(t)=0\) at a time \(t=3\text{,}\) then the object must be at rest (even momentarily) at \(t=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. When the acceleration is zero, it means that the velocity is not changing at that time. However, this does not necessarily mean that the object is at rest. The object could be moving with a constant velocity.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(v(6)>0\) and \(a(6)>0\text{,}\) then the object is speeding up at \(t=6\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Since the velocity is positive, the object is moving in the positive direction. Since the acceleration is also positive, the velocity is increasing. Therefore, the speed of the object is increasing, which means the object is speeding up.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    The total distance traveled on \([a,b]\) is always greater than or equal to \(|s(b)-s(a)|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The total distance traveled accounts for all movement, regardless of direction, while \(|s(b)-s(a)|\) only measures the net change in position. Therefore, the total distance traveled is always greater than or equal to the absolute value of the displacement.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    If an object never changes direction on \([a,b]\text{,}\) then its total distance traveled equals \(|s(b)-s(a)|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. If the object never changes direction, it means that it is either always moving in the positive direction or always moving in the negative direction. In either case, the total distance traveled is equal to the absolute value of the displacement, which is \(|s(b)-s(a)|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    An object moving along a line has position function \(s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t\text{.}\) The object is speeding up on the interval \((0,3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. First, we find the velocity and acceleration functions:
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12. \end{equation*}
    Next, we determine the signs of velocity and acceleration on the interval \((0,3)\text{.}\) Setting \(v(t)=0\text{,}\) we solve:
    \begin{equation*} 0 = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 \Rightarrow t^2 - 4t + 3 = 0 \Rightarrow (t-1)(t-3) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1, 3. \end{equation*}
    Thus, on the interval \((0,3)\text{,}\) the velocity is positive on \((0,1)\) and negative on \((1,3)\text{.}\) Setting \(a(t)=0\text{,}\) we solve:
    \begin{equation*} 0 = 6t - 12 \Rightarrow t = 2. \end{equation*}
    Thus, on the interval \((0,3)\text{,}\) the acceleration is negative on \((0,2)\) and positive on \((2,3)\text{.}\) Therefore, the object is slowing down on \((0,1)\) and \((1,2)\text{,}\) and speeding up on \((2,3)\text{.}\) Since the object is not speeding up on the entire interval \((0,3)\text{,}\) the statement is false.
