    Subsection Section 2.1 Introduction and Motivation for Derivatives

    Example 2.8.1.

    Find the average rate of change of the function \(f(x) = \tan(x)-x\) on the interval \([-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the average rate of change of the function \(f(x) = \tan(x)-x\) on the interval \([-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}]\text{,}\) we use the formula for the average rate of change:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{Average Rate of Change} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \end{equation*}
    where \(a = -\frac{\pi}{4}\) and \(b = \frac{\pi}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    First, we calculate \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \tan\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right) - \left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = -1 + \frac{\pi}{4} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \tan\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) - \frac{\pi}{4} = 1 - \frac{\pi}{4} \end{equation*}
    Now, we substitute these values into the average rate of change formula and simplify:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \text{Average Rate of Change} & = & \dfrac{\left(1 - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) - \left(-1 + \frac{\pi}{4}\right)}{\frac{\pi}{4} - \left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)} \\ & = & \dfrac{1 - \frac{\pi}{4} + 1 - \frac{\pi}{4}}{\frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{\pi}{4}} \\ & = & \dfrac{2 - \frac{\pi}{2}}{\frac{\pi}{2}} \\ & = & \dfrac{2}{\frac{\pi}{2}} - 1 \\ & = & \dfrac{4}{\pi} - 1 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the average rate of change of the function \(f(x) = \tan(x)-x\) on the interval \([-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}]\) is \(\frac{4}{\pi} - 1\text{.}\)

    Example 2.8.2.

    Let \(f(x)=x^2 - 4x + 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Find the slope of the secant line joining \(P(1,f(1))\) and \(Q(5,f(5))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Find the slope of the secant line joining \(P(2,f(2))\) and \(Q(2+h,f(2+h))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) Find the slope of the tangent line at \(P(2,f(2))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (d) Find the equation of the tangent line at \(P(2,f(2))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) The slope of the secant line is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(5)-f(1)}{5-1}. \end{equation*}
    Compute the function values:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f(1) & =& 1 - 4 + 1 = -2, \\ f(5) & =& 25 - 20 + 1 = 6. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{6-(-2)}{4} = \frac{8}{4} = 2. \end{equation*}
    So the slope of the secant line is \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) The slope of the secant line is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    First compute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(2) = 4 - 8 + 1 = -3. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(2+h) = (2+h)^2 - 4(2+h) + 1. \end{equation*}
    Expand:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(2+h) = 4 + 4h + h^2 - 8 - 4h + 1 = h^2 - 3. \end{equation*}
    So the difference quotient becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{(h^2 - 3) - (-3)}{h} = \frac{h^2}{h} = h. \end{equation*}
    (c) The slope of the tangent line is the limit as \(h \to 0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{h\to 0} h = 0. \end{equation*}
    So the slope of the tangent line at \(x=2\) is \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (d) Using point-slope form:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y - (-3) = 0(x-2). \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} y = -3. \end{equation*}
    The tangent line is horizontal at \(y=-3\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection Section 2.2 Definition of the Derivative

    Example 2.8.3.

    Consider the function \(f(x)=x^3-2x\) near \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Simplify the difference quotient as much as possible:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (b) Use your result from part (a) and the limit definition of the derivative to calculate \(f'(1)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f'(1)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (c) Consider the following table of values of \(f(x)\) near \(x=1\text{.}\) Use difference quotients to approximate \(f'(1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline x & 1 & 1.1 & 1.01 & 1.001 & 0.99 \\ \hline f(x) & -1.000 & -0.869 & -0.989699 & -0.998997 & -1.009701 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Approximate \(f'(1)\) by computing the following difference quotients:
    🔗
    • For \(h=0.1\text{,}\) compute \(\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • For \(h=0.01\text{,}\) compute \(\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • For \(h=0.001\text{,}\) compute \(\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • For \(h=-0.01\text{,}\) compute \(\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    Solution.
    (a) First compute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(1+h)=(1+h)^3-2(1+h). \end{equation*}
    Expand:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (1+h)^3 = 1+3h+3h^2+h^3. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(1+h) = 1+3h+3h^2+h^3-2-2h = -1+h+3h^2+h^3. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f(1)=1^3-2(1)=-1\text{,}\) we have:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h} = \frac{(-1+h+3h^2+h^3)-(-1)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{h+3h^2+h^3}{h} = 1+3h+h^2. \end{equation*}
    (b) Now take the limit:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(1) = \lim_{h\to 0}(1+3h+h^2) = 1. \end{equation*}
    (c) Using the table:
    🔗
    For \(h=0.1\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-0.869-(-1)}{0.1} = \frac{0.131}{0.1} = 0.131 \cdot 10 = 1.31. \end{equation*}
    For \(h=0.01\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-0.989699-(-1)}{0.01} = \frac{0.010301}{0.01} = 0.010301 \cdot 100 = 1.0301. \end{equation*}
    For \(h=0.001\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-0.998997-(-1)}{0.001} = \frac{0.001003}{0.001} = 0.001003 \cdot 1000 = 1.003. \end{equation*}
    For \(h=-0.01\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-1.009701-(-1)}{-0.01} = \frac{-0.009701}{-0.01} = -0.009701 \cdot -100 = 0.9701. \end{equation*}
    These values approach \(1\text{,}\) confirming that \(f'(1)=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.8.4.

    Let \(y=p(t)\) be the height (in meters) of a rubber ball dropped from a ledge at time \(t=0\) seconds. The ball hits the ground at \(t=0.9\) seconds and then immediately bounces upward with a smaller velocity. A graph of \(p(t)\) is shown below.
    🔗
    (a) What are the units of \(p'(t)\text{?}\) Explain what the sign of \(p'(t)\) tells you about the motion of the ball.
    🔗
    (b) Use the graph to approximate \(p'(t)\) for \(0.8 < t < 0.9\text{.}\) What is the velocity of the ball just before it hits the ground?
    🔗
    (c) Use the graph to approximate the velocity of the ball immediately after the bounce (for \(0.9 < t < 1.0\))?
    🔗
    (d) Compare the left-hand and right-hand derivatives at \(t=0.9\text{.}\) Does \(p'(0.9)\) exist? Explain your reasoning using the graph.
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) Height is measured in meters and time in seconds, so \(p'(t)\) has units of meters per second (m/sec). If \(p'(t)\) is negative, the ball is moving downward. If \(p'(t)\) is positive, the ball is moving upward.
    🔗
    (b) For \(0.8 < t < 0.9\text{,}\) we have \(p(0.8) \approx 2.5\) and \(p(0.9) = 0\text{.}\) Just before impact, the average velocity is approximately
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{p(0.9)-p(0.8)}{0.9-0.8} = \frac{0-2.5}{0.1} = -25. \end{equation*}
    So the ball is moving downward at approximately \(25\) m/sec just before hitting the ground.
    🔗
    (c) For \(0.9 < t < 1.0\text{,}\) we have \(p(0.9) = 0\) and \(p(1.0) \approx 1\text{.}\) Therefore the average velocity is approximately
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{p(1.0)-p(0.9)}{1.0-0.9} = \frac{1-0}{0.1} = 10. \end{equation*}
    So immediately after the bounce, the ball is moving upward at approximately \(10\) m/sec.
    🔗
    (d) The left-hand derivative at \(t=0.9\) is approximately \(-25\text{,}\) while the right-hand derivative is \(10\text{.}\) Since these values are not equal, the derivative does not exist at \(t=0.9\text{.}\) Graphically, the function has a sharp corner at the bounce point.
    🔗

    Example 2.8.5.

    Let \(p(t)=\dfrac{2t-1}{5+t}\) be the position of an object at time \(t\text{.}\) Here \(p\) is measured in meters and \(t\) is measured in seconds.
    🔗
    (a) Simplify the following difference quotient as much as possible:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{p(t+h)-p(t)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    (b) Use your result from part (a) and the limit definition of the derivative to calculate the instantaneous velocity of the object at time \(t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(t)=p'(t)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{p(t+h)-p(t)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    (c) What is the object’s velocity at \(t=1\) second?
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) First compute
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} p(t+h)=\frac{2(t+h)-1}{5+t+h} = \frac{2t+2h-1}{5+t+h}. \end{equation*}
    So the difference quotient becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{ \frac{2t+2h-1}{5+t+h} - \frac{2t-1}{5+t} }{h}. \end{equation*}
    Combine the fractions over a common denominator:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{ (2t+2h-1)(5+t) - (2t-1)(5+t+h) }{ h(5+t+h)(5+t) }. \end{equation*}
    After expanding and simplifying, this reduces to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{11}{(5+t+h)(5+t)}. \end{equation*}
    (b) Now take the limit as \(h\to 0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(t)=\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{11}{(5+t+h)(5+t)} = \frac{11}{(5+t)^2}. \end{equation*}
    (c) Evaluate at \(t=1\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v(1)=\frac{11}{(6)^2}=\frac{11}{36}. \end{equation*}
    So the velocity at \(t=1\) second is \(\dfrac{11}{36}\) m/s.

    Subsection Section 2.3 Basic Differentiation Rules

    Example 2.8.6.

    Let \(f(x) = 2+3x^2 - 5x + \dfrac{8}{x^3}\text{.}\) Evaluate the following:
    🔗
    (a) \(f'(x) =\)
    🔗
    (b) \(f'(2) =\)
    🔗
    (c) \(f''(x) =\)
    🔗
    (d) \(f''(2) =\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) To find \(f'(x)\text{,}\) we differentiate each term of the function.
    🔗
    Recall that \(\dfrac{8}{x^3} = 8x^{-3}\text{,}\) so we can apply the power rule to this term as well.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f'(x) & =& 0 + 6x - 5 - 24x^{-4} \\ & =& 6x - 5 - \frac{24}{x^4}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (b) To find \(f'(2)\text{,}\) we substitute \(x=2\) into the expression for \(f'(x)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(2) = 6(2) - 5 - \frac{24}{(2)^4} = 12 - 5 - \frac{24}{16} = 7 - \frac{3}{2} = \frac{11}{2}. \end{equation*}
    (c) To find \(f''(x)\text{,}\) we differentiate \(f'(x)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f''(x) & =& 6 + 0 + 96x^{-5} \\ & =& 6 + \frac{96}{x^5}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (d) To find \(f''(2)\text{,}\) we substitute \(x=2\) into the expression for \(f''(x)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f''(2) = 6 + \frac{96}{(2)^5} = 6 + \frac{96}{32} = 6 + 3 = 9. \end{equation*}

    Example 2.8.7.

    Let \(f(x) = \dfrac{3x^2 - 5x + 4}{x^3 + 1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Find \(f'(1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Find the equation of the tangent line to the graph of \(f\) at the point where \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) Find the equation of the perpendicular line to the graph of \(f\) at the point where \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) To find \(f'(1)\text{,}\) we first need to find the derivative \(f'(x)\) using the quotient rule. The quotient rule states that if \(f(x) = \dfrac{g(x)}{h(x)}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{g'(x)h(x) - g(x)h'(x)}{(h(x))^2}. \end{equation*}
    In our case, let \(g(x) = 3x^2 - 5x + 4\) and \(h(x) = x^3 + 1\text{.}\) We compute:
    \begin{equation*} g'(x) = 6x - 5, \quad h'(x) = 3x^2. \end{equation*}
    Now we can apply the quotient rule:
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{(6x - 5)(x^3 + 1) - (3x^2 - 5x + 4)(3x^2)}{(x^3 + 1)^2}. \end{equation*}
    Now we substitute \(x=1\) into the expression for \(f'(x)\) to find \(f'(1)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(1) = \frac{(6 - 5)(2) - (3 - 5 + 4)(3)}{(2)^2} = \frac{2 - 6}{4} = \frac{-4}{4} = -1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (b) To find the equation of the tangent line to the graph of \(f\) at the point where \(x=1\text{,}\) we first need to find the coordinates of the point on the graph at \(x=1\text{.}\) We compute:
    \begin{equation*} f(1) = \frac{3(1)^2 - 5(1) + 4}{(1)^3 + 1} = \frac{3 - 5 + 4}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1. \end{equation*}
    So the point of tangency is \((1, 1)\text{.}\) The slope of the tangent line is given by \(f'(1) = -1\text{.}\) Using the point-slope form of a line, we can write the equation of the tangent line as:
    \begin{equation*} y - 1 = -1(x - 1). \end{equation*}
    Simplifying this gives:
    \begin{equation*} y = -x + 2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (c) To find the equation of the perpendicular line to the graph of \(f\) at the point where \(x=1\text{,}\) we first need to find the slope of the perpendicular line. The slope of the tangent line is \(-1\text{,}\) so the slope of the perpendicular line is the negative reciprocal of \(-1\text{,}\) which is \(1\text{.}\) Using the point-slope form of a line again, we can write the equation of the perpendicular line as:
    \begin{equation*} y - 1 = 1(x - 1). \end{equation*}
    Simplifying this gives:
    \begin{equation*} y = x. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.8.8. Using Given Function and Derivative Values.

    Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions and that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(1) = -2, \quad f'(1) = 4, \quad g(1) = 5, \quad g'(1) = -3. \end{equation*}
    Evaluate the following derivatives at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) \(\displaystyle \left.\frac{d}{dx}(3f - 2g)\right|_{x=1}\)
    🔗
    (b) \(\displaystyle \left.\frac{d}{dx}(fg)\right|_{x=1}\)
    🔗
    (c) \(\displaystyle \left.\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)\right|_{x=1}\)
    🔗
    (d) \(\displaystyle \left.\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{g}{f}\right)\right|_{x=1}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (a) We use the linearity of differentiation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \left.\frac{d}{dx}(3f - 2g)\right|_{x=1} & = & 3f'(1) - 2g'(1) \\ & = & 3(4) - 2(-3) = 12 + 6 = 18. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (b) We use the product rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \left.\frac{d}{dx}(fg)\right|_{x=1} & = & f'(1)g(1) + f(1)g'(1) \\ & = & (4)(5) + (-2)(-3) = 20 + 6 = 26. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (c) We use the quotient rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \left.\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)\right|_{x=1} & = & \frac{f'(1)g(1) - f(1)g'(1)}{(g(1))^2} \\ & = & \frac{(4)(5) - (-2)(-3)}{5^2} \\ & = & \frac{20 - 6}{25} = \frac{14}{25}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (d) Again, we use the quotient rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \left.\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{g}{f}\right)\right|_{x=1} & = & \frac{g'(1)f(1) - g(1)f'(1)}{(f(1))^2} \\ & = & \frac{-3(-2) - 5(4)}{(-2)^2} \\ & = & \frac{6 - 20}{4} = \frac{-14}{4} = -\frac{7}{2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.8.9.

    Find all points of the graph of \(f(x)=x^3-6x^2+1\) that have horizontal tangents.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Horizontal tangents occur where the slope (derivative) is zero. So we are looking to solve the equation \(f'(x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f(x) & = & x^3-6x^2+1 \\ f'(x) & = & 3x^2-12x \\ & = & 3(x^2-4x) \\ & = & 3x(x-4). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Set \(f'(x)=0 \implies\) \(3x(x-4)=0\text{,}\) so \(x=0\) or \(x=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the horizontal tangents occur at \(x=0\) and \(x=4\text{.}\) To find the corresponding points on the graph, we compute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f(0) & = & 1, \\ f(4) & = & 64 - 96 + 1 = -31. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Thus, the points of the graph that have horizontal tangents are \((0, 1)\) and \((4, -31)\text{.}\)

    Example 2.8.10.

    Find the derivative of the given function.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y = x(x^2+5)\sqrt{x+1}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    There are multiple ways to approach this problem. One way is to first simplify the expression for \(y\) and then differentiate. Another way is to apply the product rule multiple times without simplifying first. Here, we will take the simpler route and simplify first.
    🔗
    First, we can rewrite \(y\) as:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} y & = & x(x^2+5)(x+1)^{1/2} \\ & = & (x^3 + 5x)(x+1)^{1/2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we can apply the product rule to differentiate \(y\) with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{dy}{dx} & = & \left[(x^3 + 5x)(x+1)^{1/2}\right]' \\ & = & (3x^2 + 5)(x+1)^{1/2} + (x^3 + 5x)\cdot\frac{1}{2}(x+1)^{-1/2} \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Although we can continue to simplify this expression, it is often acceptable to leave the derivative in this form, especially if the problem does not explicitly ask for further simplification.

    Example 2.8.11.

    Is there a point on the curve \(y=ax^2\) where the tangent line has the equation \(y=6x+1\text{.}\) If so, find the values of \(a\) and the point of tangency.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The slope of the line \(y=6x+1\) is \(6\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The derivative of the curve \(y=ax^2\) is \(y'=2ax\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For the line to be tangent to the curve, their slopes must be equal at some point. So we set:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2ax = 6 \quad \Rightarrow \quad a = \frac{3}{x}. \end{equation*}
    The point of tangency must also be on both curves. So we know that:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 6x+1 = y = ax^2 = \frac{3}{x} \cdot x^2 = 3x. \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(x\) gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 3x = 6x + 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad -3x = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = -\frac{1}{3}. \end{equation*}
    Substituting back into (either) curve equation we get:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y = 6\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) + 1 = -2 + 1 = -1. \end{equation*}
    And substituting this value back into our \(a = \frac{3}{x}\) equation gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a = \frac{3}{-\frac{1}{3}} = -9. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the value of \(a\) is \(-9\text{,}\) and the point of tangency is \(\left(-\frac{1}{3}, -1\right)\text{.}\) You can verify in Desmos that the line \(y=6x+1\) is indeed tangent to the curve \(y=-9x^2\) at the point \(\left(-\frac{1}{3}, -1\right)\text{.}\)
