    Section 2.1 Introduction and Motivation for Derivatives

    Objectives

    • Compute the average rate of change of a function on an interval and interpret it as the slope of a secant line.
      ðŸ”—
    • Describe instantaneous velocity as the limit of average velocities over shrinking time intervals.
      ðŸ”—
    • Interpret instantaneous rate of change geometrically as the slope of a tangent line.
      ðŸ”—
    • Use tables, graphs, and nearby average rates of change to estimate an instantaneous rate of change.
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Derivatives begin with a simple question: how fast is something changing right now? You already know how to compute an average rate of change over a time interval. The derivative is what happens when we shrink that interval smaller and smaller and study the limiting behavior of these average rates.
    ðŸ”—
    Throughout this section, the same expression will appear in multiple contexts: average velocity, slopes of secant lines, and instantaneous rate of change. This expression is called the difference quotient.
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Average and Instantaneous Velocity

    Suppose a particle moves along a line so that at time \(t\) it is at position \(f(t)\text{.}\) We begin by defining the average velocity over a time interval.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.1.

    Let \(f(t)\) denote the position of an object at time \(t\text{.}\) The average velocity of the object on the time interval \([a,b]\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle v_{\mathrm{avg}}=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.1.2.

    The average velocity represents the total change in position divided by the total change in time. Geometrically, it is the slope of the secant line connecting \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\) on the graph of \(y=f(t)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Instantaneous velocity cannot be measured over a single moment in time. Instead, it is defined as a limit of average velocities over shrinking time intervals.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.3.

    Let \(f(t)\) denote the position of an object at time \(t\text{.}\) The instantaneous velocity at time \(t=a\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle v=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    provided this limit exists. More generally, if \(f(t)\) represents a quantity other than position, this same limit represents the instantaneous rate of change of that quantity with respect to time at \(t = a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.1.4.

    Velocity may be positive or negative depending on direction of motion. The speed of an object is the absolute value of its velocity.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.1.5.

    A stone dropped from a bridge has position (in feet below the bridge) approximately \(f(t)=16t^2\) after \(t\) seconds.
    ðŸ”—
    (a)
    Compute the average velocity on \([3,4]\) using DefinitionÂ 2.1.1.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Using DefinitionÂ 2.1.1 with \(a=3\) and \(b=4\text{,}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \begin{array}{rcl} v_{\mathrm{avg}} &=& \displaystyle \frac{f(4)-f(3)}{4-3} \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle \frac{16(4^2)-16(3^2)}{1} \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle 16(16-9) \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle 112. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the average velocity on \([3,4]\) is \(112\) ft/sec.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    (b)
    Use the table of velocities below to estimate the instantaneous velocity at \(t=3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \begin{array}{c|ccccc} h & 1 & 0.1 & 0.01 & 0.001 & 0.0001 \\ \hline v_{\mathrm{avg}} & 112 & 97.6 & 96.16 & 96.016 & 96.0016 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    As \(h\) gets smaller, the average velocities get closer to \(96\text{.}\) So a reasonable estimate for the instantaneous velocity at \(t=3\) is \(96\) ft/sec.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    (c)
    Compute the instantaneous velocity at \(t=3\) using DefinitionÂ 2.1.3.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    By DefinitionÂ 2.1.3, the instantaneous velocity at \(t=3\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} v = \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(3+h)-f(3)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f(t)=16t^2\text{,}\) we compute
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \begin{array}{rcl} v &=& \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{16(3+h)^2-16(3^2)}{h} \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}16\cdot\frac{(3+h)^2-9}{h} \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}16\cdot\frac{(9+6h+h^2)-9}{h} \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}16\cdot\frac{6h+h^2}{h} \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}16(6+h) \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}96+16h \end{array} \end{equation*}
    As we take \(h\) smaller and smaller, the error term \(16h\) approaches zero, and the average velocity approaches the limiting value \(96\text{,}\) which by definition is the instantaneous velocity. Therefore, the instantaneous velocity at \(t=3\) is \(96\) ft/sec.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Slopes of Secant Lines and Slopes of Tangent Lines

    We have described velocity on several conceptual levels. It can be understood as a physical quantity describing motion, a numerical approximation obtained from average rates of change, and an algebraic computation using limits. Velocity also has a geometric interpretation in terms of the graph \(y=f(t)\text{,}\) where it corresponds to the slope of the graph at a given time.
    ðŸ”—
    A secant line is a line that passes through two distinct points on the graph. It cuts across the curve and represents how the function changes between two different input values. When these two points are far apart, the secant line gives a coarse description of the functionâ€™s behavior over an interval.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.7.

    Let \(f\) be a function and let \(a \neq b\text{.}\) The slope of the secant line to \(f\) between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle m_{\mathrm{sec}}=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.1.8.

    The slope of the secant line is the slope of the line through the points \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\text{.}\) Algebraically, it is the same formula used for the average rate of change.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    A tangent line, on the other hand, touches the graph at a single point and matches the direction of the curve at that point. It represents the functionâ€™s behavior at a specific input value and provides a local, instantaneous description of change.
    ðŸ”—
    As the second point used to define a secant line moves closer and closer to the first, the secant line approaches the tangent line. This idea leads directly to the following definitions.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.11.

    Let \(f\) be a function and let \(a\) be a point in its domain. The slope of the tangent line to \(f\) at \(x = a\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle m = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}, \end{equation*}
    provided this limit exists. Equivalently, the slope of the tangent line can be written as
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle m = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    provided this limit exists.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.1.12.

    The limit as \(h \to 0\) is the more common definition in practice, because it is often algebraically convenient to evaluate limits at \(h = 0\) after simplifying the expression.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.1.13.

    The slope of the tangent line gives the slope of the line that best approximates the graph of \(y=f(x)\) near \(x=a\text{.}\) This is the same limit used to define instantaneous velocity, which explains why this limit describes both slopes of curves and rates of change.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.1.14. A Trigonometric Model and Estimating Rates.

    We model average daily temperature through the year by the sinusoidal function:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle T(x)=55-40\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{365}x\right), \quad\text{degrees F on day } x. \end{equation*}
    Using this model we found that
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{Day} & x & T(x) \\ \hline \text{Mar 31} & 90 & 54.1 \\ \text{Apr 10} & 100 & 61.0 \\ \text{Apr 20} & 110 & 67.7 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Using this table, estimate how quickly the weather is warming at \(x=100\) (Apr 10), in degrees per day.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    To estimate how quickly the temperature is changing at \(x = 100\text{,}\) we approximate the instantaneous rate of change using average rates of change from nearby data values in the table.
    ðŸ”—
    First, compute the average rate of change from \(x = 90\) to \(x = 100\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{T(100) - T(90)}{100 - 90} & = & \dfrac{61.0 - 54.1}{10} \\[0.6em] & = & \dfrac{6.9}{10} \\[0.6em] & = & 0.69 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Next, compute the average rate of change from \(x = 100\) to \(x = 110\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{T(110) - T(100)}{110 - 100} & = & \dfrac{67.7 - 61.0}{10} \\[0.6em] & = & \dfrac{6.7}{10} \\[0.6em] & = & 0.67 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    These two values represent average rates of change on either side of \(x = 100\text{.}\) Since they are close in value, we use their average to estimate the instantaneous rate of change at \(x = 100\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \text{Estimated rate at } x = 100 & \approx & \dfrac{0.69 + 0.67}{2} \\[0.6em] & = & 0.68 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the temperature is increasing at approximately \(0.68\) degrees Fahrenheit per day on April 10.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In the next sections, we will turn these ideas into a precise definition and develop tools for computing instantaneous rates of change efficiently.
