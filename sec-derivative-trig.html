    Section 2.4 Trigonometric Derivatives

    Objectives

    • Use geometric and graphical reasoning to understand why \(\sin'(x)=\cos(x)\) and \(\cos'(x)=-\sin(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Use these results with the product and quotient rules to differentiate the remaining trigonometric functions.
      🔗
    • Evaluate basic trigonometric limits by rewriting them in terms of \(\displaystyle \frac{\sin(u)}{u}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Remember that standard trigonometric derivative formulas assume angles are measured in radians.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Derivative of Sine and Cosine

    Sine and cosine are fundamental for describing periodic motion. One way to predict their derivatives is to look at graphs. If we estimate the slope of the graph \(y=\sin(x)\) at many points, we can sketch its derivative function. The slopes appear to match the graph of \(\cos(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.2.

    For all real \(x\) (measured in radians),
    \begin{equation*} (\sin(x))'=\cos(x) \quad \text{ and } \quad (\cos(x))'=-\sin(x) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    A full proof of these derivative formulas begins with the limit definition of the derivative applied to the sine and cosine functions. Using trigonometric identities, this reduces the problem to understanding the behavior of certain key limits involving sine and cosine near zero.
    🔗
    The first half of this argument is presented clearly in the video Derivatives of Sine and Cosine from First Principles. As you will see, completing the proof requires evaluating limits such as the limit of sine of x over x as x approaches zero, which we will develop later in this section.
    🔗
    🔗
    For the time being, we will take this result as given and focus on how to use it to find the derivative formulas for the other standard trigonometric functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection General Trigonometric Derivatives

    Now that we know the derivatives of sine and cosine, we can quickly find the derivatives of the remaining trigonometric functions by applying our basic differentiation rules, such as the Quotient Rule.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.3.

    For all real \(x\) such that \(\tan(x)\) is defined,
    \begin{equation*} (\tan(x))' = \sec^2(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Recall that \(\tan(x)=\dfrac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\text{.}\) Since \(\cos(x)\neq 0\) wherever \(\tan(x)\) is defined, we may apply the Quotient Rule.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} (\tan(x))' &=& \left(\dfrac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\right)' \\[1em] &=& \dfrac{\cos(x)\sin'(x)-\sin(x)\cos'(x)}{\cos^2(x)} \\[1em] &=& \dfrac{\cos(x)\cos(x)-\sin(x)(-\sin(x))}{\cos^2(x)} \\[1em] &=& \dfrac{\cos^2(x)+\sin^2(x)}{\cos^2(x)} \\[1em] &=& \dfrac{1}{\cos^2(x)} \\[1em] &=& \sec^2(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \((\tan(x))'=\sec^2(x)\) wherever \(\tan(x)\) is defined.
    🔗
    🔗
    And similarly
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.4.

    For all real \(x\) such that the following functions are defined,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} (\sec(x))' &=& \sec(x)\tan(x), \\[0.5em] (\cot(x))' &=& -\csc^2(x), \\[0.5em] (\csc(x))' &=& -\csc(x)\cot(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 2.4.5.

    Again remember, the derivative formulas in this section are valid when \(x\) is measured in radians, not degrees.
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s take a moment to incorporate this new knowledge into ideas from earlier sections before moving on to our discussion of limits.
    🔗

    Example 2.4.6.

    Find \(f'(x)\) if \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\tan(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(f(x)\) is written as a product of two functions, we apply the Product Rule.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f'(x) &=& \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\tan(x)+\sqrt{x}\sec^2(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This expression is an acceptable final answer, though it could be simplified further if desired.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.7.

    Evaluate \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{\cos(x)}{3x^2}\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since the function is written as a quotient, we apply the Quotient Rule.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{\cos(x)}{3x^2}\right) &=& \dfrac{(3x^2)(-\sin(x))-\cos(x)(6x)}{(3x^2)^2}\\[0.75em] &=& \dfrac{-3x^2\sin(x)-6x\cos(x)}{9x^4}\\[0.75em] &=& \dfrac{-x\sin(x)-2\cos(x)}{3x^3}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Two Fundamental Trigonometric Limits

    Previously in this section, we outlined the proof that the derivative of the sine function is the cosine function. That argument reduces to evaluating certain key limits involving sine and cosine as the input approaches zero. In this subsection, we formally establish those limits using a generic variable \(u\text{.}\) This prepares us for the substitutions and rewrites that commonly appear in trigonometric limit problems.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.8.

    \(\displaystyle \lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\sin(u)}{u}=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Consider the unit circle and an angle \(u\) measured in radians, with \(u \geq 0\text{.}\) Geometric arguments
     1 
    This typically involves comparing the areas of triangles and circular sectors in the unit circle. In previous semesters, the full argument has been worked through in recitation. For students who are curious, Khan Academy also has a great video on this topic.
    show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sin(u) \le u \le \tan(u). \end{equation*}
    Dividing all three expressions by \(\sin(u)\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1 \le \frac{u}{\sin(u)} \le \frac{1}{\cos(u)}. \end{equation*}
    Taking reciprocals reverses the inequalities:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \cos(u) \le \frac{\sin(u)}{u} \le 1. \end{equation*}
    As \(u\to 0\text{,}\) we know \(\cos(u)\to 1\text{.}\) Since \(\frac{\sin(u)}{u}\) is trapped between two expressions that both approach \(1\text{,}\) the Squeeze Theorem implies
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\sin(u)}{u}=1. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.9.

    \(\displaystyle \lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\cos(u)-1}{u}=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Rewrite the expression by multiplying the numerator and denominator by \(\cos(u)+1\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\cos(u)-1}{u} = \frac{(\cos(u)-1)(\cos(u)+1)}{u(\cos(u)+1)} = \frac{-\sin^2(u)}{u(\cos(u)+1)}. \end{equation*}
    Rewrite this expression to isolate the sine-over-input factor:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{-\sin^2(u)}{u(\cos(u)+1)} = -\left(\frac{\sin(u)}{u}\right)\left(\frac{\sin(u)}{\cos(u)+1}\right). \end{equation*}
    As \(u\to 0\text{,}\) we have \(\frac{\sin(u)}{u}\to 1\text{,}\) \(\sin(u)\to 0\text{,}\) and \(\cos(u)+1\to 2\text{.}\) Therefore the entire product approaches \(0\text{,}\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\cos(u)-1}{u}=0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    These two limits supply the missing analytic step in the proof of the sine and cosine derivative formulas and will be used repeatedly to evaluate more complicated trigonometric limits.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Limits of Trigonometric Quotients

    Many trigonometric limits can be evaluated by rewriting the expression until a factor of the form \(\displaystyle \frac{\sin(u)}{u}\) appears, where \(u\to 0\text{.}\) Then we use \(\displaystyle \lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\sin(u)}{u}=1\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 2.4.10.

    Compute \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(3x)}{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Multiply and divide by \(3\) to create \(\displaystyle \frac{\sin(3x)}{3x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(3x)}{x} \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{\sin(3x)}{3x}\cdot \frac{3x}{x}\right) \\[1.1em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \left(\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(3x)}{3x}\right)\left(\lim_{x\to 0}3\right) \\[1.1em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \left(\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\sin(u)}{u}\right)\cdot 3 \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1\cdot 3 \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 3. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    In the third line we used the substitution \(u=3x\text{,}\) so \(x\to 0\) forces \(u\to 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.11.

    Compute \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\tan(x)}{\sin(\sqrt{x})}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Rewrite \(\tan(x)=\dfrac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\) and then introduce the standard sine-over-input factors.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\tan(x)}{\sin(\sqrt{x})} \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0^+}\left(\frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\cdot \frac{1}{\sin(\sqrt{x})}\right) \\[1.1em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0^+}\left(\frac{1}{\cos(x)}\cdot \frac{\sin(x)}{x}\cdot x \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\sin(\sqrt{x})}\cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\right) \\[1.3em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0^+}\left(\frac{\sqrt{x}}{\cos(x)}\cdot \frac{\sin(x)}{x}\cdot \frac{1}{\frac{\sin(\sqrt{x})}{\sqrt{x}}}\right) \\[1.3em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\sqrt{0}}{\cos(0)}\cdot 1\cdot \frac{1}{1} \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 0. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    We used \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(x)}{x}=1\) and also \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\sin(\sqrt{x})}{\sqrt{x}}=1\) by the substitution \(u=\sqrt{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    True/False Questions.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false. Justify your answer.
    🔗
    1.
    \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(\cos(x))=\sin(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If angles are measured in degrees, then \((\sin(x))'=\cos(x)\) still holds without modification.
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(f(x)=\cos(x)\tan(x)\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=\cos(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. We can use either algebra or the Product Rule. Since \(\tan(x)=\dfrac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\) wherever it is defined,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\cos(x)\tan(x)=\cos(x)\cdot\frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}=\sin(x) \end{equation*}
    on the domain where \(\tan(x)\) is defined. Then \(f'(x)=(\sin(x))'=\cos(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Alternatively, using the Product Rule directly,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f'(x) &=& (\cos(x))'\tan(x)+\cos(x)(\tan(x))' \\[0.5em] &=& -\sin(x)\tan(x)+\cos(x)\sec^2(x) \\[0.5em] &=& -\sin(x)\dfrac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}+\cos(x)\dfrac{1}{\cos^2(x)} \\[0.5em] &=& =\dfrac{1-\sin^2(x)}{\cos(x)} =\dfrac{\cos^2(x)}{\cos(x)}=\cos(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    The instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)=x^2\sin(x)\) at \(x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}\) is \(\pi\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The instantaneous rate of change at a point is the derivative at that point. Use the Product Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f'(x) &=& (x^2)'\sin(x)+x^2(\sin(x))' \\[0.5em] &=& 2x\sin(x)+x^2\cos(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Evaluate at \(x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f'\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right) &=& 2\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{2}\cdot\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right) +\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)^2\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right) \\[0.5em] &=& \pi\cdot 1 + \dfrac{\pi^2}{4}\cdot 0 \\[0.5em] &=& \pi. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the instantaneous rate of change at \(x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}\) is \(\pi\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    The slope of the tangent line to \(f(x)=\dfrac{\csc(x)}{\cot(x)}\) at \(x=0\) is \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. First rewrite \(f(x)\) in simpler form. Since \(\csc(x)=\dfrac{1}{\sin(x)}\) and \(\cot(x)=\dfrac{\cos(x)}{\sin(x)}\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{\csc(x)}{\cot(x)} = \frac{\dfrac{1}{\sin(x)}}{\dfrac{\cos(x)}{\sin(x)}} = \frac{1}{\cos(x)} = \sec(x), \end{equation*}
    on any interval where the expressions are defined. Thus \(f(x)=\sec(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = (\sec(x))' = \sec(x)\tan(x). \end{equation*}
    Evaluate at \(x=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(0) = \sec(0)\tan(0) = 1 \cdot 0 = 0. \end{equation*}
    The slope is \(0\text{,}\) not \(1\text{,}\) so the statement is false.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    Since \(\displaystyle\frac{\sin(x)}{x}=1\text{,}\) it follows that \(\displaystyle \left(\frac{\sin(x)}{x}\right)'=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The equation \(\sin(x)/x = 1\) is not true for all \(x\text{.}\) The expression equals \(1\) only in the limiting sense \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(x)}{x}=1\text{,}\) not as an identity. Since \(\sin(x)/x\) is not a constant function, its derivative is not zero. In fact, applying the Quotient Rule shows that \(\left(\dfrac{\sin(x)}{x}\right)'\) is nonzero for all \(x\neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(x^2)}{x}=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Rewrite as \(\frac{\sin(x^2)}{x^2}\cdot x\text{.}\) Since \(\sin(u)/u → 1\) as \(u→0\text{,}\) the product becomes \(1·0 = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos(x)}{x}=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. \(\cos(x)/x\) grows without bound as \(x→0\text{.}\) The limit does not exist as a finite number.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{x}{\tan(3x)}=\dfrac13\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Rewrite the expression to introduce the standard limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\tan(u)}{u}=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{x}{\tan(3x)} \amp= \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{3x}{\tan(3x)}\cdot \frac{1}{3}\right)\\ \amp= \left(\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{u}{\tan(u)}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{3} \quad \text{(using the substitution } u=3x\text{)}\\ \amp= 1\cdot \frac{1}{3}\\ \amp= \frac{1}{3}. \end{align*}
    Therefore the statement is true.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin(5x)}{\sin(2x)}=\frac52\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Replace each term with \(\sin(kx)/(kx)\text{:}\) \(\frac{\sin(5x)}{5x}\cdot\frac{2x}{\sin(2x)}\cdot \frac52 → 1·1·\frac52\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    11.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos(x)-1}{\sin(x)}=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Use the identity \(\cos(x)-1 = -2\sin^2(x/2)\text{.}\) Then the expression becomes \(-2\sin(x/2)\cdot\frac{\sin(x/2)}{\sin(x)}\text{,}\) which tends to \(0\text{.}\)
