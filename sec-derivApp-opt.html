    Section 3.8 Optimization Problems

    Objectives

    • Translate a word problem into a function to be maximized or minimized.
      🔗
    • Identify controlling variables, constraints, and the target quantity in an optimization setting.
      🔗
    • Determine a relevant domain based on physical or geometric restrictions.
      🔗
    • Find absolute maxima and minima using critical points and endpoint checks.
      🔗
    • Interpret an optimal solution in context and check that it is physically reasonable.
      🔗
    🔗
    In many applications, we are not just interested in computing derivatives. Instead, we use derivatives as a tool to make the best possible choice: the largest area, the smallest cost, the least time, or the greatest profit. These sorts of real-world problems are called optimization problems.
    🔗

    Subsection Motivating Example and Overall Technique

    Example 3.8.1.

    Suppose we have \(40\) meters of fencing to enclose a rectangular area. What dimensions maximize the area?
    🔗
    1. Click the Desmos model below to experiment with different rectangles and get a sense of the problem and for what the answer should be.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use calculus techniques to find the dimensions of the rectangle of greatest area.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    (a) Experimenting with the Desmos model suggests that the rectangle of greatest area is a square with side length 10 meters. We will confirm this using calculus in part (b).
    🔗
    (b) To solve this problem, we first identify the controlling variables, constraints, and target quantity.
    🔗
    Our target quantity that we are trying to maximize is the area of the rectangle (\(A=\ell w\)). The controlling variables are the length \(\ell\) and width \(w\) of the rectangle. The constraint is that the perimeter must be 40 meters.
    🔗
    Using the constraint, we can express one variable in terms of the other and write the area as a single-variable function.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 2\ell+2w \amp = \amp 40 \\ \ell+w \amp = \amp 20 \\ w \amp = \amp 20-\ell \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Using this we can express the area as a function of \(\ell\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A=\ell w=\ell(20-\ell)=20\ell-\ell^2. \end{equation*}
    The physical domain requires \(\ell\ge 0\) and \(w\ge 0\text{,}\) so \(\ell\in[0,20]\text{.}\) Now we find the absolute maximum of \(A(\ell)\) on \([0,20]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A'(\ell)=20-2\ell. \end{equation*}
    The critical point satisfies \(A'(\ell)=0\text{,}\) so \(\ell=10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the domain is a closed interval, we can use the Closed Interval Method to check the endpoints and the critical point:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A(0)=0,\qquad A(10)=100,\qquad A(20)=0. \end{equation*}
    So the largest Area value occurs at \(\ell=10\) meters. Therefore \(w=20-\ell=10\) meters. And so we can see from this example that the rectangle of greatest area is a square with side length 10 meters, confirming our initial guess from the Desmos model.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Since we are interested in finding absolute extrema, it is helpful that we already have a method for functions defined on a closed interval, as in the previous example. However, what happens when the domain is open or unbounded? Up to this point, our techniques in those settings allow us to find only local extrema. For that reason, the following two results will be especially useful.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.8.3. First Derivative Test for Absolute Extrema.

    Suppose that \(c\) is the only critical point of a continuous function \(f\) on an interval \(I\text{,}\) then the following hold:
    🔗
    • If \(f'(x)\gt 0\) for all \(x\lt c\) and \(f'(x)\lt 0\) for all \(x\gt c\text{,}\) then \(f(c)\) is an absolute maximum of \(f\) on \(I\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)\lt 0\) for all \(x\lt c\) and \(f'(x)\gt 0\) for all \(x\gt c\text{,}\) then \(f(c)\) is an absolute minimum of \(f\) on \(I\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.8.4. Second Derivative Test for Absolute Extrema.

    Suppose that \(c\) is the only critical point of a continuous function \(f\) on an interval \(I\) such that \(f'(c)=0\) , then the following hold:
    🔗
    • If \(f''(c)\lt 0\text{,}\) then \(f(c)\) is an absolute maximum of \(f\) on \(I\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(c)\gt 0\text{,}\) then \(f(c)\) is an absolute minimum of \(f\) on \(I\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s summarize the overall technique for solving optimization problems.
    🔗

    Strategy 3.8.5. Technique for Optimization.

    Most optimization problems follow the same workflow:
    🔗
    1. Draw a labeled picture (if appropriate). Identify the controlling variable(s), the constraint(s), and the target quantity you want to maximize or minimize.
      🔗
    2. Write equations that relate the variables using geometry, formulas, or context.
      🔗
    3. Choose one controlling variable as the independent variable and rewrite the target quantity as a single-variable function \(T(x)\) (in our previous example our target quantity was area). Determine the domain of \(x\text{.}\)
      🔗
    4. Find the desired absolute extrema of \(T(x)\) on its domain by one of
      • The Closed Interval Method 3.4.17 if the domain is a closed interval.
        🔗
        🔗
      • The First Derivative Test for Absolute Extrema 3.8.3 if the domain is open or unbounded and the critical point is the only candidate.
        🔗
        🔗
      • The Second Derivative Test for Absolute Extrema 3.8.4 if the domain is open or unbounded and the critical point is the only candidate (and it is difficult to analyze the sign of the first derivative).
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Interpret your answer in context and check that it makes sense.
      🔗
    🔗
    And with that we are ready to solve optimization problems! Similar to related rates problems, the key is to practice the technique on a variety of examples so that you can recognize the pattern and apply it efficiently.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection More Examples

    Example 3.8.6.

    A 10-quart bucket has cylindrical sides and a circular bottom (no top). What is the minimum surface area for such a bucket?
    🔗
    Hint: 10 quarts is \(\tfrac{1155}{2}\) cubic inches.
    🔗
    1. Use the Desmos graphing tool to visualize the situation, the surface area function, and approximate the optimal dimensions.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find the minimum surface area. What are the dimensions that minimize surface area? Confirm that they minimize surface area.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    The target quantity is the surface area
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} S \amp = \amp \text{area of bottom} + \text{area of sides} \\ S \amp = \amp \pi r^2 + 2\pi rh \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The constraint is the volume. Converting 10 quarts to cubic inches gives \(V=\tfrac{1155}{2} \text{in}^3\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V=\pi r^2h=\frac{1155}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Use the constraint to write \(h\) in terms of \(r\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} h=\frac{1155}{2\pi r^2}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute into \(S\) to obtain a single-variable function:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S(r)=\pi r^2+2\pi r\left(\frac{1155}{2\pi r^2}\right) =\pi r^2+\frac{1155}{r}. \end{equation*}
    The domain is \(r>0\text{.}\) Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S'(r)=2\pi r-\frac{1155}{r^2}. \end{equation*}
    Set \(S'(r)=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 2\pi r \amp = \amp \frac{1155}{r^2} \\ r^3 \amp = \amp \frac{1155}{2\pi} \\ r \amp = \amp \sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}} \approx 5.69 \text{ in}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Remark 3.8.8.
    Admittedly a calculator was used to get the 5.69 approximation above. However even without a calculator we can quickly and confidently say this value is between 5 and 6 inches.
    🔗
    🔗
    The second derivative is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S''(r)=2\pi+\frac{2310}{r^3}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(S''(r)>0\) for all \(r>0\text{,}\) the critical point is indeed a minimum by the Second Derivative Test for Absolute Extrema 3.8.4.
    🔗
    Now we can compute the corresponding height:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} h \amp =\amp \frac{1155}{2\pi r^2}\\ \amp =\amp \frac{1155}{2\pi\left(\sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}}\right)^2} \\ \amp =\amp \frac{1155}{2\pi\left(\frac{1155}{2\pi}\right)^{2/3}} \\ \amp =\amp \frac{1155}{2\pi}\cdot\left(\frac{2\pi}{1155}\right)^{2/3} \\ \amp =\amp \sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since this is the same value as \(r\text{,}\) we have \(h=r\text{.}\) This yields a bucket that is twice as wide as it is tall.
    🔗
    Now to get the minimum surface area, we can substitute the values of \(r\) and \(h\) into the surface area function:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} S_{min} \amp = \amp \pi r^2 + 2\pi rh\\ \amp = \amp \pi\left(\sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}}\right)^2 + 2\pi\left(\sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}}\right)\left(\sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}}\right) \\ \amp = \amp 3\pi\left(\sqrt[3]{\frac{1155}{2\pi}}\right)^2 \\ \amp = \amp 3\pi\left(\frac{1155}{2\pi}\right)^{2/3} \approx 304.7 \text{ in}^2. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Remark 3.8.9.
    Real buckets are often designed with other priorities besides minimizing material, such as stability, ease of carrying, stackability, and manufacturing constraints. Optimization models are most useful when we clearly state what is being optimized and what is being ignored.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.8.10.

    A line of ants travels from the lower-left corner to the upper-right corner of a \(10\) cm by \(10\) cm square of carpet. They may travel along the left or bottom edge at speed \(1\) cm/sec, and then cut across the carpet at speed \(\tfrac12\) cm/sec.
    🔗
    Let \(e\) be the distance traveled along an edge before entering the carpet. Find the value of \(e\) that minimizes: (a) the total distance traveled, and (b) the total travel time.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The diagonal segment across the carpet has length \(c\text{.}\) From the right triangle,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c^2=10^2+(10-e)^2. \end{equation*}
    Therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c=\sqrt{200-20e+e^2}. \end{equation*}
    The domain is \(e\in[0,10]\text{.}\) The total distance is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L(e)=e+c=e+\sqrt{200-20e+e^2}. \end{equation*}
    The total time is distance divided by speed. Along the edge the speed is \(1\text{,}\) so time is \(e\text{.}\) Across the carpet the speed is \(\tfrac12\text{,}\) so time is \(\frac{c}{1/2}=2c\text{.}\) Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T(e)=e+2\sqrt{200-20e+e^2}. \end{equation*}
    (a) Minimize distance. Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L'(e)=1+\frac{1}{2}(200-20e+e^2)^{-1/2}(2e-20) =1-\frac{10-e}{\sqrt{200-20e+e^2}}. \end{equation*}
    Solving \(L'(e)=0\) would require \(\sqrt{200-20e+e^2}=10-e\text{.}\) Squaring both sides gives \(200-20e+e^2=(10-e)^2=100-20e+e^2\text{,}\) which reduces to \(200=100\text{,}\) an impossibility. Therefore there are no critical points in \((0,10)\text{,}\) so the minimum occurs at an endpoint.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L(0)=10\sqrt{2},\qquad L(10)=20. \end{equation*}
    Since \(10\sqrt2<20\text{,}\) the minimum distance occurs at \(e=0\text{.}\) In other words, the straight diagonal is the shortest path.
    🔗
    (b) Minimize time. Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T'(e)=1+(2)\cdot\frac{1}{2}(200-20e+e^2)^{-1/2}(2e-20) =1-\frac{2(10-e)}{\sqrt{200-20e+e^2}}. \end{equation*}
    Set \(T'(e)=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{200-20e+e^2}=20-2e. \end{equation*}
    Square both sides and simplify:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 200-20e+e^2=(20-2e)^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 3e^2-60e+200=0. \end{equation*}
    Apply the quadratic formula:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e=\frac{60\pm\sqrt{60^2-4(3)(200)}}{2(3)} =10\pm\frac{10}{3}\sqrt{3}. \end{equation*}
    Only \(e=10-\frac{10}{3}\sqrt{3}\) lies in \([0,10]\text{,}\) so it is our only critical point. Compare to the endpoints:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T(0)=20\sqrt2,\qquad T(10)=30,\qquad T\!\left(10-\frac{10}{3}\sqrt3\right)=10+10\sqrt3. \end{equation*}
    Since \(10+10\sqrt3\) is the smallest of these values, the minimum time occurs at \(\displaystyle e=10-\frac{10}{3}\sqrt3\approx 4.2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Remark 3.8.11.
    Minimizing distance and minimizing time are different problems because the speed changes. The ants save time by traveling partway along the faster edge and then cutting across the slower carpet.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.8.12.

    A company produces a product for \(\$10\) per item and sells it for \(s\) dollars per item. Market demand is modeled by \(m(s)=100-s\text{,}\) meaning that when the price is \(s\text{,}\) the number of items sold is \(100-s\text{.}\)
    🔗
    What price \(s\) maximizes total profit?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The profit per item is \(s-10\text{,}\) and the number sold is \(100-s\text{.}\) Therefore total profit is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(s)=(100-s)(s-10)=-1000+110s-s^2. \end{equation*}
    The relevant domain is \(s\in[10,100]\text{,}\) since selling below cost is not sensible here, and demand reaches zero at \(s=100\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P'(s)=110-2s. \end{equation*}
    The critical point satisfies \(P'(s)=0\text{,}\) so \(s=55\text{.}\) Check the endpoints:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(10)=0,\qquad P(55)=2025,\qquad P(100)=0. \end{equation*}
    The maximum profit occurs at \(s=55\text{.}\) The most profitable selling price is \(\$55\) per item.

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Some True/False Problems.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false and briefly justify your reasoning.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(T'(c)=0\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) must give an absolute maximum or minimum of \(T\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. A critical point is only a candidate. The absolute extremum could occur at an endpoint, and some critical points are neither maxima nor minima.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    In an optimization problem on a closed interval, it is always necessary to check endpoints.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Absolute maxima and minima can occur at endpoints, so they must be included as candidates.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If the domain is \((0,\infty)\text{,}\) then an absolute minimum cannot exist.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. An absolute minimum may exist on an open or unbounded domain, but we must justify it by analyzing critical points and limits at the boundaries.
    🔗
    🔗
