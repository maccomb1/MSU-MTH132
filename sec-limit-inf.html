    Section 1.3 Limits at Infinity: Horizontal Asymptotes

    Objectives

    • Interpret limits as \(x\to\infty\) and \(x\to-\infty\) graphically as long-term behavior of a function.
      🔗
    • Identify horizontal asymptotes using limits at infinity.
      🔗
    • Evaluate limits at infinity using dominant term analysis for rational and algebraic functions.
      🔗
    • Apply the Squeeze Theorem to evaluate limits at infinity involving bounded functions.
      🔗
    • Explain the meaning of a limit at infinity in an applied context.
      🔗
    🔗
    To understand the behavior of a graph far to the left or far to the right on the \(x\)-axis, we introduce limits in which \(x\) becomes arbitrarily large in the positive or negative direction. These limits describe how a function behaves near the ends of its graph.
    🔗

    Definition 1.3.1.

    Let \(f\) be a function and let \(L\) be a real number.
    🔗
    • \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\) means that \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) by taking \(x\) sufficiently large.
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)=L\) means that \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) by taking \(x\) sufficiently negative.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Graphically, if \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\text{,}\) then the graph of \(y=f(x)\) approaches the horizontal line \(y=L\) toward the right. This line is called a horizontal asymptote. A similar interpretation applies toward the left when \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)=L\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection Visualizing Limits at Infinity

    Limits at infinity are often easiest to understand by looking at graphs. While algebra tells us what happens analytically, graphs help us see how the function behaves for very large values of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Even if the graph crosses the horizontal asymptote many times, the key idea is that the function values remain close to \(L\) once \(x\) is sufficiently large.
    🔗
    When
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=\infty \end{equation*}
    or \(-\infty\text{,}\) the graph does not level off, and no horizontal asymptote exists on that end.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Basic Limits at Infinity

    The most fundamental limits at infinity come from power functions. If \(p>0\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} x^p = \infty, \qquad \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} = 0. \end{equation*}
    These limits explain why higher powers of \(x\) dominate lower powers, constants, and reciprocal terms as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Dominant Terms and Rational Functions

    For functions made from sums and quotients of powers of \(x\text{,}\) limits at infinity are determined by the terms with the highest powers. These are called the dominant terms.
    🔗

    Example 1.3.4.

    Determine whether \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-6x+9}{x^3-6x^2+11x-6}\) has a horizontal asymptote.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The dominant term in the numerator is \(x^2\text{,}\) and the dominant term in the denominator is \(x^3\text{.}\) For large \(x\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \approx \frac{x^2}{x^3} = \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} \frac{1}{x}=0\text{,}\) the graph approaches the horizontal asymptote \(y=0\) on both ends.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.5.

    Find the horizontal asymptote of \(f(x)=\dfrac{3x^2-x+9}{5x^2+2x-6}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The dominant terms are \(3x^2\) and \(5x^2\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x)=\frac{3}{5}. \end{equation*}
    The graph approaches the horizontal line \(y=\frac35\) on both ends.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.6.

    Determine the behavior of
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \dfrac{x^2+3x^{7/2}-x^{-5}}{9x\sqrt{x} + 4x^2\sqrt{x}} \end{equation*}
    as \(x\to\pm\infty\text{,}\) and decide whether a horizontal asymptote exists.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First rewrite all terms using powers of \(x\text{.}\) In the denominator,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 9x\sqrt{x}=9x^{3/2}, \qquad 4x^2\sqrt{x}=4x^{5/2}. \end{equation*}
    The dominant term in the numerator is \(3x^{7/2}\text{,}\) and the dominant term in the denominator is \(4x^{5/2}\text{.}\) For large values of \(x\text{,}\) the function behaves like
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \approx \frac{3x^{7/2}}{4x^{5/2}}. \end{equation*}
    Simplifying,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x^{7/2}}{4x^{5/2}} = \frac34 x. \end{equation*}
    Since \(\lim_{x\to\infty} \frac34 x = \infty\text{,}\) we conclude that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=\infty. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, this function does not have a horizontal asymptote. Instead, the approximation \(f(x)\approx \frac34 x\) shows that the right end of the graph grows roughly like a line with slope \(\frac34\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The function is not defined for \(x<0\) (because of the square roots), so there is no left-end behavior to consider.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Squeeze Theorem at Infinity

    The Squeeze Theorem can also be applied to limits at infinity when a function is trapped between two simpler functions.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3.7.

    If \(g(x)\le f(x)\le h(x)\) for all sufficiently large \(x\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} g(x)=\lim_{x\to\infty} h(x)=L, \end{equation*}
    then \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.8.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} \dfrac{\sin(x)}{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(-1\le\sin(x)\le1\text{,}\) dividing by \(x>0\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{x} \le \frac{\sin(x)}{x} \le \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    Both bounds approach \(0\) as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\) By the Squeeze Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection An Application of Limits at Infinity

    Limits at infinity are useful whenever a quantity approaches a stable long-term value. In these situations, a horizontal asymptote represents an equilibrium.
    🔗

    Example 1.3.9.

    The population \(P(t)\) of a species of fish in a lake \(t\) years after conservation measures are introduced is modeled by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(t)=\frac{1200t}{t+20}. \end{equation*}
    Evaluate and interpret \(\displaystyle\lim_{t\to\infty} P(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To evaluate the limit, we compare dominant terms in the numerator and denominator.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{1200t}{t+20} = \lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{1200t}{t} = 1200. \end{equation*}
    This means that as time passes, the population approaches \(1200\) fish.
    🔗
    In context, the value \(1200\) represents the long-term sustainable population of the lake. The population increases over time but does not exceed this level, which corresponds to the horizontal asymptote \(y=1200\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.10.

    The concentration \(C(t)\) of a medication in a patient’s bloodstream \(t\) hours after administration is modeled by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C(t)=\frac{2500t+4000}{2t^2+40t+100}. \end{equation*}
    Evaluate and interpret \(\displaystyle\lim_{t\to\infty} C(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To evaluate the limit, we compare the dominant terms in the numerator and denominator.
    🔗
    The dominant term in the numerator is \(2500t\text{,}\) while the dominant term in the denominator is \(2t^2\text{.}\) For large values of \(t\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C(t)\approx \frac{2500t}{2t^2}=\frac{1250}{t}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(\displaystyle\lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{1250}{t}=0\text{,}\) we conclude that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to\infty} C(t)=0. \end{equation*}
    This means the concentration approaches \(0\) over time. In context, this indicates that the medication is gradually eliminated from the bloodstream, and no medication remains in the long term.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Limits at Infinity Beyond Algebraic Functions

    Not all limits at infinity can be evaluated using dominant term analysis or algebraic simplification. Some functions require ideas from other areas of mathematics, and others cannot be evaluated until we develop additional calculus tools later in the course.
    🔗

    Example 1.3.11.

    Consider the function \(f(x)=\arctan(x)\text{.}\) Evaluate \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The inverse tangent function has a restricted range:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{\pi}{2} < \arctan(x) < \frac{\pi}{2} \quad\text{for all } x. \end{equation*}
    From trigonometry, as \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) the angle whose tangent is \(x\) approaches \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) and as \(x\to-\infty\text{,}\) it approaches \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} \arctan(x)=\frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad \lim_{x\to-\infty} \arctan(x)=-\frac{\pi}{2}. \end{equation*}
    This function has two different horizontal asymptotes: \(y=\frac{\pi}{2}\) to the right and \(y=-\frac{\pi}{2}\) to the left.
    🔗
    Notice that dominant term analysis does not apply here, since \(\arctan(x)\) is not an algebraic function. Instead, the limits are determined by the trigonometric meaning of the function.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.3.12.

    There are many functions whose limits at infinity cannot yet be evaluated with the techniques in this section. For example, consider \(\displaystyle \frac{e^x}{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Although both the numerator and denominator grow without bound as \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) the dominant-term ideas developed here do not apply. Evaluating limits of this type requires additional tools, such as L’Hôpital’s Rule, which will be introduced later in the course.
    🔗
    For now, it is enough to recognize when a limit can be evaluated using algebraic methods and when it cannot.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Some True/False Problems.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false and explain your reasoning
    🔗
    1.
    If \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\text{,}\) then \(f(x)=L\) for all sufficiently large \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The statement \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\) means that \(f(x)\) can be made arbitrarily close to \(L\) by taking \(x\) sufficiently large. It does not require \(f(x)\) to equal \(L\) for any value of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For example, \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}\) satisfies \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=0\text{,}\) but \(\dfrac{1}{x}\neq 0\) for any finite \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} \bigl(f(x)+3\bigr)=L+3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This follows from the Limit Laws for limits at infinity: adding a constant shifts the limiting value by the same constant.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(\deg(\text{numerator}) < \deg(\text{denominator})\) for a rational function \(f(x)=\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. If the denominator has higher degree, then the denominator grows faster in magnitude than the numerator as \(|x|\to\infty\text{,}\) so the quotient approaches \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    One way to justify this is to divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of \(x\) in the denominator. Every term in the numerator becomes a power of \(\dfrac{1}{x}\) (or higher), which goes to \(0\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\text{,}\) while the denominator approaches a nonzero constant.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=5\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Limits as \(x\to\infty\) and \(x\to-\infty\) describe the behavior of a function on different ends of the graph and are independent of one another.
    🔗
    For example, the function \(f(x)=\arctan(x)\) satisfies \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=\frac{\pi}{2}\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty} f(x)=-\frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) which are not equal.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    For \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2+3x^{7/2}-x^{-5}}{9x\sqrt{x}+4x^2\sqrt{x}}\text{,}\) the function has a horizontal asymptote as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Rewrite the denominator using powers: \(9x\sqrt{x}=9x^{3/2}\) and \(4x^2\sqrt{x}=4x^{5/2}\text{.}\) The dominant term in the numerator is \(3x^{7/2}\) and in the denominator is \(4x^{5/2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, for large \(x\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)\approx \frac{3x^{7/2}}{4x^{5/2}}=\frac34 x, \end{equation*}
    so \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=\infty\text{.}\) Since the function does not approach a finite number, it has no horizontal asymptote as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If a function satisfies \(-\dfrac{1000}{x} \le f(x) \le \dfrac{1000}{x}\) for all \(x>0\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Since \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{1000}{x}=0\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}-\frac{1000}{x}=0\text{,}\) the Squeeze Theorem implies that \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} \dfrac{\cos(x)}{x}\) does not exist because \(\cos(x)\) oscillates.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Although \(\cos(x)\) oscillates, it is bounded: \(-1\le \cos(x)\le 1\) for all \(x\text{.}\) For \(x>0\text{,}\) dividing by \(x\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{x}\le \frac{\cos(x)}{x}\le \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    Both bounds approach \(0\) as \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) so by the Squeeze Theorem, \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} \dfrac{\cos(x)}{x}=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    If \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=\infty\text{,}\) then \(y=f(x)\) has no horizontal asymptote to the right.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. A horizontal asymptote to the right would require \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=L\) for some finite real number \(L\text{.}\) If instead the function grows without bound so that \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)=\infty\text{,}\) then it cannot approach any horizontal line \(y=L\text{.}\)
