    Section 0.8 Trigonometry

    Trigonometric functions are fundamental tools in calculus. They arise in problems involving motion, waves, oscillations, and periodic behavior, and they play a key role in limits, derivatives, and integrals. In this section, we review the core definitions and properties of trigonometric functions that will be used throughout the course.
    🔗

    Subsection Right Triangle Definitions

    We begin by defining sine and cosine using right triangles. These definitions apply when the angle is acute.
    🔗

    Definition 0.8.1. Sine and Cosine (Right Triangle).

    Given a right triangle and an acute angle \(\theta\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sin(\theta) = \dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \qquad \cos(\theta) = \dfrac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 0.8.3.

    In a right triangle, the side opposite \(\theta\) has length 3, the adjacent side has length 4, and the hypotenuse has length 5. Find \(\sin(\theta)\) and \(\cos(\theta)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \sin(\theta) = \dfrac{3}{5}, \qquad \cos(\theta) = \dfrac{4}{5}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.4.

    While these definitions are intuitive, they apply only to acute angles. To extend trigonometric functions to all real numbers, we use the unit circle.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Unit Circle Definition

    The unit circle allows us to define trigonometric functions for any real number and is the framework used in calculus.
    🔗

    Definition 0.8.5. Sine and Cosine (Unit Circle).

    Consider the unit circle centered at the origin. For a real number \(\theta\text{,}\) let the point on the unit circle corresponding to angle \(\theta\) (measured in radians) have coordinates \((x,y)\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \cos(\theta) = x, \qquad \sin(\theta) = y. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.7.

    In calculus, angles are always measured in radians. This choice ensures that derivatives and limits involving trigonometric functions behave correctly.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.8.8.

    Evaluate \(\sin(\pi/2)\) and \(\cos(\pi)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \sin(\pi/2) = 1, \qquad \cos(\pi) = -1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.9.

    For acute angles, the unit circle and right triangle definitions of sine and cosine agree. The unit circle framework extends these ideas to all real angles.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.10.

    Because rotating by \(2\pi\) returns to the same point on the unit circle, sine and cosine are periodic with period \(2\pi\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Other Trigonometric Functions

    In addition to sine and cosine, four other trigonometric functions are commonly used in calculus: tangent, secant, cosecant, and cotangent. These functions are defined in terms of sine and cosine.
    🔗

    Definition 0.8.11. Definitions of Tangent, Secant, Cosecant, and Cotangent.

    For an angle \(\theta\) such that the expressions are defined,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \tan(\theta) = \dfrac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)}, \qquad \sec(\theta) = \dfrac{1}{\cos(\theta)}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \csc(\theta) = \dfrac{1}{\sin(\theta)}, \qquad \cot(\theta) = \dfrac{\cos(\theta)}{\sin(\theta)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.12.

    Because these functions are defined as ratios, they are undefined whenever their denominators are zero. For example, since \(\cos(\pi/2)=0\text{,}\) both \(\tan(\pi/2)\) and \(\sec(\pi/2)\) are undefined.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Key Unit Circle Values

    Because calculus problems often require exact values rather than decimal approximations, it is important to know certain unit circle values without a calculator.
    🔗
    The most important angles on the unit circle are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0, \; \frac{\pi}{6}, \; \frac{\pi}{4}, \; \frac{\pi}{3}, \; \frac{\pi}{2}. \end{equation*}
    The corresponding sine and cosine values are:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|cc} \theta & \sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \\ \hline 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \dfrac{\pi}{6} & \dfrac{1}{2} & \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \\ \dfrac{\pi}{4} & \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ \dfrac{\pi}{3} & \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \dfrac{1}{2} \\ \dfrac{\pi}{2} & 1 & 0 \end{array} \end{equation*}

    Example 0.8.13.

    Evaluate \(\sin(\pi/3)\) and \(\cos(\pi/4)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    These values can be read directly from the table:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sin(\pi/3) = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \qquad \cos(\pi/4) = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Values in other quadrants can be determined using symmetry and the signs of sine and cosine on the unit circle. On quizzes and exams, these values are expected to be known without a calculator.
    🔗

    Example 0.8.14.

    Evaluate \(\sin(5\pi/6)\) and \(\cos(5\pi/6)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The angle \(5\pi/6\) lies in Quadrant II and has reference angle \(\pi/6\text{.}\) In Quadrant II, sine is positive and cosine is negative.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sin(5\pi/6) = \sin(\pi/6) = \dfrac{1}{2}, \qquad \cos(5\pi/6) = -\cos(\pi/6) = -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Range and Boundedness

    One of the most important properties of sine and cosine in calculus is that their values are bounded.
    🔗

    Theorem 0.8.15. Range of Sine and Cosine.

    For all real numbers \(\theta\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} -1 \le \sin(\theta) \le 1, \qquad -1 \le \cos(\theta) \le 1. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.16.

    This boundedness property is used frequently in calculus, especially when applying the Squeeze Theorem to evaluate limits.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Pythagorean Identities

    One of the most important consequences of the unit circle definition of sine and cosine is a collection of identities that come directly from the Pythagorean Theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 0.8.17. Pythagorean Identities.

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) & = & 1 \\[4pt] 1 + \tan^2(\theta) & = & \sec^2(\theta) \\[4pt] 1 + \cot^2(\theta) & = & \csc^2(\theta) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Only the first identity, \(\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1\text{,}\) needs to be memorized. The other two identities follow directly from it by dividing both sides by \(\cos^2(\theta)\) or \(\sin^2(\theta)\text{,}\) respectively.
    🔗

    Remark 0.8.18.

    These identities provide different ways to express the same geometric relationship. In calculus, choosing the appropriate form often simplifies a computation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.8.19.

    Simplify the expression \(1 + \tan^2(\theta)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} 1 + \tan^2(\theta) = \sec^2(\theta). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Angle Sum and Double Angle Formulas

    Trigonometric functions do not satisfy distributive or additive properties. Instead, they obey specific identities that describe how sine and cosine behave when angles are added or multiplied.
    🔗

    Theorem 0.8.20. Angle Sum Formulas.

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \sin(A+B) & = & \sin(A)\cos(B) + \cos(A)\sin(B) \\ \cos(A+B) & = & \cos(A)\cos(B) - \sin(A)\sin(B) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem 0.8.21. Double Angle Formulas.

    The double angle formulas are obtained by setting \(B = A\) in the angle sum formulas.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \sin(2A) & = & 2\sin(A)\cos(A) \\ \cos(2A) & = & \cos^2(A) - \sin^2(A) \\ & = & 2\cos^2(A) - 1 \\ & = & 1 - 2\sin^2(A) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
