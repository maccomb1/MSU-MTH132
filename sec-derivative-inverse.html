    Section 2.7 Inverse Functions and Their Derivatives

    Objectives

    • Use implicit differentiation to derive the formula for the derivative of an inverse function.
      🔗
    • Compute derivatives of functions involving logarithmic, trigonometric, and inverse trigonometric functions.
      🔗
    • Interpret derivative values of inverse functions geometrically as slopes of tangent lines.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Natural Logarithm and Finding Derivatives of Inverse Functions

    Here we will demonstrate how we can use implicit differentiation to find the derivative of the natural logarithm function, \(y=\ln x\text{.}\) We will use this same idea to find the derivatives of other inverse functions as well.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.7.2.

    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(y=\ln x\text{.}\) Then by the inverse relationship with the exponential function,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} y \amp = \amp \ln x \\ e^y \amp = \amp e^{\ln x} \\ e^y \amp = \amp x. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\) to get:
    \begin{equation*} e^y \cdot y' = 1. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} y' = \frac{1}{e^y}. \end{equation*}
    Now rewrite \(e^y\) in terms of \(x\text{.}\) Since we know from above that \(e^y = x\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} y' = \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s take this idea and generalize it to find a formula for the derivative of the inverse of any differentiable function.
    🔗

    Strategy 2.7.3. Finding the Derivative of an Inverse Function.

    • Start with a differentiable function \(y=f(x)\) and its inverse \(f^{-1}\text{.}\) Then by the definition of inverse functions, \(f^{-1}(y)=x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{,}\) treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Solve for \(y'\text{,}\) then rewrite the result in terms of \(x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s use this idea to find the formulas for the derivatives of various inverse trigonometric functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Derivatives of Inverse Trig Functions

    Theorem 2.7.4. Derivative of \(\arcsin(x)\).

    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arcsin(x)\bigr)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(y=\arcsin(x)\text{.}\) Then \(\sin(y)=x\text{.}\) Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\sin(y)\bigr) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle \cos(y)\,y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1 \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{\cos(y)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now rewrite \(\cos(y)\) in terms of \(x\text{.}\) Since \(\sin(y)=x\) and \(y=\arcsin(x)\) has range \(\displaystyle -\frac{\pi}{2}\le y\le \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) we have \(\cos(y)\ge 0\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \cos(y)=\sqrt{1-\sin^2(y)}=\sqrt{1-x^2}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem 2.7.5. Derivative of \(\arccos(x)\).

    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arccos(x)\bigr)=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(y=\arccos(x)\text{.}\) Then \(\cos(y)=x\text{.}\) Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\cos(y)\bigr) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle -\sin(y)\,y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1 \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle -\frac{1}{\sin(y)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\cos(y)=x\) and \(0\le y\le \pi\text{,}\) we have \(\sin(y)\ge 0\text{,}\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \sin(y)=\sqrt{1-\cos^2(y)}=\sqrt{1-x^2}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem 2.7.6. Derivative of \(\arctan(x)\).

    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arctan(x)\bigr)=\frac{1}{1+x^2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(y=\arctan(x)\text{,}\) so \(\tan(y)=x\text{.}\) Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\tan(y)\bigr) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle \sec^2(y)\,y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1 \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{\sec^2(y)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Use the pythagorean identity \(\sec^2(y)=1+\tan^2(y)\) and \(\tan(y)=x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'=\frac{1}{1+x^2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 2.7.7. Notations of Inverse Trigonometric Functions.

    Note that some both \(\sin^{-1}(x)\text{,}\) \(\cos^{-1}(x)\text{,}\) and \(\tan^{-1}(x)\) along with \(\arcsin(x)\text{,}\) \(\arccos(x)\text{,}\) and \(\arctan(x)\) are valid notations for the inverse trigonometric functions. We will use them interchangeably in our course. Students are expected to be comfortable with both notations.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Select Examples

    Now that we have the formulas for the derivatives of various inverse functions, let’s see some example of problems that would make reasonable homework, quiz, or exam questions.
    🔗

    Example 2.7.8.

    Find \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\ln(\sin x)\bigr)\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arcsin(5x)\bigr)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    For \(\ln(\sin x)\text{,}\) use the Chain Rule with outer function \(\ln(u)\) and inner function \(u=\sin x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\ln(\sin x)\bigr) =\frac{1}{\sin x}\cdot \cos x =\frac{\cos x}{\sin x}. \end{equation*}
    For \(\arcsin(5x)\text{,}\) use the Chain Rule and the derivative of \(\arcsin(x)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arcsin(5x)\bigr) =\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-(5x)^2}}\cdot 5 =\frac{5}{\sqrt{1-25x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.9.

    Find the derivative of \(f(x)=\sqrt{2+\ln x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiate using the Chain Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle (2+\ln x)^{1/2} \\[0.8em] \displaystyle f'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2}(2+\ln x)^{-1/2}\cdot \frac{d}{dx}(2+\ln x) \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2}(2+\ln x)^{-1/2}\cdot \frac{1}{x} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2x\sqrt{2+\ln x}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.10.

    Find a point on the curve \(y=\tan^{-1}(x)\) where the slope is \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{10}\text{.}\) Is there more than one answer?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\tan^{-1}(x)\bigr)=\frac{1}{1+x^2}\text{,}\) we want
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{1}{1+x^2}=\frac{1}{10}. \end{equation*}
    Solve for \(x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle 1+x^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 10 \\[0.8em] \displaystyle x^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 9 \\[0.8em] \displaystyle x \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pm 3. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So there are two points. When \(x=3\text{,}\) \(y=\tan^{-1}(3)\text{,}\) and when \(x=-3\text{,}\) \(y=\tan^{-1}(-3)=-\tan^{-1}(3)\text{.}\) Therefore the slope is \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{10}\) at
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle (3,\tan^{-1}(3)) \quad \text{and} \quad (-3,-\tan^{-1}(3)). \end{equation*}
    Yes, there is more than one answer because \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{1+x^2}\) depends on \(x^2\text{,}\) so it has the same value at \(x\) and \(-x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.11.

    Suppose that \(f\) is differentiable, \(f(2)=3\text{,}\) and \(f'(2)=5\text{.}\) Find \((f^{-1})'(3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(y=f^{-1}(x)\text{.}\) Then \(f(y)=x\text{.}\) Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(y)\cdot y'=1. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'=\frac{1}{f'(y)}. \end{equation*}
    Now evaluate at \(x=3\text{.}\) Since \(y=f^{-1}(x)\text{,}\) we have \(y=f^{-1}(3)=2\) because \(f(2)=3\text{.}\) Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle (f^{-1})'(3)=\frac{1}{f'(2)}=\frac{1}{5}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    True/False Questions.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false. Justify your answer.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(f(x)=\ln(g(x))\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{g(x)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. By the Chain Rule, \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\ln(g(x))\right)=\dfrac{1}{g(x)}\cdot g'(x)\text{.}\) The factor \(g'(x)\) is missing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If \(f(x)=\ln(x^2+1)\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Let \(u=x^2+1\text{.}\) Then \(f(x)=\ln(u)\text{.}\) By the Chain Rule, \(f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{u}\cdot u'=\dfrac{1}{x^2+1}\cdot 2x=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arcsin(5x)\bigr)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-25x^2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The Chain Rule introduces a factor of 5.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(f(x)=\dfrac{\arccos(x)}{\ln(x)}\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=\dfrac{-x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The derivative requires the Quotient Rule.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=\dfrac{-\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\ln(x)-\arccos(x)\cdot \dfrac{1}{x}}{(\ln(x))^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    The derivative of \(\arctan(x)\) is always less than 1.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Since \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\arctan(x)\bigr)=\frac{1}{1+x^2}\) and \(1+x^2 \geq 1\) for all real \(x\text{,}\) the derivative is always less than or equal to 1, and equal to 1 only at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(f(x) = \arcsin(2x)\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle f'(1) \geq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The derivative of \(f(x) = \arcsin(2x)\) is \(f'(x) = \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{1-4x^2}}\text{.}\) At \(x=1\text{,}\) the expression under the square root is negative, so \(f'(1)\) is undefined. Therefore, \(f'(1) \ne 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    If \(f(x)=\arctan(x)\text{,}\) then at \(x=1\) the tangent line equation is given by \(y = \tfrac12(x-1) + \dfrac{\pi}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The derivative of \(f(x)=\arctan(x)\) is \(f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}\text{.}\) At \(x=1\text{,}\) \(f'(1)=\tfrac12\) and \(f(1)=\tfrac{\pi}{4}\text{,}\) so the tangent line equation is \(y=\tfrac12(x-1)+\tfrac{\pi}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    If \(f\) is one-to-one and differentiable and contains the point \((a,b)\) and \(f^{-1}\) is its inverse, then \((f^{-1})'(b) = \dfrac{1}{f'(a)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Consider \(y=f^{-1}(x)\text{,}\) then \(f(y)=x \) so \(f'(y)\cdot y'=1\text{,}\) which gives \(y'=\dfrac{1}{f'(y)}\text{.}\) Since \(a=f^{-1}(b)\text{,}\) therefore
    \begin{equation*} (f^{-1})'(b)=\dfrac{1}{f'(a)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(f(x)=2^x+x\) and note that \(f(1)=3\text{,}\) then \((f^{-1})'(3)=\dfrac{1}{2\ln(2)+1} \text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Consider \(y=f^{-1}(x)\text{,}\) then \(f(y)=x \) so \(f'(y)\cdot y'=1\text{,}\) which gives \(y'=\dfrac{1}{f'(y)}\text{.}\) Since \(y=f^{-1}(3)\text{,}\) we have \(f(y)=3\text{,}\) so \(y=1\text{.}\) Therefore, \((f^{-1})'(3)=\dfrac{1}{f'(1)}=\dfrac{1}{2\ln(2)+1}.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    If \(f\) has a horizontal tangent at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f^{-1}\) has a vertical tangent at \(x=f(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. If \(f\) has a horizontal tangent at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f'(a)=0\text{.}\) Since \((f^{-1})'(f(a))=\dfrac{1}{f'(a)}\text{,}\) it follows that \((f^{-1})'(f(a))=\dfrac{1}{0}\text{,}\) which is undefined. Therefore, \(f^{-1}\) has a vertical tangent at \(x=f(a)\text{.}\) We can also verify this by sketching the graphs of \(f\) and \(f^{-1}\) and remembering that they are reflections across the line \(y=x\text{.}\)

    11.
    If \(f\) is increasing, then \(f^{-1}\) is decreasing.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Both are increasing.
    🔗
    If \(f\) is increasing, then \(f'(x) > 0\) for all \(x\) in the domain of \(f\text{.}\) Since \((f^{-1})'(f(x)) = \dfrac{1}{f'(x)}\text{,}\) and \(f'(x) > 0\text{,}\) it follows that \((f^{-1})'(f(x)) > 0\text{.}\) Therefore, \(f^{-1}\) is also increasing.

    12.
    Consider a function \(f\) and its inverse \(f^{-1}\) as shown in the figure below. The function \(f\) contains the point \((4,2)\) and \(f^{-1}\) contains the point \((2,4)\text{.}\) Since the functions are inverses of each other, the two tangent lines at these corresponding points must be perpendicular to each other.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The slopes of the tangent lines at corresponding points of a function and its inverse are multiplicative inverses of each other, not negative reciprocals. Therefore, the tangent lines are not necessarily perpendicular. You can also sketch the tangent lines to see that they are not perpendicular.
