    Section 2.6 Implicit Differentiation

    Objectives

    • Recognize when a relationship between \(x\) and \(y\) requires implicit differentiation rather than treating \(y\) as an explicit function \(y=f(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Differentiate equations that involve both \(x\) and \(y\) by treating \(y=y(x)\text{,}\) applying the Product Rule, Quotient Rule, and Chain Rule, and then solving for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Compute slopes of tangent lines and write equations of tangent lines for curves defined implicitly, including identifying where tangents are horizontal or vertical.
      🔗
    🔗
    In earlier sections, we found tangent lines and rates of change by differentiating functions written in the form \(y=f(x)\text{.}\) However, many important curves are not given as explicit functions of \(x\text{.}\) Instead, they are described by equations that relate \(x\) and \(y\) implicitly. In this section, we develop a technique called implicit differentiation that allows us to compute derivatives directly from such equations.
    🔗

    Subsection A Motivating Example

    Consider the circle defined by the equation \(x^2+y^2=25\text{.}\) If we wish to find the slope of the tangent line at the point \((3,4)\text{,}\) we immediately face a difficulty: there is no function specified in the form \(y=f(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    One possible approach is to make the equation explicit by solving for \(y\text{.}\) Let’s consider this method first
    🔗

    Example 2.6.2.

    Find the slope of the tangent line to the circle \(x^2+y^2=25\) at the point \((3,4)\) by first solving for \(y=f(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We begin by solving the equation of the circle for \(y\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y = \pm\sqrt{25-x^2}. \end{equation*}
    This shows that the circle is the union of two function graphs.
    🔗
    Since the point \((3,4)\) lies on the upper semicircle, we use \(y=\sqrt{25-x^2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Differentiate using the Chain Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(25-x^2)^{1/2} = \frac12(25-x^2)^{-1/2}(-2x) = -\frac{x}{\sqrt{25-x^2}}. \end{equation*}
    Evaluating at \(x=3\) gives \(\frac{dy}{dx}=-\frac34\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.6.4.

    While solving an equation for \(y\) can sometimes allow us to apply standard differentiation rules, this approach is often inconvenient and may even be impossible. In many equations, even relatively simple ones, such as \(e^{y}=x+y\text{,}\) the variable \(y\) appears in multiple locations and cannot be isolated as an explicit function of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Implicit differentiation avoids this difficulty by treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\) and differentiating both sides of the equation directly, using the Chain Rule wherever \(y\) appears.
    🔗
    🔗
    Instead of solving for \(y\text{,}\) we assume that \(y=y(x)\) is an unknown function of \(x\) that satisfies the equation. Then we can differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 2.6.5.

    Use implicit differentiation to find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\text{,}\) and determine the slope of the tangent line at the point \((3,4)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We treat \(y\) as a function of \(x\) and differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2 + y^2) &=& \dfrac{d}{dx}(25) \\[0.6em] (x^2)' + (y^2)' &=& 0 \\[0.6em] 2x + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} &=& 0. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Remark 2.6.6.
    The derivative \((x^2)' = 2x\) follows from a basic derivative rule. In contrast, differentiating \(y^2\) requires the Chain Rule, since \(y\) is itself a function of \(x\text{.}\) Here the outside function is \((\ \ )^2\) and the inside function is \(y = y(x)\text{.}\) As a result, the derivative introduces the unknown quantity \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\text{,}\) which we can then solve for algebraically.
    🔗
    🔗
    We now solve for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y}. \end{equation*}
    This derivative formula applies to every point on the circle. Evaluating at \((3,4)\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left.\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right|_{(3,4)} = -\dfrac{3}{4}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Let us now formalize this process into a general technique.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Key Idea: Implicit Differentiation

    Definition 2.6.7. Implicit Differentiation.

    Implicit differentiation consists of differentiating both sides of an equation with respect to \(x\) and then solving the resulting equation for \(y'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In the examples and exercises of this section, it is assumed that the given equation determines \(y\) implicitly as a differentiable function of \(x\text{,}\) so that implicit differentiation applies.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.6.8.

    The key feature is that \(y\) depends on \(x\text{,}\) even if the equation does not explicitly solve for \(y\text{.}\) So whenever you differentiate an expression containing \(y\text{,}\) you must use the Chain Rule and include a factor of \(y'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.6.9.

    Given the equation \(x\ln(y)+y^2=4x\text{,}\) find \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) using implicit differentiation.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{.}\) The left side requires the Product Rule on \(x\ln(y)\) and the Chain Rule on \(\ln(y)\) and \(y^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(x\ln(y)\bigr)+\frac{d}{dx}\bigl(y^2\bigr) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(4x) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle \Bigl(1\cdot \ln(y)+x\cdot \frac{1}{y}\,y'\Bigr)+2y\,y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 4. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now solve for \(y'\) by collecting terms with \(y'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{x}{y}\,y'+2y\,y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 4-\ln(y) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y'\left(\frac{x}{y}+2y\right) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 4-\ln(y) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{4-\ln(y)}{\frac{x}{y}+2y}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    A common simplification is to multiply top and bottom by \(y\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'=\frac{y(4-\ln(y))}{x+2y^2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.6.10.

    Find an equation of the tangent line to the curve \(y e^{\sin x}=x\cos y\) at the point \((0,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{.}\) Use the Product Rule on both sides.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(ye^{\sin x}\bigr) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(x\cos y\bigr) \\[1.4em] \displaystyle y'e^{\sin x}+y\cdot e^{\sin x}\cdot(\cos x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1\cdot \cos y + x\cdot \frac{d}{dx}(\cos y) \\[1.4em] \displaystyle y'e^{\sin x}+y e^{\sin x}\cos x \amp=\amp \displaystyle \cos y + x\cdot\bigl(-\sin y\bigr)\,y'. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now substitute \((x,y)=(0,0)\text{.}\) Note \(\sin(0)=0\text{,}\) \(e^0=1\text{,}\) \(\cos(0)=1\text{,}\) and \(\sin(0)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle y'(1)+0 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1+0 \\[0.8em] \displaystyle y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The slope at \((0,0)\) is 1, so the tangent line is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y=x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.6.11.

    Find \(y'\) if \(y^3+x^3=6xy\text{.}\) At what point in the first quadrant is the tangent line to this curve horizontal?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{.}\) On the right side, use the Product Rule on \(6xy\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(y^3)+\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(6xy) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle 3y^2y'+3x^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 6\bigl(xy'+y\bigr) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle 3y^2y'+3x^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 6xy'+6y. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Solve for \(y'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle 3y^2y'-6xy' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 6y-3x^2 \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y'(3y^2-6x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 3(2y-x^2) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle y' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{3(2y-x^2)}{3y^2-6x} \ =\ \frac{2y-x^2}{y^2-2x}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    A horizontal tangent occurs when \(\displaystyle y'=0\text{,}\) which requires the numerator to be zero (and the denominator nonzero):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 2y-x^2=0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y=\frac{x^2}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Substitute \(\displaystyle y=\frac{x^2}{2}\) into the original equation \(y^3+x^3=6xy\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right)^3+x^3 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 6x\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right) \\[1.2em] \displaystyle \frac{x^6}{8}+x^3 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 3x^3 \\[1.2em] \displaystyle \frac{x^6}{8} \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2x^3. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    In the first quadrant, \(x>0\text{,}\) so divide by \(x^3\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{x^3}{8} \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2 \\[0.8em] \displaystyle x^3 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 16 \\[0.8em] \displaystyle x \amp=\amp \displaystyle \sqrt[3]{16}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Then \(\displaystyle y=\frac{x^2}{2}=\frac{(\sqrt[3]{16})^2}{2}=\frac{\sqrt[3]{256}}{2}\text{.}\) So the point in the first quadrant where the tangent is horizontal is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \left(\sqrt[3]{16},\,\frac{(\sqrt[3]{16})^2}{2}\right). \end{equation*}
    (This point is valid as long as \(y^2-2x\neq 0\text{,}\) which you can check holds here.)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Explain it to a Friend.

    1.
    Explain how to decide when implicit differentiation is needed to find \(y'\text{,}\) as opposed to using the standard differentiation rules for explicit functions.
    🔗
    In your explanation, include:
    🔗
    • One example where implicit differentiation is required.
      🔗
    • One example where implicit differentiation is not required, and standard rules apply directly.
      🔗
    Solution.
    If you have a \(y=f(x)\) equation then you can just use the normal way but if \(y\) is not given explicitly as a function of \(x\text{,}\) and it is not easy to solve for a \(y=f(x)\) then implicit differentiation should be used
    • Example of when implicit differentiation is not needed:
      \begin{equation*} y=x^2+e^x \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Example of when implicit differentiation is needed:
      \begin{equation*} y=x^2+e^y \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    A student applies implicit differentiation to a curve and finds that \(y'=5\) at the point \((x,y)=(1,2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Explain the geometric meaning of this result in terms of the graph of the curve. Your explanation should describe what this value tells you about the tangent line and the behavior of the curve near the point.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The value \(y'=5\) at \((1,2)\) means that the tangent line to the curve at that point has slope \(5\text{.}\) Geometrically, the curve is increasing at \((1,2)\text{,}\) and very close to that point the graph rises about \(5\) units vertically for every \(1\) unit horizontally.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    True/False Questions.

    3.
    If \(y=\ln(x+y)\text{,}\) then implicit differentiation is required to find \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Since \(y\) appears on both sides of the equation, it is not possible to isolate \(y\) as an explicit function of \(x\) without also having \(y\) on the right side. Therefore, implicit differentiation is required to find \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(x^2+xy+y^2=7\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=-\frac{2x+y}{x+2y}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 2x + \left(x\frac{dy}{dx} + y\right) + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Rearranging and solving for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) yields
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{2x + y}{x + 2y}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    If \(x\sin(y)=1\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=-\frac{\cos(y)}{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \sin(y) + x\cos(y)\frac{dy}{dx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Rearranging and solving for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) yields
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{\sin(y)}{x\cos(y)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(e^{x+y}=x\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{x}-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} e^{x+y}\left(1+\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 1. \end{equation*}
    Rearranging and solving for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) yields
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{e^{x+y}} - 1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    If \(x^2y+y^3=2\text{,}\) then at \((1,1)\) the slope of the tangent line is \(-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 2xy + x^2\frac{dy}{dx} + 3y^2\frac{dy}{dx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Plugging in \((x,y)=(1,1)\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 2(1)(1) + (1)^2\frac{dy}{dx} + 3(1)^2\frac{dy}{dx} = 0, \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to
    \begin{equation*} 2 + 4\frac{dy}{dx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) yields
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    If \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=0\) at a point on a curve defined implicitly, then the tangent line at that point is horizontal.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. A slope of zero indicates that the tangent line is horizontal at that point on the curve.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) is undefined at a point on a curve defined implicitly, then the tangent line at that point is vertical.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. While an undefined derivative often indicates a vertical tangent line, it can also occur at points where the curve has a cusp or corner. Therefore, an undefined derivative does not always imply a vertical tangent line.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    If \(x^2-y^2=1\text{,}\) then the curve has a vertical tangent at points where \(y=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 2x - 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Rearranging and solving for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) yields
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x}{y}. \end{equation*}
    At points where \(y=0\text{,}\) the derivative is undefined, indicating a possible vertical tangent line. We must check if such points lie on the curve. Setting \(y=0\) in the original equation gives \(x^2=1\text{,}\) so the points \((1,0)\) and \((-1,0)\) are on the curve and we can verify with a graph that they have vertical tangents.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    11.
    If \(x^2+y^2=25\text{,}\) then at \((3,4)\) the second derivative \(y''\) is \(\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) yields
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}. \end{equation*}
    Differentiating again with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\frac{y - x\frac{dy}{dx}}{y^2}. \end{equation*}
    Substituting the value of \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) and evaluating at \((3,4)\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'' = -\frac{4 - 3\left(-\frac{3}{4}\right)}{16} = -\frac{4 + \frac{9}{4}}{16} = -\frac{\frac{25}{4}}{16} = -\frac{25}{64}. \end{equation*}
