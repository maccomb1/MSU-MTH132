    Section 3.3 Linear Approximation and L’Hopital’s Rule

    Objectives

    • Understand the idea of linearization (tangent line approximation) and recognize why it can be used to approximate a function near \(x=a\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Recognize the purpose of linearization as a tool for simplifying complicated functions and interpreting the result in context.
      🔗
    • Apply the linear approximation formula \(\displaystyle L(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Recognize when L’Hôpital’s Rule applies, particularly for indeterminate forms \(\displaystyle \frac{0}{0}\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Apply L’Hôpital’s Rule in limit problems, including those that require up to two applications.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Linearization

    One of the most useful applications of derivatives is linearization, which uses tangent lines to approximate the values of a function. Earlier, we interpreted the derivative at a point as the slope of the tangent line to a curve. Linearization builds on this idea by treating the tangent line as a stand-in for the function itself. When we focus on values of \(x\) near a chosen point, the tangent line provides a simple and accurate approximation of the function.
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.1.

    Let \(f\) be differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\) The linearization of \(f\) at \(x=a\) is the linear function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle L(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a). \end{equation*}
    The graph of \(y=L(x)\) is the tangent line to \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((a,f(a))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The idea is that when \(x\) is close to \(a\text{,}\) the curve \(y=f(x)\) and its tangent line have very similar values. So \(L(x)\approx f(x)\) near \(x=a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To see this idea in action consider the GIF below which shows us zooming in on the curve and that the curve and tangent line become indistinguishable.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.3.

    Find the linearization of \(f(x)=\sqrt{x+3}\) at \(a=1\text{.}\) Use it to approximate \(\sqrt{3.98}\) and \(\sqrt{4.05}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First let’s build the linearization by computing \(f(1)\) and \(f'(1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle (x+3)^{1/2} \\[0.8em] \displaystyle f'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2}(x+3)^{-1/2} \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x+3}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f(1) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \sqrt{4}=2, \\[0.8em] \displaystyle f'(1) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2\sqrt{4}}=\frac{1}{4}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Which gives the linearization at \(a=1\) as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle L(x)=2+\frac{1}{4}(x-1). \end{equation*}
    To approximate \(\sqrt{3.98}\text{,}\) notice that \(\sqrt{3.98}=\sqrt{0.98+3}=f(0.98)\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \sqrt{3.98} \amp=\amp \displaystyle f(0.98) \\[0.6em] \amp\approx\amp \displaystyle L(0.98) \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2+\frac{1}{4}(0.98-1) \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2-\frac{1}{4}(0.02) \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2-0.005 \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1.995. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Similarly, \(\sqrt{4.05}=f(1.05)\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \sqrt{4.05} \amp=\amp \displaystyle f(1.05) \\[0.6em] \amp\approx\amp \displaystyle L(1.05) \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2+\frac{1}{4}(1.05-1) \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2+\frac{1}{4}(0.05) \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2+0.0125 \\[0.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2.0125. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Using a calculator, we find that \(\sqrt{3.98}\approx 1.99499\) and \(\sqrt{4.05}\approx 2.01246\text{,}\) so our approximations are quite close! Indeed the linearization provides a good estimate for values of \(x\) near \(a=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.4.

    Use linear approximation to estimate \(e^{0.1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(f(x)=e^x\) and linearize at \(a=0\) (since \(0.1\) is close to \(0\)). We have \(f(0)=1\) and \(f'(x)=e^x\text{,}\) so \(f'(0)=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle L(x)=f(0)+f'(0)(x-0)=1+x. \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle e^{0.1}=f(0.1)\approx L(0.1)=1+0.1=1.1. \end{equation*}
    Again we can check with a calculator: \(e^{0.1}\approx 1.10517\text{,}\) so our approximation is quite good!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Linearization gives us a powerful way to understand how functions behave near a point by replacing them with their tangent lines. This local linear behavior turns out to be especially useful when studying limits that initially appear undefined.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection L’Hôpital’s Rule

    Theorem 3.3.5. L’Hôpital’s Rule (Informal Statement).

    Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable near \(x=c\) (except possibly at \(x=c\)), and \(g'(x)\neq 0\) near \(x=c\text{.}\) If
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to c} f(x)=\lim_{x\to c} g(x)=0 \quad\text{or}\quad \displaystyle \lim_{x\to c} f(x)=\lim_{x\to c} g(x)=\pm\infty, \end{equation*}
    then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}, \end{equation*}
    provided the limit on the right exists (or is infinite).
    🔗
    🔗

    Formal Proof.

    A formal proof of L’Hôpital’s Rule requires something called the Cauchy Mean Value Theorem, along with a careful analysis of limits. This is beyond the scope of our class. However students interested in seeing a formal proof are encouraged to pursue a math major/minor and take MTH 320 (or equivalent) where this proof is typically covered.
    🔗
    🔗

    Idea of a Proof.

    We will focus on the \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) case, that is where \(f(c)=0\) and \(g(c)=0\text{.}\) The case where both approach \(\pm\infty\) is similar
    🔗
    The linearizations of \(f\) and \(g\) at \(x=c\) are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L_f(x) = 0+f'(c)(x-c) = f'(c)(x-c) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} L_g(x) = 0+g'(c)(x-c) = g'(c)(x-c) \end{equation*}
    Since again, \(f(c)=0\) and \(g(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now as we have seen earlier, differentiable functions behave like their tangent lines when we are close to the point of tangency. Therefore, as \(x\to c\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \approx \frac{L_f(x)}{L_g(x)} = \frac{f'(c)(x-c)}{g'(c)(x-c)} = \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, we can conclude that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to c} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} = \lim_{x\to c} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 3.3.6.

    We have previously called the expressions \(\frac{0}{0}\) and \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) "meaningless expressions". A more precise term for these expressions that you may encounter on the internet or other texts is indeterminate forms.
    🔗
    This is because these expressions do not have a well-defined value and must be evaluated on a case-by-case basis. We will see cases where \(\frac{0}{0}\) evaluates to 2 but other cases where it evaluates to 5 or 0 or does not exist at all.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.7.

    Compute \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\ln x}{x-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    As \(x\to 1\text{,}\) we have \(\ln x\to 0\) and \(x-1\to 0\text{,}\) so this is an indeterminate form \(\displaystyle \frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Apply L’Hôpital’s Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\ln x}{x-1} \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x)}{\frac{d}{dx}(x-1)} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\frac{1}{x}}{1} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{1}{x} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.8.

    Compute \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2}\text{.}\) (This requires two applications of L’Hôpital’s Rule.)
    🔗
    Solution.
    As \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) both \(e^x\) and \(x^2\) go to \(\infty\text{,}\) so this is an indeterminate form \(\displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) Apply L’Hôpital’s Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2} \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\frac{d}{dx}(e^x)}{\frac{d}{dx}(x^2)} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2x}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This is still \(\displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{,}\) so apply L’Hôpital’s Rule again:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2x} \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\frac{d}{dx}(e^x)}{\frac{d}{dx}(2x)} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \infty. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2}=\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    True/False Questions.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false. Justify your answers.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\) then the linearization \(L(x)\) gives an exact value of \(f(x)\) for all \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The linearization \(L(x)\) is only an approximation of \(f(x)\) near \(x=a\text{.}\) It is not exact for all values of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    The linearization of \(f(x)=\ln x\) at \(x=1\) is \(L(x)=x-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. We can verify this by computing \(f(1)=\ln 1=0\) and \(f'(x)=\frac{1}{x}\text{,}\) so \(f'(1)=1\text{.}\) Therefore, the linearization at \(x=1\) is \(L(x)=f(1)+f'(1)(x-1)=0+1(x-1)=x-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    The limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x}\) can be evaluated using L’Hôpital’s Rule.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Although previously we have used geometric arguments to evaluate this limit, we can also apply L’Hôpital’s Rule since both the numerator and denominator approach 0 as \(x\to 0\text{.}\) Applying L’Hôpital’s Rule gives
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos x}{1} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    Using linearization for \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) at \(x=9\) tells us that \(\sqrt{9.02}\approx 3+\tfrac{1}{300}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The linearization of \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) at \(x=9\) is \(L(x)=\frac{1}{6}(x-9)+3\text{.}\) Evaluating at \(x=9.02\text{,}\) we get:
    \begin{equation*} L(9.02)=\frac{1}{6}(9.02-9)+3=\frac{1}{6}\left(\frac{1}{50}\right)+3=\frac{1}{300}+3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    Using a linearization at \(x=8\) gives us \(\sqrt[3]{8.1}\approx 2.01\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The linearization of \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) at \(x=8\) is \(L(x)=\frac{1}{12}(x-8)+2\text{.}\) Evaluating at \(x=8.1\text{,}\) we get:
    \begin{equation*} L(8.1)=\frac{1}{12}(8.1-8)+2=\frac{1}{12}(0.1)+2=\frac{1}{120}+2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is not equal to \(2.01 = 2 + \frac{1}{100}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    Using a linearization at \(x=0\) gives us \(\sin(0.1)\approx 0.1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The linearization of \(f(x)=\sin x\) at \(x=0\) is \(L(x)=x\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\sin(0.1)\approx 0.1\text{.}\) Remember that for trigonometric functions, the angle must be in radians!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{e^x-1}{x}=1\) by L’Hôpital’s Rule.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Plugging in \(x=0\) gives the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Applying L’Hôpital’s Rule, we get
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{e^x-1}{x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{e^x}{1} = e^0 = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}=0\) by L’Hôpital’s Rule.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Plugging in \(x=0\) gives the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Applying L’Hôpital’s Rule, we get
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{1} = \sin(0) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}=\frac12\) using L’Hôpital’s Rule.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Plugging in \(x=0\) gives the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Applying L’Hôpital’s Rule, we get
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{2x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    When we attempt to plug in \(x=0\) again, we still get the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) so we apply L’Hôpital’s Rule again:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{2x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos x}{2} = \frac{\cos(0)}{2} = \frac12\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the original limit is \(\frac12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x^2}=1\) by L’Hôpital’s Rule.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Plugging in \(x=0\) gives the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Applying L’Hôpital’s Rule, we get
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x^2} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos x}{2x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    When we attempt to plug in \(x=0\) again, we get \(\frac{1}{0}\text{,}\) which is undefined. Therefore, the limit does not exist.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    11.
    Consider the table of values below
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline x \amp f(x) \amp f'(x) \amp g(x) \amp g'(x) \\ \hline 2 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 1 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Evaluating \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) yields \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. As \(x\to 2\text{,}\) we have \(f(2)=0\) and \(g(2)=4\text{,}\) so the limit evaluates to
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f(2)}{g(2)} = \frac{0}{4} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Notice that L’Hôpital’s Rule does not apply here since the limit is not an indeterminate form.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    12.
    Consider the graph below that shows \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = -\dfrac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Since both functions pass through the origin, we have \(f(0)=0\) and \(g(0)=0\text{,}\) so this is an indeterminate form \(\displaystyle \frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Applying L’Hôpital’s Rule, we get
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} = \frac{f'(0)}{g'(0)} = \frac{-2}{3} \end{equation*}
