    Subsection Key Derivative Rules

    Theorem 2.5.1. Derivative of \(e^x\).

    The exponential function \(e^x\) is its own derivative:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(e^x\bigr)=e^x. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 2.5.2. Derivative of \(\ln x\).

    For \(x>0\text{,}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\ln x\bigr)=\frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    Remark 2.5.3.
    We will justify this formula in the next section. For now, we use it as a rule.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection The Chain Rule

    Theorem 2.5.4. Chain Rule.

    If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable and \(y=f(g(x))\text{,}\) then
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(f(g(x))\bigr)=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.5.5.

    Intuitively, a small change in \(x\) produces a change in \(g(x)\text{,}\) and that change in \(g(x)\) produces a change in \(f(g(x))\text{.}\) The Chain Rule multiplies these â€œrates of change along the way.â€
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.5.6. Chain Rule from a Table.

    Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions with values given in the table below.
    ðŸ”—
    Using the Chain Rule, find:
    ðŸ”—
    1. \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl[f(g(x))\bigr]\) at \(x=1\)
      ðŸ”—
    2. \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl[g(f(x))\bigr]\) at \(x=2\)
      ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    (1) By the Chain Rule,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl[f(g(x))\bigr]=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x). \end{equation*}
    At \(x=1\text{,}\) the table gives \(g(1)=2\) and \(g'(1)=-1\text{.}\) We also need \(f'(2)\text{,}\) and the table gives \(f'(2)=-2\text{.}\) Therefore,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \left.\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[f(g(x))\bigr]\right|_{x=1}=f'(2)\cdot(-1)=(-2)(-1)=2. \end{equation*}
    (2) By the Chain Rule,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl[g(f(x))\bigr]=g'(f(x))\cdot f'(x). \end{equation*}
    At \(x=2\text{,}\) the table gives \(f(2)=3\) and \(f'(2)=-2\text{.}\) We also need \(g'(3)\text{,}\) and the table gives \(g'(3)=7\text{.}\) Therefore,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \left.\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[g(f(x))\bigr]\right|_{x=2}=g'(3)\cdot(-2)=7(-2)=-14. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Derivatives of Exponential Functions \(a^x\)

    Theorem 2.5.8. Derivative of \(a^x\).

    If \(a>0\) and \(a\neq 1\text{,}\) then
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(a^x\bigr)=a^x\ln(a). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Write \(a^x\) in terms of \(e^x\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle a^x=e^{x\ln(a)}. \end{equation*}
    Differentiate using the Chain Rule and the fact that \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(e^u)=e^u\cdot u'\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(a^x\bigr) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\Bigl(e^{x\ln(a)}\Bigr) \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle e^{x\ln(a)}\cdot \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(x\ln(a)\bigr) \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle e^{x\ln(a)}\cdot \ln(a) \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle a^x\ln(a). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Sample Problems

    Example 2.5.9. Straightforward Derivatives.

    Find the derivative of each function:
    ðŸ”—
    1. \(\displaystyle f(x)=x^e+e^x\)
      ðŸ”—
    2. \(\displaystyle g(x)=\sin\!\left(\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}\right)\)
      ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    (1) Since \(e\) is a constant, \(x^e\) is a power function. Using the Power Rule and \(\displaystyle (e^x)'=e^x\text{,}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(x)=e\,x^{e-1}+e^x. \end{equation*}
    (2) Let \(u(x)=\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}\text{.}\) Then \(g(x)=\sin(u(x))\text{,}\) so by the Chain Rule,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle g'(x)=\cos(u(x))\cdot u'(x). \end{equation*}
    Compute \(u'(x)\) using the Quotient Rule (or product plus chain rule):
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle u'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{(e^x)(1+e^x)-(e^x)(e^x)}{(1+e^x)^2} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{e^x}{(1+e^x)^2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle g'(x)=\cos\!\left(\frac{e^x}{1+e^x}\right)\cdot \frac{e^x}{(1+e^x)^2}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.5.10. No Tangent Line with a Given Slope.

    Show that the curve \(y=2e^x+3x+5x^3\) has no tangent line with slope \(2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    A tangent line has slope 2 exactly when \(\displaystyle y'(x)=2\text{.}\) Differentiate:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'(x)=2e^x+3+15x^2. \end{equation*}
    Now observe that \(e^x>0\) for all \(x\) and \(15x^2\ge 0\text{,}\) so
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'(x)=2e^x+3+15x^2 \gt 3. \end{equation*}
    In particular, \(y'(x)\neq 2\) for any \(x\text{.}\) Therefore the curve has no tangent line with slope 2.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection More Challenging and Conceptual Problems

    Activity 2.5.1. Deriving the Quotient Rule from Product and Chain Rules.

    Writing \(\displaystyle \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) in the form \(f(x)\cdot[g(x)]^{-1}\text{,}\) use the Product Rule and the Chain Rule to prove the Quotient Rule.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Let \(h(x)=\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}=f(x)\cdot (g(x))^{-1}\text{,}\) where \(g(x)\neq 0\text{.}\) By the Product Rule,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle h'(x)=f'(x)\,(g(x))^{-1}+f(x)\,\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[(g(x))^{-1}\bigr]. \end{equation*}
    Now apply the Chain Rule to \((g(x))^{-1}\text{.}\) If \(u(x)=g(x)\) and \(\phi(u)=u^{-1}\text{,}\) then \(\phi'(u)=-u^{-2}\text{,}\) so
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\bigl[(g(x))^{-1}\bigr]=-(g(x))^{-2}\,g'(x). \end{equation*}
    Substitute and simplify:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle h'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{f'(x)}{g(x)}-f(x)\frac{g'(x)}{(g(x))^2} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{f'(x)g(x)-f(x)g'(x)}{(g(x))^2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This is the Quotient Rule.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.5.11. Compositions Involving Exponentials.

    Suppose \(f\) is differentiable on \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and let \(F(x)=f(e^x)\) and \(G(x)=e^{f(x)}\text{.}\) Compute \(F'(x)\) and \(G'(x)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    For \(F(x)=f(e^x)\text{,}\) apply the Chain Rule with inner function \(e^x\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle F'(x)=f'(e^x)\cdot e^x. \end{equation*}
    For \(G(x)=e^{f(x)}\text{,}\) use the Chain Rule with inner function \(f(x)\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle G'(x)=e^{f(x)}\cdot f'(x). \end{equation*}
