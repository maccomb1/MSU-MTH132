    Section 2.5 Exponential and Logarithmic Derivatives and the Chain Rule

    Objectives

    • Apply the basic derivative formulas for \(e^x\text{,}\) \(\ln(x)\text{,}\) and \(a^x\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Understand and use the Chain Rule to differentiate composite functions algebraically, graphically, and through values provided in tables.
      🔗
    • Compute derivatives of functions that combine exponentials, logarithms, powers, and trigonometric expressions using the Product Rule, Quotient Rule, and Chain Rule.
      🔗
    🔗
    Exponential and logarithmic functions appear throughout calculus. Their derivatives play a central role in growth models, rates of change, and the behavior of composite functions. This section develops the essential derivative formulas for these functions and introduces the Chain Rule, which allows us to differentiate compositions of functions.
    🔗
    If you feel a bit out of practice with exponential rules, logarithm rules, or composition rules from algebra, it may be helpful to revisit those ideas before diving into the calculus. A strong foundation makes the new material much more approachable.

    Subsection Key Derivative Formulas

    We begin by collecting the fundamental derivative rules for exponential and logarithmic functions. Some are stated without proof here and will be justified later in the text.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.5.1. Derivatives of the Natural Exponential and Natural Logarithm.

    For all \(x\) in the domains of the functions below:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (e^x)' = e^x, \qquad (\ln x)' = \frac{1}{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    We will use these two derivative formulas throughout the course, but we will not prove both of them at this moment.
    🔗
    The fact that \((e^x)' = e^x\) is a classical result that can be established in several different ways: by studying the compound interest definition of \(e\text{,}\) by using limits, or by analyzing the unique solution to a differential equation. Curious students can find many proofs available online.
    🔗
    The proof of \((\ln x)' = 1/x\) will appear later, once we have developed the tools needed to understand derivatives of inverse functions. See Section 2.7 for a full derivation.
    🔗
    For now, we take both results as known facts and use them to develop more general differentiation techniques.
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we know the derivatives of \(e^x\) and \(\ln(x)\text{,}\) we can combine them with the Product Rule, Quotient Rule, and earlier power–function rules to differentiate more complicated expressions.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.2.

    Compute the derivatives of the following functions.
    🔗
    (a)
    \(f(x)=x^e + e^x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    For \(f(x)\text{,}\) use the power rule and the fact that \((e^x)'=e^x\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = e x^{e-1} + e^x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    \(g(x)=\sqrt{x}\,e^x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Rewrite \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\) and apply the Product Rule.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(x^{1/2}e^x\right) &=& (x^{1/2})'e^x + x^{1/2}(e^x)' \\[1.0em] &=& \left(\tfrac12 x^{-1/2}\right)e^x + x^{1/2}e^x \\[1.0em] &=& e^x\!\left(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} + \sqrt{x}\right). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    \(h(x)=\dfrac{\ln(x)}{\sin(x)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Apply the Quotient Rule with \(u(x)=\ln(x)\) and \(v(x)=\sin(x)\text{.}\) Then \(u'(x)=1/x\) and \(v'(x)=\cos(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} h'(x) &=& \dfrac{u'(x)v(x) - u(x)v'(x)}{[v(x)]^2} \\[1.1em] &=& \dfrac{\frac{1}{x}\sin(x) - \ln(x)\cos(x)}{\sin^2(x)} \\[1.1em] \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.3.

    Show that the curve \(y = 2e^x + 3x + 5x^3\) has no tangent line with slope 2.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Compute the derivative:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y' = 2e^x + 3 + 15x^2. \end{equation*}
    To find a point where the slope is 2, we solve
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2e^x + 3 + 15x^2 = 2. \end{equation*}
    Rearranging gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2e^x + 15x^2 + 1 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Every term on the left side is positive for all real \(x\text{.}\) The equation has no solution. Thus the slope never equals 2.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Statement and Applications of The Chain Rule

    Composite functions arise everywhere. For example, if \(u=g(x)\) measures a temperature that changes with time, and \(f(u)\) converts that temperature to pressure, then \(f(g(x))\) describes how pressure changes with time. The Chain Rule captures how variations in the inner function affect the slope of the composite function.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.5.4. The Chain Rule.

    If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions, then their composition \(f(g(x))\) is differentiable and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f(g(x)))' = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x). \end{equation*}
    In Leibniz notation, if we let \(u=g(x)\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{df}{dx} = \frac{df}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    A full proof of the Chain Rule requires careful algebra using the limit definition of the derivative and the structure of composite functions. We present the result here without a complete derivation. Students who would like to explore the proof in detail may find the following video especially helpful:
    🔗
    A visual and intuitive explanation of the Chain Rule
    🔗
    The central idea behind the proof is that small changes in the input propagate through the inner function \(g(x)\) and then through the outer function \(f\text{.}\) The Chain Rule formalizes this by multiplying the rate of change of \(f\) with respect to \(g\) by the rate of change of \(g\) with respect to \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.5.5.

    Informally, the Chain Rule says that the rate of change of a composite function is the rate of change of the outer function, evaluated at the inner function, multiplied by how fast the inner function itself is changing.
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s see the chain rule applied with a few examples.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.6.

    Compute the derivative of \(f(x)=\sin\!\left(\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}\right)\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Apply the Chain Rule and Quotient rule. Let \(u=\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}\text{,}\) then \(f(u)=\sin(u)\) and \(f'(u)=\cos(u)\) so:
    \begin{equation*} u' = \dfrac{e^x(1+e^x) - e^x(e^x)}{(1+e^x)^2} = \dfrac{e^x}{(1+e^x)^2} \end{equation*}
    Therefore \(f'(x) = \cos\!\left(\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}\right)\cdot \dfrac{e^x}{(1+e^x)^2}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.7.

    Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions with the values shown below.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline x & f(x) & f'(x) & g(x) & g'(x) \\ \hline 1 & 5 & 4 & 2 & -1 \\ \hline 2 & 3 & -2 & 4 & 3 \\ \hline 3 & -1 & 6 & 1 & 7 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Compute the following derivatives.
    🔗
    (a)
    Compute \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))]\) at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the Chain Rule,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}(f(g(x))) = f'(g(x))\, g'(x). \end{equation*}
    At \(x=1\text{,}\) we have \(g(1)=2\) and \(g'(1)=-1\text{,}\) and the table gives \(f'(2)=-2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}(f(g(x)))\big|_{x=1} = (-2)(-1) = 2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Compute \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[g(f(x))]\) at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Again apply the Chain Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}(g(f(x))) = g'(f(x))\, f'(x). \end{equation*}
    At \(x=2\text{,}\) the table shows \(f(2)=3\text{,}\) so \(g'(3)=7\text{,}\) and \(f'(2)=-2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}(g(f(x)))\big|_{x=2} = 7\cdot(-2) = -14. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Lastly, we can use the Chain Rule to extend our exponential derivative formulas. This allows us to differentiate any exponential function, not just those with base \(e\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 2.5.8. Derivative of the General Exponential Functions.

    For any positive constant \(a \ne 1\text{,}\) the derivative of \(a^x\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (a^x)' = a^x \ln(a). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    To compute the derivative of \(a^x\) for a positive constant \(a\ne 1\text{,}\) we rewrite \(a^x\) in terms of the natural exponential function. Recall that by using the power rule for logarithms and the inverse identity we can write:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a^x = e^{x \ln(a)}. \end{equation*}
    We now differentiate \(e^{x\ln(a)}\) using the Chain Rule. Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} u(x) = x\ln(a). \end{equation*}
    Then \(a^x = e^{u(x)}\text{,}\) and by the Chain Rule,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(e^{u(x)}\right) = e^{u(x)} \cdot u'(x). \end{equation*}
    Since \(u(x)=x\ln(a)\text{,}\) we have \(u'(x)=\ln(a)\text{.}\) Substituting back:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(a^x\right) = e^{x\ln(a)} \cdot \ln(a). \end{equation*}
    Using \(e^{x\ln(a)} = a^x\) once more, we obtain the final formula:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (a^x)' = a^x \ln(a). \end{equation*}
    This completes the proof.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.9.

    Compute the derivative of \(f(x)=2^x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Apply the exponential derivative formula \((a^x)' = a^x \ln(a)\) with \(a=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(2^x\right) = 2^x \ln(2). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    True/False Questions.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false. Justify your answer.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(f(x)=e^{g(x)}\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=e^{g(x)}g'(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This is the Chain Rule with outer function \(e^u\) and inner function \(u=g(x)\text{.}\) Since \(\dfrac{d}{du}(e^u)=e^u\text{,}\) we get \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^{g(x)}\right)=e^{g(x)}g'(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If \(f(x)=\ln(g(x))\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{g(x)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. By the Chain Rule, \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\ln(g(x))\right)=\dfrac{1}{g(x)}\cdot g'(x)\text{.}\) The factor \(g'(x)\) is missing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(f(x)=a^x\) for a constant \(a>1\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)>0\) for all real \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Using \((a^x)'=a^x\ln(a)\text{,}\) we have \(a^x>0\) for all real \(x\) and \(\ln(a)>0\) when \(a>1\text{.}\) Therefore \(f'(x)=a^x\ln(a)>0\) for all \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(g'(2)=0\text{,}\) then \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))]\big|_{x=2}=0\text{,}\) regardless of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True (assuming the Chain Rule applies at \(x=2\)). By the Chain Rule, \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left[f(g(x))\right]\big|_{x=2}=f'(g(2))\,g'(2)\text{.}\) Since \(g'(2)=0\text{,}\) the product is \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    The derivative of \(\sin(e^x)\) and the derivative of \(e^{\sin(x)}\) are the same.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Using the Chain Rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\sin(e^x)\right)=\cos(e^x)\cdot e^x\text{,}\) while \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^{\sin(x)}\right)=e^{\sin(x)}\cdot\cos(x)\text{.}\) These expressions are not equal in general.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}e^x\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)\) exists for all real \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}e^x\) is only defined for \(x\ge 0\text{,}\) so the derivative cannot exist for negative \(x\text{.}\) (On \((0,\infty)\) it is differentiable; at \(x=0\) one must check separately.)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    If \(f'(x)>0\) and \(g'(x)<0\) on an interval, then \(f(g(x))\) is decreasing on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True (where the composition is defined). By the Chain Rule, \((f(g(x)))'=f'(g(x))\,g'(x)\text{.}\) Since \(f'(g(x))>0\) and \(g'(x)<0\text{,}\) their product is negative, so \(f(g(x))\) is decreasing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    If \(f(g(x))\) has a horizontal tangent at \(x=3\text{,}\) then either \(f'(g(3))=0\) or \(g'(3)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True (assuming differentiability so the Chain Rule applies at \(x=3\)). A horizontal tangent means \((f(g(x)))'\big|_{x=3}=0\text{.}\) By the Chain Rule, \((f(g(x)))'\big|_{x=3}=f'(g(3))\,g'(3)\text{.}\) A product is zero only if at least one factor is zero, so \(f'(g(3))=0\) or \(g'(3)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(f(x)=\ln(x^2+1)\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Let \(u=x^2+1\text{.}\) Then \(f(x)=\ln(u)\text{.}\) By the Chain Rule, \(f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{u}\cdot u'=\dfrac{1}{x^2+1}\cdot 2x=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    Consider the graphs of \(f\) and \(g\) below. Since \(f(x)\) is not differentiable at \(x=1\) we know that \(f(g(x))\) is not is not differentiable at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The fact that \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=1\) does not automatically mean that the composite \(f(g(x))\) is not differentiable at \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In fact, we can compute the derivative using the Chain Rule. The slope of \(g\) is constant:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g'(x)=\frac{3-1.5}{3-0}=\frac{1.5}{3}=\frac12. \end{equation*}
    For \(x>1\text{,}\) the graph of \(f\) is the line segment from \((1,1.5)\) to \((3,1)\text{,}\) so its slope is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=\frac{1-1.5}{3-1}=\frac{-0.5}{2}=-\frac14 \end{equation*}
    on that segment, including at \(x=2\text{.}\) Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f(g(x)))'\big|_{x=1}=f'(g(1))\,g'(1)=f'(2)\cdot\frac12=\left(-\frac14\right)\left(\frac12\right)=-\frac18. \end{equation*}
    So \(f(g(x))\) is differentiable at \(x=1\text{.}\)
