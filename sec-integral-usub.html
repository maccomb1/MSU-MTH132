    Section 4.6 Substitution Rule for Integration

    Objectives

    • Recognize when an integrand has the form \(f(g(x))g'(x)\) and apply substitution.
      🔗
    • Use substitution to evaluate indefinite integrals and then check results by differentiation.
      🔗
    • Use substitution with updated limits to evaluate definite integrals.
      🔗
    • Use symmetry (odd functions) to evaluate certain definite integrals quickly.
      🔗
    • Model a real situation with an integral and interpret a substitution physically.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Reversing the Chain Rule

    By the Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, one efficient way to evaluate a definite integral \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) is to find an antiderivative \(F\) and compute \(F(b)-F(a)\text{.}\) In this section we develop a powerful method for finding antiderivatives by reversing the Chain Rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\big(F(g(x))\big)=F'(g(x))g'(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For example, consider the indefinite integral \(\displaystyle \int x\cos(x^2)\,dx\text{.}\) The inside function \(g(x)=x^2\) appears inside the cosine, and its derivative is \(g'(x)=2x\text{.}\) Since the integrand contains a factor \(x\text{,}\) we rewrite it to match the Chain Rule pattern:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x\cos(x^2) \ =\ \tfrac12\cos(x^2)\cdot (2x) \ =\ \tfrac12\cos(x^2)\cdot (x^2)'. \end{equation*}
    Now set \(u=g(x)=x^2\text{.}\) Then \(du=g'(x)\,dx=2x\,dx\text{,}\) so \(\tfrac12\,du=x\,dx\text{,}\) and the integral becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int x\cos(x^2)\,dx \ =\ \tfrac12\int \cos(u)\,du \ =\ \tfrac12\sin(u)+C \ =\ \tfrac12\sin(x^2)+C. \end{equation*}
    A quick check comes from the Chain Rule: if \(F(x)=\tfrac12\sin(x^2)\text{,}\) then \(F'(x)=\tfrac12\cos(x^2)\cdot 2x=x\cos(x^2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Substitution Method

    Substitution is a structured way to reverse the Chain Rule. The goal is to rewrite the integrand so it looks like \(f(g(x))g'(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Start with an integral \(\int h(x)\,dx\text{.}\) Look for an inside function \(g(x)\) so that \(g'(x)\) appears as a factor (possibly after multiplying and dividing by a constant):
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} h(x)\ =\ f(g(x))\cdot g'(x). \end{equation*}
      After factoring out \(g'(x)\text{,}\) rewrite the remaining part as a function of \(u=g(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Let \(u=g(x)\text{,}\) so \(du=g'(x)\,dx\text{.}\) Then rewrite the integral in \(u\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \int h(x)\,dx \ =\ \int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx \ =\ \int f(u)\,du. \end{equation*}
      Compute \(\int f(u)\,du\) using basic antiderivatives and rules.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Substitute back \(u=g(x)\) to express the final answer in terms of \(x\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \int h(x)\,dx \ =\ F(u)+C \ =\ F(g(x))+C. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Examples

    Example 4.6.1.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int (3x+4)\sqrt{3x+4}\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(u=3x+4\text{.}\) Then \(du=3\,dx\text{,}\) so \(dx=\tfrac13\,du\text{.}\) Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int (3x+4)\sqrt{3x+4}\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int u\sqrt{u}\cdot \tfrac13\,du \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle \tfrac13\int u^{3/2}\,du \ =\ \tfrac13\cdot \tfrac{2}{5}u^{5/2}+C \\[0.8em] &=& \displaystyle \tfrac{2}{15}(3x+4)^{5/2}+C. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.6.2.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int x\sqrt{3x+4}\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(u=3x+4\text{,}\) so \(du=3\,dx\) and \(dx=\tfrac13\,du\text{.}\) We also solve for \(x\text{:}\) \(u=3x+4\) gives \(x=\tfrac13u-\tfrac43\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int x\sqrt{3x+4}\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int \left(\tfrac13u-\tfrac43\right)\sqrt{u}\cdot \tfrac13\,du \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \int \left(\tfrac19u^{3/2}-\tfrac49u^{1/2}\right)\,du \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \tfrac19\cdot \tfrac{2}{5}u^{5/2}-\tfrac49\cdot \tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}+C \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \tfrac{2}{45}(3x+4)^{5/2}-\tfrac{8}{27}(3x+4)^{3/2}+C. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.6.3.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \frac{\sec^2(\sqrt{x})}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(u=\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\text{.}\) Then \(du=\tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}\,dx=\tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\,dx\text{.}\) Rewrite to produce \(du\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int \frac{\sec^2(\sqrt{x})}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int 2\sec^2(\sqrt{x})\cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\,dx \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \int 2\sec^2(u)\,du \ =\ 2\tan(u)+C \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle 2\tan(\sqrt{x})+C. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.6.4.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \frac{\sin(x)}{(1+\cos(x))^2}\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(u=1+\cos(x)\text{.}\) Then \(du=-\sin(x)\,dx\text{,}\) so \(\sin(x)\,dx=-du\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int \frac{\sin(x)}{(1+\cos(x))^2}\,dx &=& \displaystyle -\int \frac{1}{u^2}\,du \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle -\left(\frac{u^{-1}}{-1}\right)+C \ =\ \frac{1}{u}+C \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \frac{1}{1+\cos(x)}+C. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.6.5.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int \sec^2(x)\tan(x)\,dx\) in two ways, and explain why the answers agree.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Method 1: Let \(u=\tan(x)\text{.}\) Then \(du=\sec^2(x)\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int \sec^2(x)\tan(x)\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int u\,du \ =\ \tfrac12 u^2 + C \ =\ \tfrac12\tan^2(x)+C. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Method 2: Write the integrand as \(\sec(x)\cdot (\tan(x)\sec(x))\) and let \(z=\sec(x)\text{.}\) Then \(dz=\tan(x)\sec(x)\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int \sec^2(x)\tan(x)\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int z\,dz \ =\ \tfrac12 z^2 + C \ =\ \tfrac12\sec^2(x)+C. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    These differ by a constant because \(\sec^2(x)=\tan^2(x)+1\text{.}\) So \(\tfrac12\sec^2(x)=\tfrac12\tan^2(x)+\tfrac12\text{,}\) meaning the two antiderivative families are the same.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Substitution for Definite Integrals

    Substitution can be used directly with definite integrals by changing the limits. If \(u=g(x)\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx \ =\ \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\,du. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.6.6.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_2^3 x(1+x^2)^5\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(u=1+x^2\text{,}\) so \(du=2x\,dx\) and \(x\,dx=\tfrac12\,du\text{.}\) Update the limits: when \(x=2\text{,}\) \(u=1+2^2=5\text{;}\) when \(x=3\text{,}\) \(u=1+3^2=10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int_2^3 x(1+x^2)^5\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int_2^3 \tfrac12 (1+x^2)^5\cdot 2x\,dx \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \int_{5}^{10} \tfrac12 u^5\,du \ =\ \left.\tfrac{1}{12}u^6\right|_{u=5}^{u=10} \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \tfrac{1}{12}\left(10^6-5^6\right). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Integral Symmetry for Odd Functions

    Theorem 4.6.7. Integral Symmetry Theorem.

    If \(f\) is odd, meaning \(f(-x)=-f(x)\text{,}\) then for any \(a>0\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-a}^{a} f(x)\,dx \ =\ 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    By the splitting rule,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-a}^{a} f(x)\,dx \ =\ \int_{-a}^{0} f(x)\,dx + \int_{0}^{a} f(x)\,dx. \end{equation*}
    In the first integral, substitute \(u=-x\text{,}\) so \(du=-dx\text{.}\) When \(x=-a\text{,}\) \(u=a\text{;}\) when \(x=0\text{,}\) \(u=0\text{.}\) Then:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \int_{-a}^{0} f(x)\,dx &=& \displaystyle \int_{a}^{0} f(-u)\,(-du) \ =\ \int_{a}^{0} (-f(u))\,(-du) \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle \int_{a}^{0} f(u)\,du \ =\ -\int_{0}^{a} f(u)\,du. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore \(\int_{-a}^{0} f(x)\,dx + \int_{0}^{a} f(x)\,dx=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.6.8.

    Evaluate \(\displaystyle \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} x\cos(x)\,dx\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The integrand \(f(x)=x\cos(x)\) is odd because \(f(-x)=(-x)\cos(-x)=(-x)\cos(x)=-f(x)\text{.}\) By the symmetry theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} x\cos(x)\,dx \ =\ 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Application: Heart Flow Rate

    In a medical test, a known amount of dye is injected into a vein flowing toward the heart. Let \(c(t)\) be the measured dye concentration in arterial blood (mg per liter) at time \(t\) minutes. If the (unknown) blood flow rate is constant at \(r\) liters per minute, then the total blood volume that has passed is \(\ell=rt\text{.}\) Let \(C(\ell)\) be the dye concentration after \(\ell\) liters have passed, so \(C(rt)=c(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The total amount of dye \(D\) (in mg) can be computed by integrating concentration with respect to volume:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} D \ =\ \int_0^{\infty} C(\ell)\,d\ell. \end{equation*}
    Substitute \(\ell=rt\text{,}\) so \(d\ell=r\,dt\) and \(C(\ell)=C(rt)=c(t)\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle D &=& \displaystyle \int_0^{\infty} C(\ell)\,d\ell \ =\ \int_0^{\infty} C(rt)\,r\,dt \\[0.9em] &=& \displaystyle r\int_0^{\infty} c(t)\,dt. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Solving for the flow rate gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r \ =\ \frac{D}{\displaystyle \int_0^{\infty} c(t)\,dt}. \end{equation*}
    In practice, once the dye has passed through the system, \(c(t)\) becomes (essentially) zero, so the improper integral can be replaced by a finite upper limit chosen from the data. The remaining integral can be approximated numerically (for example, by a Riemann sum or a spreadsheet).
