    Section 3.7 Curve Sketching Part 2

    Objectives

    • Explain why qualitative graphing is useful even when graphing technology is available.
      🔗
    • Use end behavior ideas (limits and leading-term comparisons) to understand how a graph behaves for large \(|x|\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Use derivative sign charts to classify behavior near critical points and vertical asymptotes.
      🔗
    • Recognize and use symmetry (even, odd, periodic, and other transformations) to streamline a sketch.
      🔗
    🔗
    Computers can produce a graph instantly by plotting many points, so why learn to sketch by hand? One reason is that calculus helps us focus on the important parts of the graph: critical points, asymptotes, and regions where behavior changes. Another reason is error checking. A quick sketch, combined with a few well-chosen test values, can reveal when a result is unreasonable long before a mistake spreads into a larger calculation.
    🔗

    Subsection A Rational Function Example

    Example 3.7.1.

    Use the curve sketching method to analyze \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^3-6x^2+11x-6}{2x^2-8x}\text{.}\) Identify the domain, intercepts, critical points, vertical asymptotes, an inflection point, and end behavior.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First factor the function:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{x^3-6x^2+11x-6}{2x^2-8x} =\frac{(x-1)(x-2)(x-3)}{2x(x-4)}. \end{equation*}
    The domain excludes values that make the denominator zero, so \(x\neq 0\) and \(x\neq 4\text{.}\) These produce vertical asymptotes at \(x=0\) and \(x=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Intercepts come from \(f(x)=0\text{,}\) which occurs at zeros of the numerator that are in the domain: \(x=1,2,3\text{.}\) There is no \(y\)-intercept because \(x=0\) is not in the domain.
    🔗
    The derivatives (after simplification) are:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=\frac{x^4-8x^3+13x^2+12x-24}{2x^2(x-4)^2}, \qquad f''(x)=-\frac{3(x^3-6x^2+24x-32)}{x^3(x-4)^3}. \end{equation*}
    Solving \(f'(x)=0\) requires locating roots of the numerator. A numerical approach (graphing or a root finder) gives approximate solutions:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a_1\approx -1.26,\quad a_2\approx 1.39,\quad a_3\approx 2.61,\quad a_4\approx 5.26. \end{equation*}
    Along with the vertical asymptotes at \(x=0\) and \(x=4\text{,}\) these break the real line into intervals where the sign of \(f'(x)\) is constant. A sign chart then determines where the function increases or decreases, which in turn classifies the critical points as local maxima or minima.
    🔗
    For concavity, solve \(f''(x)=0\) by setting the numerator to zero:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x^3-6x^2+24x-32=0. \end{equation*}
    Factoring shows \(x^3-6x^2+24x-32=(x-2)(x^2-4x+16)\text{,}\) and the quadratic has no real roots. Thus \(x=2\) is the only inflection point in the domain.
    🔗
    Finally, to understand end behavior, compare leading terms:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{x^3-6x^2+11x-6}{2x^2-8x}\approx \frac{x^3}{2x^2}=\tfrac12 x \quad \text{for large } |x|. \end{equation*}
    This tells us that the graph grows roughly like a line with slope \(\tfrac12\) as \(|x|\) becomes large. In practice, this is enough to guide a reasonable sketch when combined with critical points and asymptotes.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A Trigonometric Example

    Example 3.7.2.

    Apply the curve sketching method to \(s(x)=x-2\sin(x)\text{.}\) Identify critical points, concavity, intercepts, and symmetry.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The derivatives are \(s'(x)=1-2\cos(x)\) and \(s''(x)=2\sin(x)\text{.}\) The domain is all real numbers.
    🔗
    Critical points occur where \(s'(x)=0\text{,}\) so \(\cos(x)=\tfrac12\text{.}\) This happens at \(x=\tfrac{\pi}{3}\) and \(x=\tfrac{5\pi}{3}\text{,}\) repeating every \(2\pi\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x=\tfrac{\pi}{3}+2\pi n \quad\text{and}\quad x=\tfrac{5\pi}{3}+2\pi n \quad\text{for integers } n. \end{equation*}
    Inflection points occur where \(s''(x)=0\text{,}\) so \(\sin(x)=0\text{,}\) which gives \(x=n\pi\) for any integer \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The point \((0,s(0))=(0,0)\) is both an \(x\)- and \(y\)-intercept.
    🔗
    This function is odd: \(s(-x)=-x-2\sin(-x)=-x+2\sin(x)=-(x-2\sin(x))=-s(x)\text{.}\) Therefore its graph has 180-degree rotational symmetry about the origin.
    🔗
    Remark 3.7.3.
    Since \(s(x)=x-2\sin(x)\) behaves like \(x\) for large \(|x|\text{,}\) a reasonable long-range guide is the line \(y=x\text{.}\) However, the term \(-2\sin(x)\) keeps oscillating, so the graph continues to wiggle above and below this line.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Curve Sketching Checklist

    Strategy 3.7.4. Method for Graphing (Detailed).

    1. Compute \(f'(x)\) and \(f''(x)\text{.}\) Determine the domain of \(f\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find critical points by solving \(f'(x)=0\) and locating where \(f'(x)\) is undefined.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Make a sign table for \(f'(x)\) to determine intervals of increase and decrease and to classify each critical point. Critical points can correspond to local maxima, local minima, stationary points, cusps, corners, or vertical asymptotes.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Find possible inflection points by solving \(f''(x)=0\) and locating where \(f''(x)\) is undefined. Confirm an inflection point by a sign change in \(f''(x)\) (and typically requiring \(f'(x)\) to exist at the point).
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Find intercepts: solve \(f(x)=0\) for \(x\)-intercepts and compute \(f(0)\) if \(x=0\) is in the domain.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Describe end behavior as \(x\to\pm\infty\text{.}\) When possible, use limit language. For polynomials and many rational functions, comparing leading terms gives a useful approximation of the long-range trend.
      🔗
      🔗
    7. Check symmetry when it helps: even symmetry if \(f(-x)=f(x)\text{,}\) odd symmetry if \(f(-x)=-f(x)\text{,}\) and periodicity if \(f(x+c)=f(x)\) for some constant \(c\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Combine the information into a clear sketch. Mark intercepts, extrema, inflection points, and asymptotes.
      🔗
      🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Conceptual Questions.

    1.
    True or False: A calculator graph can hide important features if the viewing window is not chosen carefully.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. A window can miss a vertical asymptote, a local extremum, or a change in concavity.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    True or False: If \(f(x)\approx cx^p\) for large \(|x|\text{,}\) then this approximation can help predict the end behavior of the graph.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The dominant term controls the long-range trend.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    True or False: If \(f\) is odd, then its graph has 180-degree rotational symmetry about the origin.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This follows from \(f(-x)=-f(x)\text{.}\)
