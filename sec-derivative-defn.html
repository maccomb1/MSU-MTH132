    Section 2.2 Definition of the Derivative

    Objectives

    • Compute the derivative \(f'(a)\) using the limit definition.
      🔗
    • Interpret the derivative as an instantaneous rate of change and as the slope of a tangent line.
      🔗
    • Rewrite the derivative definition using either \(h\to 0\) or \(x\to a\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Define the derivative function \(f'(x)\) and interpret it as a new function.
      🔗
    • Recognize common situations where a derivative fails to exist (vertical tangents, corners, discontinuities).
      🔗
    • Identify where a function is differentiable on an interval by locating vertical tangents, corners/cusps, and discontinuities.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Definition of the Derivative

    In the last section, we saw that instantaneous velocity can be obtained as a limit of average velocities over shorter and shorter time increments. Likewise, the slope of a tangent line can be obtained as a limit of secant slopes as the secant point moves closer and closer to the point of tangency.
    🔗
    Both ideas measure an instantaneous rate of change. For a general function \(f(x)\text{,}\) we define its instantaneous rate of change at \(x=a\) using the following limit.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.1. Derivative at a Point.

    The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\text{,}\) denoted \(f'(a)\text{,}\) is defined by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    provided the limit exists.
    🔗
    🔗
    Here \(f(a+h)-f(a)\) is the change in the output, and \(h\) is the change in the input. The quotient \(\displaystyle\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) is the average rate of change over the interval from \(a\) to \(a+h\text{,}\) and the derivative is the limit as the increment \(h\) shrinks to 0.
    🔗
    Another common form comes from substituting \(x=a+h\) (so that \(h=x-a\)):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}. \end{equation*}
    Geometrically, if \(f'(a)\) exists, then it is the slope of the tangent line to \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((a,f(a))\text{.}\) The tangent line has equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a). \end{equation*}

    Remark 2.2.2.

    If the limit defining \(f'(a)\) exists, we say \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\) If the limit does not exist, then \(f'(a)\) is undefined and \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Computing Derivatives from the Definition

    A derivative is always a limit of a difference quotient, so direct substitution usually produces the meaningless expression \(\tfrac{0}{0}\text{.}\) The goal is to simplify the difference quotient until the limit can be evaluated.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.3. A Rational Example.

    Find \(f'(2)\) for \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x+1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First let’s start by writing out the difference quotient:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \dfrac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h} &=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\frac{1}{(2+h)+1}-\frac{1}{2+1}}{h} \\ &=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\frac{1}{3+h}-\frac{1}{3}}{h} \\ &=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\frac{3-(3+h)}{3(3+h)}}{h} \\ &=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\frac{-h}{3(3+h)}}{h} \\ &=\amp \displaystyle \frac{-1}{3(3+h)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we can evaluate the limit as \(h\to 0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-1}{3(3+h)}=\frac{-1}{9}. \end{equation*}
    So the slope of the tangent line to \(y=\frac{1}{x+1}\) at \(x=2\) is \(-1/9\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.4. A Radical Example.

    Find \(f'(2)\) for \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We multiply by the conjugate to eliminate the radical in the numerator.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcll} \displaystyle f'(2) \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{2+h}-\sqrt{2}}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{2+h}-\sqrt{2}}{h}\cdot \frac{\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2}} \\[1.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(2+h)-2}{h(\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2})} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2}} \ =\ \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.5. A Cube Root Example.

    Find \(f'(a)\) for \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Here we use the difference of cubes identity \((A-B)(A^2+AB+B^2)=A^3-B^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f'(a) = \amp \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt[3]{a+h}-\sqrt[3]{a}}{h}\\ = \amp \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{\sqrt[3]{a+h}-\sqrt[3]{a}}{h} \cdot \frac{ \left(\sqrt[3]{a+h}\right)^2 +\sqrt[3]{a+h}\sqrt[3]{a} +\left(\sqrt[3]{a}\right)^2 }{ \left(\sqrt[3]{a+h}\right)^2 +\sqrt[3]{a+h}\sqrt[3]{a} +\left(\sqrt[3]{a}\right)^2 }\\ = \amp \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{(a+h)-a}{ h\left( \left(\sqrt[3]{a+h}\right)^2 +\sqrt[3]{a+h}\sqrt[3]{a} +\left(\sqrt[3]{a}\right)^2 \right) }\\ = \amp \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1}{ \left(\sqrt[3]{a+h}\right)^2 +\sqrt[3]{a+h}\sqrt[3]{a} +\left(\sqrt[3]{a}\right)^2 }\\ = \amp \frac{1}{3\left(\sqrt[3]{a}\right)^2} \end{align*}
    This example is a good preview of why we will soon develop derivative rules: computing derivatives from the limit definition can become algebraically heavy.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Derivative Function

    The derivative at a point \(f'(a)\) is a single number. But we can compute the derivative for many input values, producing a new function.
    🔗
    For example, consider \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) from the previous example. At each value of \(x\text{,}\) the function has a slope. Plotting these slopes as points creates a new graph, which we call the graph of the derivative function.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.7. Derivative Function.

    The derivative function of \(f\text{,}\) denoted \(f'\text{,}\) is defined by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) where this limit exists.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.8. Computing a Derivative Function.

    Let \(f(x)=x(x-2)\text{.}\) Find \(f'(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step by Step:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(x+h)(x+h-2)-x(x-2)}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{x^2+2xh+h^2-2x-2h-x^2+2x}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{2xh-2h+h^2}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\left(2x-2+h\right) \ =\ 2x-2. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Graphically this is what we have accomplished
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection When the Derivative Fails to Exist

    Even if a function is defined at a point, the derivative may fail to exist there. Graphically, this happens when the curve does not have a single well-defined tangent line.
    🔗
    Common ways differentiability can fail include:
    🔗
    • Vertical Tangent: slopes become infinite (unbounded).
      🔗
    • Corner: the left-hand and right-hand slopes are different.
      🔗
    • Discontinuity: if a function is not continuous at a point, then it cannot be differentiable there.
      🔗
    We first consider what happens when a function has a vertical tangent. Recall that the derivative at a point represents the slope of the tangent line. Because vertical lines have undefined slope, the derivative cannot exist at any point where the tangent line is vertical.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.10. A Vertical Tangent.

    Consider the function \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\text{,}\) which we analyzed previously in Example 2.2.5. Show that \(f\) has a vertical tangent at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    From Example 2.2.5, the derivative of \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a)=\frac{1}{3(\sqrt[3]{a})^2}. \end{equation*}
    Trying to substitute in \(a=0\text{,}\) the denominator \((\sqrt[3]{a})^2\) yields \(0\text{,}\) causing the derivative \(f'(x)\) to grow without bound like we saw in our section on infinite limits . In other words, the slopes of the tangent lines become arbitrarily large in magnitude.
    🔗
    Graphically, this behavior appears as the tangent lines becoming steeper and steeper near \(x=0\text{,}\) eventually approaching a vertical line. Since vertical lines have undefined slope, the derivative does not exist at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\) is not differentiable at \(x=0\) due to the presence of a vertical tangent line.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Next we consider two other common ways differentiability can fail: corners and discontinuities. In these situations, it is helpful to introduce right-hand and left-hand derivatives, defined using one-sided limits in the same spirit as one-sided limits for functions. These allow us to determine whether the derivative exists by comparing the slopes approaching a point from the right and from the left.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.12. Right-Hand Derivative.

    Let \(f\) be a function and let \(a\) be a point in its domain. The right-hand derivative of \(f\) at \(x=a\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a^+)=\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    provided this limit exists.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.13. Left-Hand Derivative.

    Let \(f\) be a function and let \(a\) be a point in its domain. The left-hand derivative of \(f\) at \(x=a\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a^-)=\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    provided this limit exists.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.2.14.

    Remember that the derivative is defined as a limit. A two-sided limit exists only when the corresponding one-sided limits both exist and agree. Therefore, \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\) only if both \(f'(a^-)\) and \(f'(a^+)\) exist and satisfy \(f'(a^-)=f'(a^+)\text{.}\) If the left-hand and right-hand derivatives do not agree, then the derivative \(f'(a)\) does not exist.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.15. A Corner.

    Show that the function \(f(x)=|x|\) is not differentiable at \(x=0\) by computing the left-hand and right-hand derivatives at that point.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We compute the one-sided derivatives at \(a=0\text{.}\) First note that \(f(0)=|0|=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To evaluate the limits, we recall the definition of absolute value from Definition 0.5.1. This definition tells us how \(|h|\) behaves depending on whether \(h\) is positive or negative.
    🔗
    The right-hand derivative is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(0^+) =\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{f(0+h)-f(0)}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{|h|}{h}. \end{equation*}
    For \(h>0\text{,}\) we have \(|h|=h\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(0^+)=\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{h}{h}=1. \end{equation*}
    The left-hand derivative is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(0^-) =\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f(0+h)-f(0)}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{|h|}{h}. \end{equation*}
    For \(h<0\text{,}\) we have \(|h|=-h\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(0^-)=\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{-h}{h}=-1. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f'(0^-)\neq f'(0^+)\text{,}\) the derivative at \(x=0\) does not exist. This corresponds graphically to a sharp corner at the origin, where the slopes approaching from the left and right are different.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The function \(f(x)=|x|\) is continuous at \(x=0\text{,}\) yet it is not differentiable there because of a sharp corner. Next, we examine a case where the function is not even continuous. In this situation, the derivative must fail to exist.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.16. A Discontinuity.

    Consider the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if } x<2, \\ 3x-1, & \text{if } x\ge 2. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Determine whether \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the definitions of Definition 2.2.13 and Definition 2.2.12, we compute:
    🔗
    The left-hand derivative at \(x=2\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^-) =\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{1-5}{h}. \end{equation*}
    This limit does not exist because the numerator is constant while the denominator approaches zero. The right-hand derivative is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^+) =\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{(3(2+h)-1)-5}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{3h}{h}=3. \end{equation*}
    Since the one-sided derivatives do not both exist and agree, the derivative \(f'(2)\) does not exist.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In all of the situations we have examined so far, differentiability has required the function to behave continuously at the point in question. This relationship between differentiability and continuity is fundamental and can be stated precisely as a theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.17. Differentiability Implies Continuity.

    If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.2.18.

    The contrapositive is often the useful version in practice: if \(f\) is not continuous at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we have studied the main ways a derivative can fail to exist, we will put these ideas together and identify the regions where a function is differentiable (that is, where the derivative exists).
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Differentiability on an Interval

    Definition 2.2.19. Differentiable on an Interval.

    Let \(I\) be an interval. We say that \(f\) is differentiable on \(I\) if \(f'(x)\) exists for every point \(x\) in \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We say that \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\) if \(f'(x)\) exists for all \(a<x<b\text{.}\) We say that \(f\) is differentiable on \([a,b]\) if \(f'(x)\) exists for all \(a<x<b\) and the one-sided derivatives \(f'(a^+)\) and \(f'(b^-)\) exist.
    🔗
    🔗
    Problems that ask where a function is differentiable often come in two common forms. In the first, only a graph is given, with no algebraic expression for the function.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.20. Differentiability from a Graph.

    The graph below represents a function \(y=f(x)\text{.}\) Identify all values of \(x\) where \(f\) is differentiable. Give your answer in interval notation.
    🔗
    Solution.
    A function is differentiable at a point only if it is continuous there and has a well-defined tangent line. We examine each notable feature of the graph.
    🔗
    At \(x=-1\text{,}\) the graph has a sharp corner. The slopes approaching from the left and right are different, so the derivative does not exist at this point.
    🔗
    At \(x=0\text{,}\) the graph has a jump discontinuity. Because the function is not continuous at \(x=0\text{,}\) it cannot be differentiable there.
    🔗
    At \(x=2\text{,}\) the graph has another discontinuity, indicated by the vertical dashed line. Since the function does not have a defined value that matches nearby behavior, the derivative does not exist at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    On all other intervals where the graph is defined, smooth, and continuous, the function is differentiable. Therefore, \(f\) is differentiable on the intervals
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (-3,-1)\cup(-1,0)\cup(0,2)\cup(2,5]. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    In the second common type of problem, the function is given by an explicit formula, often defined piecewise, and we must use algebraic reasoning to determine where the derivative exists.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.22. Determining Differentiability for a Piecewise Function.

    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} x+1, & \text{if } x<0, \\ |x|, & \text{if } 0\le x<2, \\ -\frac14x^2+2x-1, & \text{if } x\ge 2. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Determine where \(f\) is differentiable. Give your answer in interval notation.
    🔗
    Solution.
    On each open interval where a single formula applies, the function is differentiable: \(x+1\) is differentiable for \(x<0\text{,}\) \(|x|\) is differentiable for \(0<x<2\text{,}\) and \(-\frac14x^2+2x-1\) is differentiable for \(x>2\text{.}\) Therefore, the only points we need to check are the junctions \(x=0\) and \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At \(x=0\text{,}\) we first check continuity: for \(x<0\text{,}\) \(f(x)=x+1\text{,}\) so \(\lim_{x\to 0^-}f(x)=1\text{.}\) Also \(f(0)=|0|=0\text{.}\) Since \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0^-}f(x)\neq f(0)\text{,}\) the function is not continuous at \(x=0\text{,}\) and therefore it is not differentiable at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At \(x=2\text{,}\) we again check continuity: for \(0\le x<2\text{,}\) \(f(x)=|x|\text{,}\) so \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 2^-}f(x)=|2|=2\text{.}\) For \(x\ge 2\text{,}\) \(f(2)=-\frac14(2^2)+2(2)-1=-1+4-1=2\text{.}\) Since the left-hand limit equals the function value, \(f\) is continuous at \(x=2\text{.}\) To determine differentiability, we compare one-sided derivatives.
    🔗
    The left-hand derivative at \(x=2\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^-)=\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    For \(h<0\) close to 0, we have \(2+h\in(0,2)\text{,}\) so \(f(2+h)=|2+h|=2+h\text{.}\) Also \(f(2)=2\text{.}\) Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^-)=\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{(2+h)-2}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{h}{h}=1. \end{equation*}
    The right-hand derivative at \(x=2\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^+)=\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    For \(h>0\text{,}\) we have \(2+h\ge 2\text{,}\) so \(f(2+h)=-\frac14(2+h)^2+2(2+h)-1\text{.}\) Using \(f(2)=2\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^+)=\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{-\frac14(2+h)^2+2(2+h)-1-2}{h}. \end{equation*}
    Expand the numerator. Since \((2+h)^2=4+4h+h^2\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{align*} -\frac14(2+h)^2+2(2+h)-3 = \amp -\frac14(4+4h+h^2)+4+2h-3\\ = \amp \left(-1-h-\frac14h^2\right)+1+2h\\ = \amp h-\frac14h^2 \end{align*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(2^+)=\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{h-\frac14h^2}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^+}\left(1-\frac14h\right)=1. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f'(2^-)=f'(2^+)=1\text{,}\) the derivative exists at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Putting everything together, \(f\) is differentiable for all real numbers except at \(x=0\text{.}\) In interval notation, \(f\) is differentiable on
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (-\infty,0)\cup(0,\infty). \end{equation*}

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Some True/False Problems.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false and explain your reasoning.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(f(x)=x^2\text{,}\) then \(f'(4)=16\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. We can calculate the derivative \(f'(x)=2x\text{,}\) so \(f'(4)=8\text{,}\) not 16.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If \(f(x)=3x-5\text{,}\) then the limit definition shows that \(f'(a)=3a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The derivative of a linear function is constant. Using the limit definition gives \(f'(a)=3\text{,}\) not \(3a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=-\dfrac{1}{x^2}\) for all \(x\neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. For \(x\neq 0\) the derivative exists, and \(f'(x)=-\dfrac{1}{x^2}\text{.}\) This can be verified directly from the limit definition by combining the fractions and simplifying the difference quotient.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    The derivative function \(f'(x)\) assigns to each input \(x\) the slope of the tangent line to \(f\) at that input.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Where the derivative exists, it equals the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(y=f(x)\) at that point.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    The derivative function \(f'(x)\) can exist at some values of \(x\) but fail to exist at others.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Many functions are differentiable on part of their domain but not everywhere, for example at corners, discontinuities, or vertical tangents.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\) then the graph of \(f\) must be smooth near \(x=a\) with no corners or jumps.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Differentiability requires both continuity and a well-defined tangent line.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    If \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. A function can be continuous but not differentiable. For example, \(f(x)=|x|\) is continuous at \(x=0\) but has a corner there, so \(f'(0)\) does not exist.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    If \(\displaystyle f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\) in an interval, then \(f\) is increasing on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. A positive derivative means tangent slopes are positive, which corresponds to increasing behavior.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(f'(a)\) exists, then \(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) must be a finite number.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. By definition, \(f'(a)\) is the value of this limit. If the limit were infinite or did not exist, then \(f'(a)\) would be undefined.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    A vertical tangent line corresponds to a derivative value of zero.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. A vertical tangent line has undefined slope. This corresponds to the derivative failing to exist (the slopes become unbounded), not to a derivative of zero.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    11.
    Algebraic simplification of the difference quotient is used to eliminate the \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) form before taking the limit.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Direct substitution often yields \(\tfrac{0}{0}\text{,}\) so we simplify the difference quotient (factor, combine fractions, use conjugates, identities) to evaluate the limit.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    12.
    The right-hand derivative \(f'(a^+)\) uses only values of the function for inputs greater than \(a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) uses \(h>0\text{,}\) so \(a+h>a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    13.
    If \(f'(a^-)\) and \(f'(a^+)\) both exist and are equal, then \(f'(a)\) exists.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The derivative \(f'(a)\) is a two-sided limit. If the one-sided limits exist and agree, then the two-sided limit exists and equals their common value.
