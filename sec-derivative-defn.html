    Section 2.2 Definition of the Derivative

    Objectives

    • Compute the derivative \(f'(a)\) using the limit definition.
      ðŸ”—
    • Interpret the derivative as an instantaneous rate of change and as the slope of a tangent line.
      ðŸ”—
    • Rewrite the derivative definition using either \(h\to 0\) or \(x\to a\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
    • Define the derivative function \(f'(x)\) and interpret it as a new function.
      ðŸ”—
    • Recognize common situations where a derivative fails to exist (corners, cusps, vertical tangents, discontinuities).
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Definition of the Derivative

    In earlier sections, we saw that instantaneous velocity can be obtained as a limit of average velocities over shorter and shorter time increments. Likewise, the slope of a tangent line can be obtained as a limit of secant slopes as the secant point moves closer and closer to the point of tangency.
    ðŸ”—
    Both ideas measure an instantaneous rate of change. For a general function \(f(x)\text{,}\) we define its instantaneous rate of change at \(x=a\) using the following limit.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.2.1. Derivative at a Point.

    The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\text{,}\) denoted \(f'(a)\text{,}\) is defined by
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    provided the limit exists.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Here \(f(a+h)-f(a)\) is the change in the output, and \(h\) is the change in the input. The quotient \(\displaystyle\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\) is the average rate of change over the interval from \(a\) to \(a+h\text{,}\) and the derivative is the limit as the increment \(h\) shrinks to 0.
    ðŸ”—
    Another common form comes from substituting \(x=a+h\) (so that \(h=x-a\)):
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}. \end{equation*}
    Geometrically, if \(f'(a)\) exists, then it is the slope of the tangent line to \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((a,f(a))\text{.}\) The tangent line has equation
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} y=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a). \end{equation*}

    Remark 2.2.2.

    If the limit defining \(f'(a)\) exists, we say \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\) If the limit does not exist, then \(f'(a)\) is undefined and \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Computing Derivatives from the Definition

    A derivative is always a limit of a difference quotient, so direct substitution usually produces the meaningless expression \(\tfrac{0}{0}\text{.}\) The goal is to simplify the difference quotient until the limit can be evaluated.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.3. A Rational Example.

    Find \(f'(2)\) for \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x+1}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Step by Step:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcll} \displaystyle f'(2) \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\frac{1}{(2+h)+1}-\frac{1}{2+1}}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{h}\left(\frac{1}{h+3}-\frac{1}{3}\right) \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{h}\left(\frac{3-(h+3)}{3(h+3)}\right) \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-h}{3h(h+3)} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-1}{3(h+3)} \ =\ -\frac{1}{9}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.4. A Radical Example.

    Find \(f'(2)\) for \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We multiply by the conjugate to eliminate the radical in the numerator.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcll} \displaystyle f'(2) \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{2+h}-\sqrt{2}}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{2+h}-\sqrt{2}}{h}\cdot \frac{\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2}} \\[1.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(2+h)-2}{h(\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2})} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2+h}+\sqrt{2}} \ =\ \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.5. A Cube Root Example.

    Find \(f'(x)\) for \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Here we use the identity \((a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2)=a^3-b^3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt[3]{x+h}-\sqrt[3]{x}}{h} \\[1.4em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sqrt[3]{x+h}-\sqrt[3]{x}}{h}\cdot \frac{\left(\sqrt[3]{x+h}\right)^2+\sqrt[3]{x+h}\sqrt[3]{x}+\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right)^2} {\left(\sqrt[3]{x+h}\right)^2+\sqrt[3]{x+h}\sqrt[3]{x}+\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right)^2} \\[1.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(x+h)-x} {h\left(\left(\sqrt[3]{x+h}\right)^2+\sqrt[3]{x+h}\sqrt[3]{x}+\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right)^2\right)} \\[1.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1} {\left(\sqrt[3]{x+h}\right)^2+\sqrt[3]{x+h}\sqrt[3]{x}+\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right)^2} \\[1.6em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\frac{1}{3\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right)^2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This example is a good preview of why we will soon develop derivative rules: computing derivatives from the limit definition can become algebraically heavy.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection The Derivative Function

    The derivative at a point \(f'(a)\) is a single number. But we can compute the derivative for many input values, producing a new function.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.2.6. Derivative Function.

    The derivative function of \(f\text{,}\) denoted \(f'\text{,}\) is defined by
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}, \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) where this limit exists.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.7. Computing a Derivative Function.

    Let \(f(x)=x(x-2)\text{.}\) Find \(f'(x)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Step by Step:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(x+h)(x+h-2)-x(x-2)}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{x^2+2xh+h^2-2x-2h-x^2+2x}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{2xh-2h+h^2}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\left(2x-2+h\right) \ =\ 2x-2. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection When the Derivative Fails to Exist

    Even if a function is defined at a point, the derivative may fail to exist there. Graphically, this happens when the curve does not have a single well-defined tangent line.
    ðŸ”—
    Common ways differentiability can fail include:
    ðŸ”—
    • Corner: the left-hand and right-hand slopes are finite but different.
      ðŸ”—
    • Cusp: the left-hand and right-hand slopes become infinite with opposite signs.
      ðŸ”—
    • Vertical tangent: slopes become infinite (unbounded) with the same sign.
      ðŸ”—
    • Discontinuity: if a function is not continuous at a point, then it cannot be differentiable there.
      ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.8. One-Sided Derivatives Agree.

    Let
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} 2x-1, & x\le 1,\\ x^2, & x\ge 1. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Show that \(f'(1)\) exists and find its value.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We compute the right-hand and left-hand derivatives.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h} \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{(1+h)^2-1}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{h(2+h)}{h} \ =\ 2, \end{array} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h} \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{(2(1+h)-1)-1}{h} \\[1.2em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{2h}{h} \ =\ 2. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since the one-sided derivatives agree, \(f'(1)=2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.9. A Corner.

    Consider \(g(x)=|x|\text{.}\) Explain why \(g'(0)\) does not exist.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    For \(x>0\text{,}\) \(|x|=x\text{,}\) so the slope near 0 from the right is 1. For \(x<0\text{,}\) \(|x|=-x\text{,}\) so the slope near 0 from the left is \(-1\text{.}\) The left-hand and right-hand derivatives are not equal, so the derivative at 0 does not exist. Graphically, the curve has a corner at the origin.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.10. A Vertical Tangent.

    Consider \(h(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\text{.}\) Explain why the derivative at \(x=0\) fails to exist as a finite number.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    From the derivative function computed earlier, \(\displaystyle h'(x)=\frac{1}{3(\sqrt[3]{x})^2}\text{.}\) As \(x\to 0\text{,}\) the denominator approaches 0, so the slopes become unbounded. Graphically, the curve has a vertical tangent at the origin, so there is no finite tangent slope at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.2.11. A Discontinuity.

    Suppose \(p(x)\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x=a\text{.}\) Explain why \(p'(a)\) cannot exist.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    If \(p'(a)\) existed, then \(p\) would be differentiable at \(a\text{,}\) and differentiability forces continuity. But a jump discontinuity means \(p\) is not continuous at \(a\text{.}\) Therefore \(p'(a)\) cannot exist.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Differentiability Implies Continuity

    Theorem 2.2.12. Differentiability Implies Continuity.

    If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.2.13.

    The contrapositive is often the useful version in practice: if \(f\) is not continuous at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Practice / Study Problems

    Exercises Exercises

    Some True/False Problems.
    Determine whether each statement is true or false and explain your reasoning.
    ðŸ”—
    1.
    If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. Differentiability implies continuity.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    2.
    If \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    False. A corner (like \(|x|\) at 0) is continuous but not differentiable.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    3.
    The derivative \(f'(a)\) is the limit of slopes of secant lines through \((a,f(a))\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. That is exactly what the difference quotient represents.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    4.
    If \(\displaystyle f'(a)=0\text{,}\) then \(f(a)\) must be a local maximum or minimum.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    False. Horizontal tangent does not guarantee an extremum.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    5.
    If the left-hand derivative and right-hand derivative at \(a\) are equal, then \(f'(a)\) exists.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. Agreement of the one-sided derivative limits gives the two-sided derivative limit.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    6.
    If a graph has a corner at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f'(a)\) does not exist.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. The one-sided slopes are different.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    7.
    A vertical tangent at \(x=a\) means \(f'(a)=0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    False. A vertical tangent corresponds to unbounded slope, not zero slope.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    8.
    If \(f\) is discontinuous at \(x=a\text{,}\) then \(f'(a)\) does not exist.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. Discontinuity prevents differentiability.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    9.
    The derivative function \(f'(x)\) is defined for every \(x\) where \(f(x)\) is defined.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    False. A function can be defined but not differentiable at some points.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    10.
    If \(\displaystyle f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\) in an interval, then \(f\) is increasing on that interval.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. Positive derivative corresponds to increasing behavior.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    11.
    For \(f(x)=c\) (a constant function), we have \(\displaystyle f'(a)=0\) for every \(a\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. The output does not change as the input changes.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    12.
    If two functions have the same derivative on an interval, then they differ by a constant on that interval.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    True. This idea will be justified more formally later, but it matches the meaning of â€œsame rate of change.â€
