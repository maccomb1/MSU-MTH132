    Section 3.2 Related Rates

    Objectives

    • Identify all relevant quantities in a related rates problem, distinguishing between constants, variables, known rates, and unknown rates.
      🔗
    • Translate a physical or geometric situation into an equation relating the variables using appropriate formulas.
      🔗
    • Create and label an appropriate diagram to support problem setup and reasoning.
      🔗
    • Apply differentiation rules to a relationship involving multiple time-dependent quantities using the chain rule and possibly implicit differentiation.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Introduction to Related Rates

    Many real-world situations involve quantities that change over time. In some cases, the rates of change of certain quantities are known, and we want to find the rates of change of other related quantities. These problems are called related rates problems.
    🔗
    The key steps in solving related rates problems are:
    🔗

    Strategy 3.2.1. Steps for Solving Related Rates Problems.

    1. Establish a relationship between the variables using geometry or known formulas.
      🔗
    2. Differentiate the relationship with respect to time, applying the Chain Rule as needed.
      🔗
    3. Substituting known values and rates to find the unknown rate.
      🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.2.2.

    Related rates problems often involve geometric relationships, such as those found in triangles, circles, or other shapes. Some students find that every problem feels a bit different, but the overall method remains consistent across various scenarios.
    🔗
    Remember that our formula sheet, included in Appendix A includes many geometric formulas that can be useful in setting up these problems and that many of the quantities involved may depend on time, even if not explicitly stated (so the chain rule will often be needed).
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we have the technique outlined, let’s explore some examples to see how these steps are applied in practice.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Select Examples

    Example 3.2.3. Spill Example.

    A stream of water spreads a circular puddle on the floor. If the puddle is 1 meter across and the stream increases the area at a rate of \(2\) m\(^2\)/min, how quickly is the puddle’s radius widening at that instant?
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can see in this problem that they give us the rate of change of the area of the puddle, and we are asked to find the rate of change of the radius, these are the two rates we are try to relate together in this problem.
    🔗
    So for this problem, since the puddle is circular, we can use the formula for the area of a circle, which is \(A=\pi r^2\text{,}\) where \(r\) is the radius of the circle.
    🔗
    Since both the area and the radius are changing over time, we can express them as functions of time: \(A(t)\) and \(r(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We are given that the area is increasing at a rate of \(2\) m\(^2\)/min, which means \(A'(t)=2\) m\(^2\)/min. We are asked to find how quickly the radius is widening, which means we need to find \(r'(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find \(r'(t)\text{,}\) we can differentiate the area formula with respect to time using the Chain Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pi r(t)^2, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle A'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pi \cdot 2r(t) \cdot r'(t) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2\pi r(t) r'(t). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we can solve for \(r'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{A'(t)}{2\pi r(t)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we will take the \(t=0\) to be the time where the puddle is 1 meter across (so \(r(0)=\frac12\)). Recall also we know that \(A'(0)=2\text{.}\) So therefore:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{A'(0)}{2\pi r(0)} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{2}{2\pi(\frac12)} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{2}{\pi} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the radius is widening at a rate of \(\frac{2}{\pi}\) m/min when the puddle is 1 meter across.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.5. Pulley Example.

    Consider a weight hanging from a rope that goes up to a pulley 10 ft above the floor and then down to your hand, which is 3 ft above the floor and 15 ft horizontally from the pulley. If you walk away from the pulley at \(2\) ft/sec, how fast will the weight rise?
    🔗
    Solution.
    First let’s define our variables. Let \(x(t)\) be the horizontal distance from the pulley to your hand, let \(r(t)\) be the length of the rope segment from the pulley to your hand, and finally let \(h(t)\) be the height (vertical distance) from your hand to the pulley.
    🔗
    Since your hand is 3 ft above the floor and the pulley is 10 ft above the floor, the height \(h(t)\) between the two will be a constant can be expressed as:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle h(t) = \displaystyle 10-3 = \displaystyle 7 \end{equation*}
    So no matter the time, \(h(t)=7\) is constant. Also notice that because the rope is connected to the weight, as the rope length \(r(t)\) increases, the height of the weight will decrease at the same rate. So if we can find \(r'(t)\text{,}\) we will also have found the rate at which the weight is rising.
    🔗
    Finally, notice that these three variables (\(r(t)\text{,}\) \(h(t)\text{,}\) and \(x(t)\)) form a right triangle with the hypotenuse being the rope segment from the pulley to your hand. So we can use the Pythagorean theorem to get:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle h(t)^2 + x(t)^2, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 7^2 + x(t)^2, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 49 + x(t)^2. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we can differentiate both sides with respect to time \(t\) using the Chain Rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \left(r(t)^2\right)' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \left(49 + x(t)^2\right)', \\[0.9em] \displaystyle 2r(t) r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2x(t) x'(t). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we can solve for \(r'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{x(t) x'(t)}{r(t)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Next we can let \(t=0\) be the time of interest (when we are 15 ft from the pulley), so \(x(0)=15\) and \(x'(0)=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Also, at time \(t=0\text{,}\) we can compute \(r(0)\) using the Pythagorean theorem:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r(0)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 49 + x(0)^2 \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(0)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 49 + 15^2 \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(0)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 49 + 225 \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(0)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 274 \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \sqrt{274}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Substituting into our expression for \(r'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{x(0) x'(0)}{r(0)} \\[1.5em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{15 \cdot 2}{\sqrt{274}} \\[1.5em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{30}{\sqrt{274}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the rope length is increasing at a rate of \(\frac{30}{\sqrt{274}}\) ft/sec when you are 15 ft from the pulley which means that the box is rising at the same rate of \(\frac{30}{\sqrt{274}}\) ft/sec.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.2.7.

    Plug in the current values only at the last step. If you substitute before differentiating, you turn variables into constants and their derivatives become zero.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.8. Searchlight Example.

    A searchlight shines along a wall 20 meters away. If the light is currently \(30^\circ\) away from pointing directly at the wall and the light is rotating at \(5^\circ\) per second, what is the speed of the bright spot moving along the wall at that instant?
    🔗
    Remark 3.2.9.
    We are intentionally not providing a figure for this example (until you look at the solution). Try drawing your own diagram to help visualize the situation. This is an important skill when solving related rates problems that may be required for quizzes or exams.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Here is a sketch of the situation:
    🔗
    Let \(\theta(t)\) be the angle of the light beam from the horizontal and \(s(t)\) be the distance of the spot from the point on the wall closest to the light. We are given that at some time of interest (which we will call \(t=0\)) that \(\theta(0)=30^\circ\) and \(\theta'(0)=5^\circ/\text{sec}\text{,}\) and we want to find \(s'(0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the situation is about a right triangle formed by the light beam, the wall, and the ground, we have several options to consider (like sine, cosine, or tangent or the pythagorean theorem). However, to relate the angle and the distance along the wall we should use the tangent function. This gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \tan(\theta(t)) = \frac{s(t)}{20} \end{equation*}
    Differentiating both sides with respect to time:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sec^2(\theta(t)) \cdot \theta'(t) = \frac{s'(t)}{20} \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(s'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s'(t) = 20 \cdot \sec^2(\theta(t)) \cdot \theta'(t) \end{equation*}
    Now we can substitute in our known values at \(t=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s'(0) = 20 \cdot \sec^2(30^\circ) \cdot 5^\circ/\text{sec} \end{equation*}
    However, because the trig differentiation formulas are only valid for radian measure (See Remark 2.4.5), we need to convert he angle and angular speed to radians before substituting:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle 30^\circ \amp=\amp \displaystyle 30\left(\frac{2\pi}{360}\right) \ =\ \frac{\pi}{6}\ \text{rad}, \\[1.0em] \displaystyle 5^\circ/\text{sec} \amp=\amp \displaystyle 5\left(\frac{2\pi}{360}\right) \ =\ \frac{\pi}{36}\ \text{rad/sec}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now substituting in:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle s'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 20 \cdot \sec^2\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) \cdot \frac{\pi}{36} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\cos(\pi/6)=\sqrt{3}/2\text{,}\) we have \(\sec^2(\pi/6)=\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\right)^2=\frac{4}{3}\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle s'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 20 \cdot \frac{4}{3} \cdot \frac{\pi}{36} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{20\pi}{27} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So the speed of the bright spot moving along the wall at that instant is \(\frac{20}{27}\pi\) m/sec.
    🔗
    Remark 3.2.11.
    The problem asks for speed, so report \(|s'(0)|\text{.}\) If the rotation direction were opposite, the derivative would change sign but the speed would be the same.
    🔗
    🔗
    Remark 3.2.12.
    If you mistakenly use \(\theta'(0)=5\) in degrees/sec inside the derivative formula, the numerical answer will be wrong. Convert to radians/sec before substituting.
    🔗

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    True/False Questions.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false. Justify your answers.
    🔗
    1.
    In any related rates problem, all quantities that change must be written as functions of time, even if time is not mentioned explicitly in the problem.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. In related rates problems, all variables must be expressed as functions of time to properly apply the chain rule. It is possible that some quantities do not change (and thus are constants), but any quantity that does change must be treated as a function of time.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If a related rates problem asks for a speed, the final answer should be reported as a nonnegative value even if the derivative is negative.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Speed is defined as the magnitude of velocity, which is always nonnegative. Therefore, when a related rates problem asks for speed, the final answer should be reported as a nonnegative value, regardless of the sign of the derivative.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    In a related rates problem involving a right triangle, the Pythagorean Theorem must be used to relate the variables before differentiating.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. While the Pythagorean Theorem is often useful in related rates problems involving right triangles, it is not always necessary. Depending on the specific problem, other relationships such as trigonometric functions (sine, cosine, tangent) or geometric formulas may be more appropriate to relate the variables before differentiating.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(x(t)^2 + y(t)^2 = 25\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} 2x(t)x'(t) + 2y(t)y'(t) = 0 \end{equation*}
    after differentiating with respect to time.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Differentiating both sides of the equation \(x(t)^2 + y(t)^2 = 25\) with respect to time using the chain rule gives:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dt}(x(t)^2) + \frac{d}{dt}(y(t)^2) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dt}(25) \\[0.9em] \displaystyle 2x(t)x'(t) + 2y(t)y'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 0. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This confirms that the statement is true.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    A circle has radius \(4\) meters at a certain instant. If the radius is increasing at \(3\) m/min, then the area is increasing at \(12\pi\) m\(^2\)/min at that instant.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The area \(A\) of a circle is given by the formula \(A = \pi r^2\text{,}\) where \(r\) is the radius. To find the rate of change of the area with respect to time, we differentiate both sides with respect to time using the chain rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pi r(t)^2, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle A'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pi \cdot 2r(t) \cdot r'(t) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2\pi r(t) r'(t). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Substituting the given values \(r = 4\) m and \(r' = 3\) m/min into the equation for \(A'\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A' \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2\pi (4)(3) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 24\pi \text{ m}^2/\text{min}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This shows that the area is increasing at \(24\pi\) m\(^2\)/min, not \(12\pi\) m\(^2\)/min as stated.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If the volume of a cube is increasing at \(12\) cm\(^3\)/sec when the side length is \(2\) cm, then the side length is increasing at \(1\) cm/sec at that instant.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The volume \(V\) of a cube is given by the formula \(V = s^3\text{,}\) where \(s\) is the side length. To find the rate of change of the side length with respect to time, we differentiate both sides with respect to time using the chain rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle V(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle s(t)^3, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle V'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 3s(t)^2 \cdot s'(t). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Substituting the given values \(V' = 12\) cm\(^3\)/sec and \(s = 2\) cm into the equation for \(V'\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle 12 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 3(2)^2 \cdot s'(t) \\[0.9em] \displaystyle 12 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 12 \cdot s'(t) \\[0.9em] \displaystyle s'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 1 \text{ cm/sec}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This confirms that the side length is increasing at \(1\) cm/sec at that instant.

    7.
    A ladder 10 ft long is leaning against a vertical wall. If the bottom of the ladder is sliding away from the wall at 1 ft/sec, then the top of the ladder is sliding down the wall at 1 ft/sec when the bottom of the ladder is 6 ft from the wall.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. Let \(x(t)\) be the distance from the wall to the bottom of the ladder and \(y(t)\) be the height of the top of the ladder on the wall. The relationship between \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) is given by the Pythagorean theorem:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle x(t)^2 + y(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 10^2, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle x(t)^2 + y(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 100. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Differentiating both sides with respect to time:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle 2x(t)x'(t) + 2y(t)y'(t) = \displaystyle 0. \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(y'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle y'(t) = \displaystyle -\frac{x(t)x'(t)}{y(t)}. \end{equation*}
    At the instant when \(x(t) = 6\) ft and \(x'(t) = 1\) ft/sec, we can find \(y(t)\) using the Pythagorean theorem:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle 6^2 + y(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 100, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle 36 + y(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 100, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle y(t)^2 \amp=\amp \displaystyle 64, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle y(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 8 \text{ ft}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Substituting these values into the equation for \(y'(t)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle y'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle -\frac{6 \cdot 1}{8} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle -\frac{3}{4} \text{ ft/sec}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This shows that the top of the ladder is sliding down the wall at a rate of \(\frac{3}{4}\) ft/sec, not 1 ft/sec as stated.

    8.
    If the radius of a sphere is increasing at a constant rate, then the volume of the sphere is also increasing at a constant rate.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The volume of a sphere is given by the formula \(V=\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\text{.}\) If the radius \(r\) is increasing at a constant rate, the volume \(V\) will not increase at a constant rate because the relationship between volume and radius is cubic. As the radius increases, the rate of change of the volume will also increase.
    🔗
    A simple example would be if \(r(t) = t\) (which has a constant rate of change of \(r'(t) = 1\)), then \(V(t) = \frac{4}{3}\pi t^3\text{,}\) and the derivative \(V'(t) = 4\pi t^2\) is not constant.
