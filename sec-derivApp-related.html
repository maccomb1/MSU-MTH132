    Section 3.2 Related Rates

    Objectives

    • Set up a related rates problem by identifying variables, constants, current values, and known rates.
      ðŸ”—
    • Write a geometric relationship among variables, then differentiate with respect to time using the Chain Rule.
      ðŸ”—
    • Compute an unknown rate at a specific instant by substituting current values only at the end.
      ðŸ”—
    • Check answers for sign and real-world plausibility, including unit consistency.
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Pulley Example

    Consider a weight hanging from a rope that goes up to a pulley 10 ft above the floor and then down to your hand, which is 3 ft above the floor and 15 ft horizontally from the pulley. If you walk away from the pulley at \(2\) ft/sec, how fast will the weight rise?
    ðŸ”—
    This is a related rates problem: we want an unknown rate of change from a known rate, using a geometric relationship. Start by drawing a picture and labeling constants and variables. Let \(h(t)\) be the horizontal distance from the weight to your hand, and let \(r(t)\) be the length of the rope segment from the pulley to your hand. The problem gives the current value \(h(0)=15\) and the current rate \(h'(0)=2\) (ft/sec). We want \(r'(0)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The vertical distance from your hand up to the pulley is constant: \(10-3=7\) ft. By the Pythagorean Theorem, the geometry implies
    \begin{equation*} r(t)^2=h(t)^2+7^2=h(t)^2+49. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    One approach is to solve for \(r(t)\) and then differentiate.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \sqrt{h(t)^2+49} \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2}(h(t)^2+49)^{-1/2}\cdot \left(h(t)^2+49\right)' \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2}(h(t)^2+49)^{-1/2}\cdot 2h(t)h'(t) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{h(t)h'(t)}{\sqrt{h(t)^2+49}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now substitute the current values:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{h(0)\,h'(0)}{\sqrt{h(0)^2+49}} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{(15)(2)}{\sqrt{15^2+49}} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{30}{\sqrt{274}} \approx 1.8\ \text{ft/sec}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The weight rises at the same rate that the rope segment \(r(t)\) increases, so the weight rises at about \(1.8\) ft/sec at that instant.
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 3.2.2.

    Plug in the current values only at the last step. If you substitute before differentiating, you turn variables into constants and their derivatives become zero.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We can also do this more directly by implicitly differentiating the relationship \(r(t)^2=h(t)^2+49\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \left(r(t)^2\right)' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \left(h(t)^2+49\right)' \\[0.9em] \displaystyle 2r(t)r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2h(t)h'(t) \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{h(t)h'(t)}{r(t)}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since \(r(0)=\sqrt{h(0)^2+49}=\sqrt{274}\text{,}\) this gives the same numerical result as above.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Spill Radius Example

    A stream of water spreads a circular puddle on the floor. If the puddle is 1 meter across and the stream increases the area at a rate of \(2\) m2/min, how quickly is the puddle widening at that instant?
    ðŸ”—
    Let \(r(t)\) be the puddleâ€™s radius and \(A(t)\) its area. A diameter of 1 m means the current radius is \(r(0)=\frac12\text{.}\) The given rate is \(A'(0)=2\) m2/min. We want \(r'(0)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The geometric relationship is
    \begin{equation*} A(t)=\pi r(t)^2. \end{equation*}
    Differentiate both sides with respect to time:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pi\left(r(t)^2\right)' \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 2\pi r(t)r'(t). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Solve for the target rate and substitute the current values:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle r'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{A'(t)}{2\pi r(t)} \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{A'(0)}{2\pi r(0)} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{2}{2\pi(\frac12)} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{2}{\pi} \approx 0.64\ \text{m/min}. \end{array} \end{equation*}

    Remark 3.2.4.

    Always check plausibility. A radius increase of about 0.6 m per minute can be reasonable, depending on flow rate and surface.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Searchlight Example

    A searchlight shines along a wall 20 meters away. If the light is currently \(30^\circ\) away from pointing directly at the wall and the light is rotating at \(5^\circ\) per second, what is the speed of the bright spot moving along the wall at that instant?
    ðŸ”—
    Let \(\theta(t)\) be the angle and \(s(t)\) the distance of the spot from the point on the wall closest to the light. The geometry gives
    \begin{equation*} \tan(\theta(t))=\frac{s(t)}{20}, \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} s(t)=20\tan(\theta(t)). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Differentiate with respect to time:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle s'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 20\left(\tan(\theta(t))\right)'\,\theta'(t) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 20\sec^2(\theta(t))\,\theta'(t). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Trig derivatives require radians. Convert the current angle and angular speed:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \theta(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 30^\circ \ =\ 30\left(\frac{2\pi}{360}\right) \ =\ \frac{\pi}{6}\ \text{rad}, \\[1.0em] \displaystyle \theta'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 5^\circ/\text{sec} \ =\ 5\left(\frac{2\pi}{360}\right) \ =\ \frac{\pi}{36}\ \text{rad/sec}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now compute:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle s'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 20\sec^2\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right)\cdot \frac{\pi}{36}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\cos(\pi/6)=\sqrt{3}/2\text{,}\) we have \(\sec^2(\pi/6)=\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\right)^2=\frac{4}{3}\text{,}\) so
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle s'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 20\cdot \frac{4}{3}\cdot \frac{\pi}{36} \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{20\pi}{27} \approx 2.3\ \text{m/sec}. \end{array} \end{equation*}

    Remark 3.2.6.

    The problem asks for speed, so report \(|s'(0)|\text{.}\) If the rotation direction were opposite, the derivative would change sign but the speed would be the same.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 3.2.7.

    If you mistakenly use \(\theta'(0)=5\) in degrees/sec inside the derivative formula, the numerical answer will be wrong. Convert to radians/sec before substituting.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Flashlight Examples

    A flashlight shines a cone of light straight toward a wall. The lit region on the wall is a circle. What is the rate of change of the lit circular area if: (a) the flashlight moves toward the wall at 2 m/sec; (b) the flashlight stays 5 m from the wall, but the focus narrows so the total angle decreases at \(10^\circ\) per second?
    ðŸ”—

    Subsubsection Case (a): Moving Toward the Wall

    Let \(\ell(t)\) be the distance from the wall, \(r(t)\) the radius of the lit circle, and \(A(t)\) its area. We are given \(\ell(0)=5\text{,}\) \(\ell'(0)=-2\text{,}\) and \(r(0)=\frac12\text{.}\) Similar triangles show \(\frac{r(t)}{\ell(t)}=\frac{1/2}{5}=\frac{1}{10}\text{,}\) so \(r(t)=\frac{1}{10}\ell(t)\text{,}\) and therefore \(A(t)=\pi r(t)^2=\frac{\pi}{100}\ell(t)^2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Differentiate and substitute:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\pi}{100}\left(\ell(t)^2\right)' \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\pi}{50}\ell(t)\,\ell'(t), \\[1.2em] \displaystyle A'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\pi}{50}\ell(0)\,\ell'(0) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{\pi}{50}(5)(-2) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle -\frac{\pi}{5}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s} \approx -0.63\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The negative sign matches the situation: as the flashlight moves closer, the circle on the wall shrinks.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsubsection Case (b): Narrowing the Beam

    Now the distance is constant at 5 m, but the beam angle changes. Let \(\theta(t)\) be the total cone angle and let \(r(t)\) be the radius of the lit circle. The right triangle in the diagram uses the half-angle \(\theta(t)/2\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \tan\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{r(t)}{5}, \\[0.9em] \displaystyle r(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 5\tan\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right), \\[0.9em] \displaystyle A(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \pi r(t)^2 \ =\ 25\pi \tan^2\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Differentiate using the Chain Rule:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 25\pi \left(\tan^2\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right)\right)' \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 50\pi \tan\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right)\left(\tan\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right)\right)'\cdot \left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right)' \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle 50\pi \tan\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right)\sec^2\!\left(\frac{\theta(t)}{2}\right)\cdot \frac{\theta'(t)}{2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    We are given \(\theta'(0)=-10^\circ/\text{sec}\text{,}\) so \(\theta'(0)/2=-5^\circ/\text{sec}\text{.}\) Convert to radians/sec: \(\displaystyle \frac{\theta'(0)}{2}=-5\left(\frac{2\pi}{360}\right)=-\frac{\pi}{36}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We are not given \(\theta(0)\text{,}\) but we are given \(r(0)=\frac12\) and distance 5 m, so \(\tan(\theta(0)/2)=\frac{(1/2)}{5}=\frac{1}{10}\text{.}\) Also, using the same right triangle, the adjacent side is 5 and the opposite side is \(1/2\text{,}\) so the hypotenuse squared is \(5^2+(1/2)^2=25+\frac14=\frac{101}{4}\text{,}\) giving \(\sec^2(\theta(0)/2)=\frac{\text{hyp}^2}{\text{adj}^2}=\frac{101/4}{25}=\frac{101}{100}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Substitute into the derivative formula:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle A'(0) \amp=\amp \displaystyle 50\pi\left(\frac{1}{10}\right)\left(\frac{101}{100}\right)\left(-\frac{\pi}{36}\right) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle -\frac{101}{720}\pi^2 \approx -1.38\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The sign makes sense: the beam narrows, so the lit area decreases.
    ðŸ”—
    Remark 3.2.9.
    A quick check: \(\theta(0)=2\arctan(1/10)\approx 11^\circ\text{.}\) If the angle is decreasing at about \(10^\circ\)/sec, the circle can shrink very quickly at that instant, so a relatively large negative area rate is plausible.
