    Section 4.2 Riemann Sums and Areas

    Objectives

    • Connect the definite integral to accumulation: total change from a rate of change.
      🔗
    • Approximate a definite integral using Riemann sums (left, right, and “upper/lower” estimates).
      🔗
    • Interpret a definite integral geometrically as (signed) area under a curve.
      🔗
    • Set up \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) from a real-world description involving small increments \(\Delta x\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Use upper and lower sums to bound an integral and estimate a value to a specified accuracy.
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Review: Derivatives as Rates of Change

    A function \(y=f(x)\) has a derivative \(f'(c)\) with several complementary meanings. We will lean on these meanings as we introduce the integral.
    🔗
    • Physical: If \(y\) depends on \(x\text{,}\) then \(f'(c)\) is the rate of change of \(y\) per tiny change in \(x\text{,}\) starting at \(x=c\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Geometric: \(f'(c)\) is the slope of the graph \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((c,f(c))\text{,}\) meaning the tangent line near \(x=c\) is \(y=f(c)+f'(c)(x-c)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Numerical: For \(x\) near \(c\text{,}\) the derivative is approximated by a difference quotient: \(f'(c)\approx \dfrac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}\text{.}\) This matches the linear approximation \(f(x)\approx f(c)+f'(c)(x-c)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Algebraic: We define \(f'(c)=\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\dfrac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}\) and use derivative rules to compute \(f'(x)\) from a formula.
      🔗
      🔗
    The physical idea is the driving one: derivatives describe change, so they are essential in any system that evolves. Once a situation is translated into a derivative statement, we can use numerical approximations or algebraic rules to answer questions. For example, to find maximum values of \(f(x)\text{,}\) we look for places where the tangent slope is zero, so we solve \(f'(x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Accumulation of Influence

    In the previous section we reversed differentiation algebraically: given a function \(f(x)\text{,}\) we looked for an antiderivative \(F(x)\) with \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{.}\) Now we will build a numerical definition of antiderivative that works even when no simple formula exists.
    🔗
    Think of \(f(x)\) as the influence on a quantity \(F(x)\text{.}\) Near \(x=c\text{,}\) the derivative relationship \(F'(c)=f(c)\) says \(f(c)\approx \dfrac{\Delta F}{\Delta x}\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \Delta F \approx f(c)\,\Delta x. \end{equation*}
    A small input change \(\Delta x\) produces a small output change \(\Delta F\) controlled by \(f(c)\text{.}\) Adding many small changes gives the total change in \(F\text{.}\) This “add up tiny effects” viewpoint is what the definite integral will formalize.
    🔗
    Here are several examples of “accumulation” in everyday language:
    🔗
    • Position is the cumulative effect of velocity.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Velocity is the cumulative effect of acceleration.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Temperature is the cumulative effect of warming and cooling rates.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Energy used is the cumulative effect of power (wattage) over time.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Total mass is the cumulative effect of density over volume.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Total force is the cumulative effect of pressure over an area.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Population is the cumulative effect of net growth rate over time.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Total cost is the cumulative effect of marginal cost as production increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Riemann Sums for Distance

    To make accumulation computable, we introduce a new operation: the definite integral \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\text{.}\) We will define it numerically as a limit of approximations.
    🔗
    Imagine a toy car whose velocity is \(v(t)=t^2\) (in meters per second) over \(t\in[0,2]\text{.}\) If \(s(t)\) is position and \(s(0)=0\text{,}\) then the total distance traveled by time \(t=2\) is \(s(2)\text{.}\) Over a short time interval, distance is approximately velocity times time: \(\Delta s \approx v(t)\,\Delta t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A very rough estimate would treat the velocity as constant at the final value \(v(2)=4\text{,}\) giving \(s(2)\approx 4(2-0)=8\text{,}\) which is far too large because the car starts at rest. To improve the estimate, split \([0,2]\) into many short intervals.
    🔗
    For example, take \(n=20\) subintervals, each of width \(\Delta t=\dfrac{2}{20}=0.1\text{.}\) Using right endpoints \(t_i=0.1,0.2,\ldots,2.0\text{,}\) we get a right Riemann sum:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} s(2) &\approx& v(0.1)\Delta t+v(0.2)\Delta t+\cdots+v(2.0)\Delta t \\[0.6em] &=& (0.1)^2(0.1)+(0.2)^2(0.1)+\cdots+(2.0)^2(0.1) \\[0.6em] &\approx& 2.87. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since \(v(t)=t^2\) is increasing, sampling at the right endpoints gives an overestimate. Sampling at the left endpoints \(t_i=0.0,0.1,\ldots,1.9\) gives an underestimate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} s(2) &\approx& v(0.0)\Delta t+v(0.1)\Delta t+\cdots+v(1.9)\Delta t \\[0.6em] &=& (0.0)^2(0.1)+(0.1)^2(0.1)+\cdots+(1.9)^2(0.1) \\[0.6em] &\approx& 2.47. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So \(s(2)\) lies between 2.47 and 2.87. If we increase \(n\text{,}\) the rectangles get thinner and the estimates improve. This motivates the definition of the integral as a limit.
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.1. Definite Integral (Riemann Sum Definition).

    Let \(f\) be a function on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Partition \([a,b]\) into \(n\) subintervals of equal width \(\Delta x=\dfrac{b-a}{n}\text{,}\) and choose a sample point \(x_i\) in each subinterval. If the limit exists as \(n\to\infty\text{,}\) we define
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \stackrel{\text{def}}= \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(f(x_1)\Delta x+f(x_2)\Delta x+\cdots+f(x_n)\Delta x\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The symbol \(\int\) is an elongated \(S\) for “sum.” The \(dx\) reminds us that \(\Delta x\) is becoming very small.
    🔗
    For this particular example we can also check against an algebraic antiderivative. Since \(s'(t)=t^2\) and \(s(0)=0\text{,}\) we have \(s(t)=\tfrac13 t^3\text{,}\) so \(s(2)=\tfrac13(2^3)=\tfrac83\approx 2.667\text{,}\) which lies between the two estimates above.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Integral as Total Change

    The distance story is one example of a more general idea. If \(f(x)\) is a rate of change of some quantity \(F(x)\text{,}\) meaning \(F'(x)=f(x)\text{,}\) then adding up tiny changes \(\Delta F\approx f(x)\Delta x\) produces the total change \(F(b)-F(a)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F(b)-F(a)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx. \end{equation*}
    You can read this as: “the total change equals the accumulated rate of change.” Later, we will prove this relationship (and learn fast techniques for computing integrals) in the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Area Under a Curve

    Now comes one of the most useful interpretations of the integral. Suppose \(f(x)\ge 0\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) is the area under the graph \(y=f(x)\) above the \(x\)-axis from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To see why, approximate the area with rectangles. Over each small subinterval, the area of a rectangle is (height)\(\times\)(width) \(\approx f(x_i)\Delta x\text{.}\) Adding these areas produces the same type of Riemann sum we used for distance. Taking a limit gives the exact area.
    🔗

    Example 4.2.2.

    Interpret \(\displaystyle \int_0^2 x^2\,dx\) as an area, and connect it to the distance example.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(x^2\ge 0\) on \([0,2]\text{,}\) the integral equals the area under \(y=x^2\) above \([0,2]\text{.}\) Approximating with right-endpoint rectangles produces the same sum we computed for distance with velocity \(v(t)=t^2\text{.}\) The area and the distance are numerically equal because both are defined by the same limiting process: in each case we add up terms of the form “height” \(\times\) “width.”
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In this section we are focusing on functions that stay nonnegative, so “area under the curve” matches the integral directly. Later, when a function dips below the axis, the integral still makes sense but it represents signed area (area above minus area below).
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Approximating an Integral with Upper and Lower Sums

    Sometimes we want a numerical value of an integral even when we do not yet have a quick algebraic method. Upper and lower Riemann sums give guaranteed bounds.
    🔗

    Example 4.2.3.

    Approximate \(\displaystyle \int_0^\pi \sin^2(x)\,dx\) to one decimal place. (That is, find a value within \(\pm 0.05\text{.}\))
    🔗
    Solution.
    The function \(\sin^2(x)\) increases on \([0,\tfrac{\pi}{2}]\) and decreases on \([\tfrac{\pi}{2},\pi]\text{.}\) So an upper estimate uses right endpoints on the first half and left endpoints on the second half. A lower estimate does the opposite.
    🔗
    Take \(n=100\) equal subintervals, so \(\Delta x=\dfrac{\pi}{100}=0.01\pi\text{.}\) A computed upper sum gives about 1.60 and a computed lower sum gives about 1.54, so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1.54 < \int_0^\pi \sin^2(x)\,dx < 1.60. \end{equation*}
    Averaging the bounds gives a good estimate: \(\displaystyle \int_0^\pi \sin^2(x)\,dx \approx 1.57\text{,}\) which is certainly accurate to one decimal place.
    🔗
    (Later, once we have more integral tools, we can compute this exactly and confirm the approximation.)
    🔗
    🔗
