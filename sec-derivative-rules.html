    Section 2.3 Basic Differentiation Rules

    Objectives

    • Interpret derivative notation in both Newton form \(f'(x)\) and Leibniz form \(\displaystyle \frac{df}{dx}\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Use basic derivative formulas to differentiate power functions and constants.
      üîó
    • Apply the sum, constant multiple, product, and quotient rules to compute derivatives algebraically.
      üîó
    • Interpret higher derivatives in an applied context (velocity and acceleration).
      üîó
    üîó
    So far, we have seen how real-world rate-of-change problems and geometric tangent-line problems lead to derivatives. In this section, we focus on computing derivatives efficiently by using algebraic rules, so we do not need to return to the limit definition every time.
    üîó

    Subsection Notation for the Derivative

    The derivative of a function \(f\) can be written in several equivalent ways. In Newton notation we write \(f'(x)\text{.}\) In Leibniz notation we write \(\displaystyle \frac{df}{dx}\text{,}\) which is meant to remind us of the definition of the derivative as a limit of difference quotients:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f'(x)=\frac{df}{dx}=\lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}. \end{equation*}
    Here \(\Delta x\) is a change in the input, and \(\Delta f\) is the corresponding change in the output: \(\Delta x=h\) and \(\Delta f=f(x+h)-f(x)\text{.}\) The notation \(\displaystyle \frac{df}{dx}\) does not literally mean ‚Äúa quotient of two tiny numbers.‚Äù It is a single symbol that represents a limit.
    üîó
    For example, if \(f(x)=x^2\text{,}\) then the derivative formula \(f'(x)=2x\) can be written as
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)=2x. \end{equation*}
    To emphasize the difference between ‚Äúthe derivative function‚Äù and ‚Äúthe derivative at a point,‚Äù we write \(\displaystyle f'(a)=\left.\frac{df}{dx}\right|_{x=a}\text{.}\) You may also see the notation \(Df\) for the derivative of \(f\text{,}\) so that \(f'(x)=Df(x)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection Basic Derivatives

    The strategy is the same as earlier limit techniques: we establish derivatives of basic functions, then build more complicated derivatives from those. The most important basic formulas are the following.
    üîó

    Theorem 2.3.1. Derivatives of Constant and Linear Functions.

    • If \(f(x) = c\) is a constant function, then \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(c) = 0\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • If \(f(x) = x\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x) = 1\text{.}\)
      üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    We use the limit definition of the derivative.
    üîó
    First, suppose \(f(x) = c\text{,}\) where \(c\) is a constant. By definition,
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(c) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f(x+h) = c\) and \(f(x) = c\text{,}\) the numerator is zero.
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(c) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{c - c}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{0}{h} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the derivative of a constant function is zero.
    üîó
    Next, suppose \(f(x) = x\text{.}\) Using the limit definition,
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h) - x}{h}. \end{equation*}
    Simplifying the numerator gives
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} 1 = 1. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the derivative of \(f(x) = x\) is equal to \(1\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Theorem 2.3.2. The Power Rule.

    Let \(p\) be any real number for which \(x^p\) is defined. If \(f(x) = x^p\text{,}\) then
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^p) = p x^{p-1}. \end{equation*}
    üîó

    Proof.

    We begin with a geometric intuition. For the function \(f(x)=x^2\text{,}\) increasing \(x\) by a small amount \(h = \Delta x\) produces a change in area \(\Delta f\) that is approximately \(2x\,\Delta x\text{.}\)
    üîó
    This suggests that
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) \approx \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} \approx 2x. \end{equation*}
    A similar idea applies to volume. For \(f(x)=x^3\text{,}\) the change in volume due to a small increase \(\Delta x\) is approximately \(3x^2\,\Delta x\text{,}\)
    üîó
    suggesting that \(f'(x) \approx 3x^2\text{.}\)
    üîó
    More generally, for the growth of an \(n\)-dimensional cube with side length \(x\text{,}\) this geometric reasoning suggests that \(f'(x)=n x^{n-1}\text{.}\) While this intuition cannot be visualized for higher dimensions, it motivates the algebraic proof that follows.
    üîó
    We now prove the Power Rule rigorously using the limit definition of the derivative. The constant and linear cases were proved earlier. We focus on the case \(f(x)=x^p\text{.}\)
    üîó
    The proof proceeds in stages, beginning with whole number powers. Throughout, we repeatedly use the identity
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} a^n - b^n = (a-b)\big(a^{n-1}+a^{n-2}b+a^{n-3}b^2+\cdots+b^{n-1}\big), \end{equation*}
    which holds for all positive integers \(n\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Step 1: Positive integer powers. Let \(p=n\) be a positive integer. Using the limit definition of the derivative,
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(x+h)^n-x^n}{h}. \end{equation*}
    Apply the factorization identity with \(a=x+h\) and \(b=x\text{.}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{(x+h)^n-x^n}{h} = \frac{(x+h-x)\big((x+h)^{n-1}+(x+h)^{n-2}x+\cdots+x^{n-1}\big)}{h} \end{equation*}
    Cancel the factor of \(h\) to obtain
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle (x+h)^{n-1}+(x+h)^{n-2}x+\cdots+x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    Taking the limit as \(h\to 0\) gives
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle x^{n-1}+x^{n-1}+\cdots+x^{n-1} = n x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, for positive integers \(n\text{,}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^n)=n x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    Step 2: Negative integer powers. Let \(p=-n\text{,}\) where \(n\) is a positive integer. Then \(x^p=\frac{1}{x^n}\text{.}\) In the derivative limit, we combine fractions and apply the same factorization identity with \(a=x\) and \(b=x+h\text{.}\)
    üîó
    After simplification, the resulting expression reduces to
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle -\frac{n x^{n-1}}{x^{2n}} = (-n)x^{-n-1}. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the Power Rule holds for negative integers as well.
    üîó
    Step 3: Rational powers. Let \(p=\frac{n}{m}\text{,}\) where \(m\) is a positive integer and \(n\) is any integer. Then
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} x^p = x^{n/m} = \sqrt[m]{x^n}. \end{equation*}
    In the derivative limit, the numerator has the form \(\sqrt[m]{(x+h)^n}-\sqrt[m]{x^n}\text{.}\) As in earlier work with cube roots, we multiply the numerator and denominator by
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} a^{m-1}+a^{m-2}b+\cdots+b^{m-1}, \end{equation*}
    where \(a=\sqrt[m]{(x+h)^n}\) and \(b=\sqrt[m]{x^n}\text{.}\) This eliminates the radicals and produces the numerator
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} a^m-b^m=(x+h)^n-x^n, \end{equation*}
    which we have already handled. Simplifying the resulting limit again yields \(p x^{p-1}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Therefore, the Power Rule holds for all rational exponents.
    üîó
    The formula \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^p)=p x^{p-1}\) is also valid for irrational exponents such as \(p=\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) but proving this rigorously requires more advanced theory.
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.3.5.

    Compute each derivative:
    üîó
    (a)
    \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^{10})\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    Apply the power rule with \(p=10\text{.}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^{10}) & = & 10x^{9} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    (b)
    \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    First rewrite the cube root using exponent notation, then apply the power rule.
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\sqrt[3]{x}\right) & = & \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(x^{1/3}\right) \\[0.8em] & = & \displaystyle \frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3} \\[0.8em] & = & \displaystyle \frac{1}{3\sqrt[3]{x^2}} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    (c)
    \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    Rewrite the reciprocal using a negative exponent, then apply the power rule.
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) & = & \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(x^{-1}\right) \\[0.8em] & = & - x^{-2} \\[0.8em] & = & -\dfrac{1}{x^2} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection Derivative Rules

    Once we know the derivatives of basic functions, we can differentiate combinations of functions using rules. Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable at \(x\text{.}\)
    üîó

    Theorem 2.3.6.

    • Sum Rule: \(\displaystyle (f(x)+g(x))'=f'(x)+g'(x)\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Difference Rule: \(\displaystyle (f(x)-g(x))'=f'(x)-g'(x)\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Constant Multiple Rule: \(\displaystyle (c\,f(x))'=c\,f'(x)\) for any constant \(c\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Product Rule: \(\displaystyle (f(x)g(x))'=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x)\text{.}\)
      üîó
    • Quotient Rule: \(\displaystyle \left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right)'=\frac{f'(x)g(x)-f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2}\text{,}\) where \(g(x)\neq 0\text{.}\)
      üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    üîó

    Remark 2.3.7.

    Warning: the derivative of a product is not the product of derivatives. In general, \(\displaystyle (f(x)g(x))'\neq f'(x)g'(x)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.3.8.

    Use the definition of the derivative to justify the Sum Rule: \(\displaystyle (f(x)+g(x))'=f'(x)+g'(x)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    Start with the limit definition and separate the difference quotient into two pieces:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle (f(x)+g(x))' \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(f(x+h)+g(x+h))-(f(x)+g(x))}{h} \\[1.1em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}+\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}\right) \\[1.1em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} +\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h} \\[1.1em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle f'(x)+g'(x). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This step uses limit laws for sums.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    The Product Rule can be motivated geometrically using an area model for a rectangle with side lengths \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\text{.}\)
    üîó

    Remark 2.3.10.

    When \(\Delta x=h\) is small, the changes \(\Delta f\) and \(\Delta g\) are small as well. The change in area is \(\Delta(f\cdot g)=(\Delta f)\,g(x)+f(x)(\Delta g)+(\Delta f)(\Delta g)\text{.}\) After dividing by \(\Delta x\) and taking \(\Delta x\to 0\text{,}\) the term involving \((\Delta f)(\Delta g)\) vanishes, producing \(\displaystyle (fg)'=f'g+fg'\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection Derivative Computations

    Example 2.3.11.

    Compute \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(\sqrt{x})\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    Write \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\) and use the power rule.
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(\sqrt{x}) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(x^{1/2}) \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.3.12.

    Compute \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}(\sqrt{10})\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    \(\sqrt{10}\) is a constant, so its derivative is \(0\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.3.13.

    Let \(f(x)=(5x^2+1)(\sqrt{x}-3)\text{.}\) Compute \(f'(x)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    Use the Product Rule with \(u(x)=5x^2+1\) and \(v(x)=\sqrt{x}-3\text{.}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f'(x) \amp=\amp \displaystyle u'(x)v(x)+u(x)v'(x) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle (10x)(\sqrt{x}-3)+(5x^2+1)\left(\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\right). \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (Optional) You can also expand first and then differentiate term-by-term. Both approaches agree.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.3.14.

    Differentiate \(g(t)=\dfrac{t^5+1}{t\sqrt{t}}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Solution.
    First rewrite the denominator: \(t\sqrt{t}=t^{3/2}\text{.}\) Then \(\displaystyle g(t)=(t^5+1)t^{-3/2}=t^{7/2}+t^{-3/2}\text{,}\) so
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle g'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dt}\left(t^{7/2}+t^{-3/2}\right) \\[0.9em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{7}{2}t^{5/2}-\frac{3}{2}t^{-5/2}. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    You could also use the Quotient Rule directly; both methods produce equivalent results.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.3.15.

    A block of ice has length \(\ell=10\) cm, width \(w=5\) cm, and height \(h=20\) cm. Its length and width are decreasing at \(1\) cm/hr, and its height is decreasing at \(2\) cm/hr. How fast is the volume decreasing at that instant?
    üîó
    Solution.
    The volume is \(V=\ell w h\text{,}\) and all three dimensions depend on time \(t\text{.}\) Differentiate using the Product Rule (applied twice):
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{dV}{dt} \amp=\amp \displaystyle \frac{d}{dt}(\ell w h) \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle \ell'wh+\ell w'h+\ell w h'. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    At the instant described: \(\ell=10\text{,}\) \(w=5\text{,}\) \(h=20\text{,}\) \(\ell'=-1\text{,}\) \(w'=-1\text{,}\) \(h'=-2\) (cm/hr).
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle \frac{dV}{dt} \amp=\amp \displaystyle (-1)(5)(20)+(10)(-1)(20)+(10)(5)(-2) \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle -100-200-100 \\[0.8em] \amp=\amp \displaystyle -400. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The volume is decreasing at \(400\) cm3/hr.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection Higher Derivatives

    Because the derivative of a function is itself a function, we can differentiate again. The second derivative of \(f\) is denoted \(f''(x)\text{,}\) and in Leibniz notation it is written \(\displaystyle \frac{d^2f}{dx^2}\text{:}\)
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle f''(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(f'(x)\right)=\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{df}{dx}\right)=\frac{d^2f}{dx^2}. \end{equation*}
    If \(f\) represents position as a function of time, then \(f'(t)\) is velocity and \(f''(t)\) is acceleration.
    üîó

    Example 2.3.16.

    A stone falls \(f(t)=16t^2\) feet in \(t\) seconds. Compute \(f'(t)\text{,}\) \(f''(t)\text{,}\) and interpret them.
    üîó
    Solution.
    Differentiate repeatedly using the power rule:
    üîó
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \displaystyle f'(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle (16t^2)' =32t, \\[0.8em] \displaystyle f''(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle (32t)' =32, \\[0.8em] \displaystyle f'''(t) \amp=\amp \displaystyle (32)' =0. \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Here \(f'(t)=32t\) is velocity (ft/sec), increasing with time. The constant \(f''(t)=32\) is acceleration (ft/sec2), and \(f'''(t)=0\) means the acceleration is not changing.
    üîó
