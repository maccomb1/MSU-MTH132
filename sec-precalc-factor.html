    Section 0.2 Factoring

    Factoring is one of the most important algebra skills for calculus. Many problems in limits, derivatives, and integrals require expressions to be simplified before any calculus ideas can be applied. In this section, we review the most common factoring techniques, starting from very basic patterns and gradually building toward the types of expressions that appear in calculus problems.
    🔗

    Subsection Factoring Out a Greatest Common Factor

    The first and most important factoring technique is factoring out the greatest common factor. If every term in an expression shares a common factor, that factor can be factored out.
    🔗

    Principle 0.2.1.

    If every term of an expression contains a factor \(a\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} ab + ac & = & a(b + c) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 0.2.2.

    Factor \(6x + 12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Both terms share a factor of \(6\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 6x + 12 & = & 6(x + 2) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.3.

    Factor \(4x^2 + 8x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Both terms share a factor of \(4x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 4x^2 + 8x & = & 4x(x + 2) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.4.

    Factor \(-3x^3 + 6x^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Both terms share a factor of \(3x^2\text{.}\) Factoring out a negative sign is often helpful.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} -3x^3 + 6x^2 & = & -3x^2(x - 2) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 0.2.5.

    Always check for a greatest common factor first. Many factoring mistakes come from skipping this step.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Difference of Squares

    One of the most common factoring patterns is the difference of squares. This pattern appears frequently in limits and rational expressions.
    🔗

    Principle 0.2.6.

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} a^2 - b^2 & = & (a - b)(a + b) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 0.2.7.

    Factor \(x^2 - 16\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Recognize \(16 = 4^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} x^2 - 16 & = & (x - 4)(x + 4) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.8.

    Factor \(9x^2 - 25\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Rewrite each term as a square.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 9x^2 - 25 & = & (3x)^2 - 5^2 \\ & = & (3x - 5)(3x + 5) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.9.

    Simplify \(\dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}\) for \(x \neq 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Factor the numerator.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} \dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} & = & \dfrac{(x - 3)(x + 3)}{x - 3} \\[0.6em] & = & x + 3 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 0.2.10.

    The sum of squares \(a^2 + b^2\) does not factor over the real numbers.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Perfect Square Trinomials

    Some trinomials arise from squaring a binomial. Recognizing these patterns allows quick factoring.
    🔗

    Principle 0.2.11.

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} a^2 + 2ab + b^2 & = & (a + b)^2 \\[0.6em] a^2 - 2ab + b^2 & = & (a - b)^2 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 0.2.12.

    Factor \(x^2 + 6x + 9\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The middle term matches \(2ab\) with \(a = x\) and \(b = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} x^2 + 6x + 9 & = & (x + 3)^2 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.13.

    Factor \(x^2 - 10x + 25\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} x^2 - 10x + 25 & = & (x - 5)^2 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Factoring General Trinomials Using the AC Method

    Not all trinomials factor easily by inspection. For expressions of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\text{,}\) the AC method provides a systematic way to factor. This method is especially useful when the leading coefficient is not equal to 1, and it works equally well when it is.
    🔗

    Strategy 0.2.14.

    To factor \(ax^2 + bx + c\) using the AC method:
    🔗
    1. Compute the product \(ac\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Find two numbers whose product is \(ac\) and whose sum is \(b\text{.}\)
      🔗
    3. Rewrite the middle term using these two numbers.
      🔗
    4. Factor by grouping.
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.15.

    Factor \(x^2 + 5x + 6\) using the AC method.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Identify \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} a = 1, & b = 5, & c = 6 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Step 2: Compute \(ac\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} ac = 1 \cdot 6 = 6. \end{equation*}
    Step 3: Find two numbers that multiply to \(6\) and add to \(5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2 \quad \text{and} \quad 3 \end{equation*}
    Step 4: Rewrite the middle term and factor by grouping.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} x^2 + 5x + 6 & = & x^2 + 2x + 3x + 6 \\[0.6em] & = & x(x + 2) + 3(x + 2) \\[0.6em] & = & (x + 2)(x + 3) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 0.2.16.

    Factor \(x^2 - x - 12\) using the AC method.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Identify \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} a = 1, & b = -1, & c = -12 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Step 2: Compute \(ac\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} ac = 1 \cdot (-12) = -12. \end{equation*}
    Step 3: Find two numbers that multiply to \(-12\) and add to \(-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} -4 \quad \text{and} \quad 3 \end{equation*}
    Step 4: Rewrite the middle term and factor by grouping.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} x^2 - x - 12 & = & x^2 - 4x + 3x - 12 \\[0.6em] & = & x(x - 4) + 3(x - 4) \\[0.6em] & = & (x - 4)(x + 3) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 0.2.17.

    The AC method always works when a trinomial factors over the real numbers. With practice, you may recognize factors quickly, but this method provides a dependable fallback when guessing is difficult.
