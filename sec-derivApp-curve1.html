    Section 3.6 Curve Sketching Part 1

    Objectives

    • Determine where a function is increasing or decreasing using the sign of \(f'(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Use the First Derivative Test to classify critical points as local maxima, local minima, or neither.
      🔗
    • Determine concavity using the sign of \(f''(x)\) and identify inflection points.
      🔗
    • Combine derivative information with intercepts and asymptotes to sketch a reasonable graph.
      🔗
    🔗

    Warning 3.6.1. Prerequisite Review.

    A key skill needed for curve sketching is the ability to solve equations and inequalities involving derivatives. If you feel a bit out of practice with your inequality solving techniques, it may be helpful to revisit those ideas before diving into the calculus.
    • Review section on inequalities 
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Derivatives do more than provide slopes at individual points. They also reveal the overall shape of a graph. In this section, we build a toolkit for sketching the graph of \(y=f(x)\) by analyzing \(f'(x)\) and \(f''(x)\text{.}\) The goal is not a perfect drawing, but a reliable picture that captures the key features.
    🔗

    Subsection Increasing and Decreasing

    Definition 3.6.2. Increasing and Decreasing.

    A function \(f\) is increasing on an interval \([a,b]\) if \(f(x_1)<f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1<x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\) A function \(f\) is decreasing on \([a,b]\) if \(f(x_1)>f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1<x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The derivative provides an efficient test: the graph rises where slopes are positive and falls where slopes are negative.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.3. Increasing/Decreasing Test.

    Let \(f\) be continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is increasing on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)<0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is decreasing on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Assume \(f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) and choose any \(x_1<x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\) By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(c\in(x_1,x_2)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(c)=\frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(x_2-x_1>0\) and \(f'(c)>0\text{,}\) we have \(f(x_2)-f(x_1)>0\text{,}\) so \(f(x_2)>f(x_1)\text{.}\) Therefore \(f\) is increasing on \([a,b]\text{.}\) The decreasing case is similar.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.4.

    Let \(f(x)=x^5-15x^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Identify all critical points of \(f\) and determine where it is increasing and decreasing.
    🔗
    (b) Use your results from (a) to plot a sketch of the graph of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) Use your sketch from (b) to classify the critical points as local maxima, minima, or neither.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Video Solution
    Here is a video solution to this problem.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Written Solution
    (a) Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=5x^4-45x^2=5x^2(x^2-9)=5x^2(x-3)(x+3). \end{equation*}
    The critical points occur where \(f'(x)=0\) (since it is never undefined), so \(x=-3,0,3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since we will need to plot the graph, we should also compute the \(y\)-coordinates of the critical points:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f(-3) & = & (-3)^5-15(-3)^3 = 162\\ f(0) & = & 0^5-15\cdot 0^3 = 0\\ f(3) & = & 3^5-15\cdot 3^3 = -162 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now we will use the test point method to determine where \(f'(x) \gt 0\) (i.e., where the function is increasing) and where \(f'(x) \lt 0\) (i.e., where the function is decreasing).
    🔗
    Choose test points in each of the intervals determined by the critical points:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{Interval} & \text{Test Point} & \text{Sign of } f'(x) \\ \hline (-\infty,-3) & -4 & (+)(-)(-)\to(+) \\ \hline (-3,0) & -1 & (+)(-)(+)\to(-) \\ \hline (0,3) & 1 & (+)(-)(+)\to(-) \\ \hline (3,\infty) & 4 & (+)(+)(+)\to(+) \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Remember: That the value of the derivative at the test point is not important; only the sign matters. The sign of \(f'(x)\) is determined by the signs of the factors \(5x^2\text{,}\) \((x-3)\text{,}\) and \((x+3)\) (hence the short-hand notation in the table above).
    🔗
    A sign chart is summarized below.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|c|c|c} x & -4 & -3 & -1 & 0 & 1 & 3 & 4 \\ \hline f'(x) & (+) & 0 & (-) & 0 & (-) & 0 & (+) \\ \hline f(x) & \nearrow & 162 & \searrow & 0 & \searrow & -162 & \nearrow \end{array} \end{equation*}
    (b) To sketch the graph we can first plot the critical points and then use the increasing/decreasing behavior to connect them.
    🔗
    Here is a good place to start
    🔗
    And here is a good final sketch. Remember at the critical points the slope is zero so the graph should flatten out near these points. Also polynomial functions are differentiable everywhere so we should not have any sharp corners or cusps.
    🔗
    (c) The function changes from increasing to decreasing at \(x=-3\text{,}\) so there is a local maximum at \(x=-3\text{.}\) The function changes from decreasing to increasing at \(x=3\text{,}\) so there is a local minimum at \(x=3\text{.}\) At \(x=0\text{,}\) the derivative is zero but the function is decreasing on both sides, so \(x=0\) is neither a local maximum nor a local minimum.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s formalize the classification of critical points like we did in the last example using the first derivative.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.7. First Derivative Test.

    Let \(f\) be differentiable on an interval around \(c\) and suppose \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f'(x)>0\) for \(x<c\) and \(f'(x)<0\) for \(x>c\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local maximum.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)<0\) for \(x<c\) and \(f'(x)>0\) for \(x>c\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local minimum.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)\) has the same sign on both sides of \(c\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is neither a local maximum nor a local minimum.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    So with only the first derivative, we can determine where a function is increasing or decreasing and classify critical points as local maxima, minima, or neither. However, there are other features of the graph that we can analyze using the second derivative.
    🔗

    Subsection Concavity and Inflection Points

    Increasing and decreasing behavior describes whether the graph rises or falls. Concavity describes how the graph bends.
    🔗

    Definition 3.6.8.

    A graph is concave up when it looks like a smile, the curve is bending upwards. The graph is concave down when it looks like a frown, the curve is bending downwards. An inflection point is a point where the concavity changes.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.6.9.

    Concavity is independent of increasing/decreasing behavior. A graph can be increasing and concave down at the same time, for example. Here are some examples of graphs with different combinations of increasing/decreasing and concavity.
    🔗
    🔗
    A helpful way to connect this to derivatives is to think about slopes. When a function is concave up, its slopes tend to increase as \(x\) increases. When a function is concave down, its slopes tend to decrease.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.11. Concavity Test.

    Let \(f\) be twice differentiable on an interval \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f''(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is concave up on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(x)<0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is concave down on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(c)=0\) and \(f''\) changes sign at \(x=c\text{,}\) then \((c,f(c))\) is an inflection point.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Apply the Increasing/Decreasing Test to \(g(x)=f'(x)\text{.}\) If \(g'(x)>0\text{,}\) then \(g\) is increasing. Since \(g'(x)=(f'(x))'=f''(x)\text{,}\) the condition \(f''(x)>0\) means \(f'(x)\) is increasing, which corresponds to concave up behavior. The concave down case is similar, and the inflection point criterion follows from a sign change in \(f''\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.12.

    Continue with \(f(x)=x^5-15x^3\text{.}\) Find where the graph is concave up or concave down, and identify inflection points.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Video Solution
    Here is a video solution to this problem.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Written Solution
    We already computed \(f'(x)=5x^4-45x^2\text{.}\) Differentiate again:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f''(x)=20x^3-90x=10x(2x^2-9). \end{equation*}
    Candidate inflection points occur where \(f''(x)=0\) or where \(f''(x)\) is undefined (while \(f\) is defined). However since \(f''(x)\) is defined for all real \(x\text{,}\) we only consider where \(f''(x)=0\text{.}\) So either \(10x=0 \implies x=0\) or \(2x^2-9=0\text{,}\) which gives \(x=\pm\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Remember that \(\sqrt{2}\approx 1.4\) so the inflection points are approximately at \(x=-2.1\text{,}\) \(x=0\text{,}\) and \(x=2.1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now we will use the test point method to determine where \(f''(x) \gt 0\) (i.e., where the function is concave up) and where \(f''(x) \lt 0\) (i.e., where the function is concave down).
    🔗
    Choose test points in each of the intervals and check the sign of \(f''(x)=(2x^2-9)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{Interval} & \text{Test Point} & \text{Sign of } f''(x) \\ \hline (-\infty,-\tfrac{3}{\sqrt{2}}) & -4 & (-)(+) \to (-) \\ \hline (-\tfrac{3}{\sqrt{2}},0) & -1 & (-)(-)\to(+) \\ \hline (0,\tfrac{3}{\sqrt{2}}) & 1 & (+)(-)\to(-) \\ \hline (\tfrac{3}{\sqrt{2}},\infty) & 4 & (+)(+)\to(+) \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So therefore \(f\) is concave down on \((-\infty,-\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}) \cup (0,\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}})\text{,}\) and concave up on \((-\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}},0) \cup (\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}},\infty)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Because \(f''\) changes sign at each of the three candidates, there are inflection points at \(x=-\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}\text{,}\) \(x=0\text{,}\) and \(x=\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s illustrate these ideas with a more complicated example that combines all of the features we have discussed so far.
    🔗
    This upcoming example will also utilize vertical asymptotes from Locating Vertical Asymptotes  as well as horizontal asymptotes from Calculating Horizontal Asymptotes  back in Chapter 1.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.13. Longer Example.

    Consider the function \(f(x)=x+\dfrac{1}{x+1}-\dfrac{3}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (a) Identify intercepts, vertical asymptotes, and horizontal asymptotes of the graph of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) Locate critical points and intervals of increase/decrease.
    🔗
    (c) Determine concavity and inflection points.
    🔗
    (d) Use your results from (a)-(c) to sketch the graph of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution 1. Video Solution
    Here is a video solution to this problem.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Written Solution
    (a) The \(y\)-intercept occurs at \(f(0)=\frac{-1}{2}\text{.}\) Setting \(f(x)=0\) and solving for \(x\text{,}\) we find:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 0 & = & x+\dfrac{1}{x+1}-\dfrac{3}{2} \\ 2(x+1) (0) & = & 2(x+1) ( x+\dfrac{1}{x+1} - \dfrac{3}{2} ) \\ 0 & = & 2x(x+1) + 2 - 3(x+1) \\ 0 & = & 2x^2 + 2x + 2 - 3x - 3 \\ 0 & = & 2x^2 - x - 1 \\ 0 & = & (2x+1)(x-1) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    so the \(x\)-intercepts are at \(x=-\frac{1}{2}\) and \(x=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is a vertical asymptote at \(x=-1\) since the denominator of the rational part is zero there and the numerator is not. To find horizontal asymptotes, we compute limits as \(x\to\pm\infty\text{.}\) We can use dominant term analysis to find that
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\pm\infty} f(x) \approx \lim_{x\to\pm\infty} x = \pm\infty \end{equation*}
    so there are no horizontal asymptotes.
    🔗
    (b) To find critical points, we first compute the derivative:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = 1 - \dfrac{1}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{(x+1)^2 - 1}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}. \end{equation*}
    The critical points occur where \(f'(x)=0\) or where \(f'(x)\) is undefined (while \(f\) is defined). Since \(f'(x)\) only undefined when \(x=-1\) (since the denominator is zero there), and \(f\) is undefined at \(x=-1\text{,}\) we only consider where \(f'(x)=0\text{.}\) Setting the derivative equal to zero and solving for \(x\text{,}\) we find:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 0 & = & \dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2} \\ 0 & = & x^2+2x \\ 0 & = & x(x+2) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    so the critical points are at \(x=0\) and \(x=-2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To determine where \(f\) is increasing or decreasing, we can use the test point method to check the sign of \(f'(x)\) in each interval determined by the critical points and vertical asymptote:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{Interval} & \text{Test Point} & \text{Sign of } f'(x) \\ \hline (-\infty,-2) & -3 & \dfrac{(-)(-)}{(+)} \to (+) \\ \hline (-2,-1) & -\frac{3}{2} & \dfrac{(-)(+)}{(+)} \to (-) \\ \hline (-1,0) & -\frac{1}{2} & \dfrac{(-)(+)}{(+)} \to (-) \\ \hline (0,\infty) & 1 & \dfrac{(+)(+)}{(+)} \to (+) \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So \(f\) is increasing on \((-\infty,-2) \cup (0,\infty)\) and decreasing on \((-2,-1) \cup (-1,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) To determine concavity, we compute the second derivative:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} f''(x) & = & \left(1 - \dfrac{1}{(x+1)^2}\right)' \\ & = & 0 - \dfrac{2}{(x+1)^3} \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Candidate inflection points occur where \(f''(x)=0\) or where \(f''(x)\) is undefined (while \(f\) is defined). Since \(f''(x)\) is undefined at \(x=-1\text{,}\) we do not consider that point since \(f\) is also undefined there. Setting the second derivative equal to zero and solving for \(x\text{,}\) we find:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rcl} 0 & = & \dfrac{-2}{(x+1)^3} \\ 0 & = & -2 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Since there is no value of \(x\) that makes \(f''(x)=0\text{,}\) there are no inflection points.
    🔗
    To determine where \(f\) is concave up or concave down, we can use the test point method to check the sign of \(f''(x)\) in each interval determined by the vertical asymptote:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{Interval} & \text{Test Point} & \text{Sign of } f''(x) \\ \hline (-\infty,-1) & -2 & \dfrac{-2}{(-)} \to (+) \\ \hline (-1,\infty) & 0 & \dfrac{-2}{(+)} \to (-) \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    So \(f\) is concave up on \((-\infty,-1)\) and concave down on \((-1,\infty)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (d) We can now combine all of this information to sketch the graph of \(f\text{.}\) First I would plot the intercepts, asymptotes, and critical points. Then I would use the intervals of increase/decrease and concavity to determine the shape of the graph in each region.
    🔗
    Now we can use the information from the sign chart, asymptotes and critical points to connect all the key points into a coherent sketch of the graph.

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Some True/False Problems.

    Determine whether each statement is true or false and briefly justify your reasoning.
    🔗
    1.
    If \(f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\) in an interval \((a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is increasing on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This is exactly the Increasing/Decreasing Test.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    If \(f'(3)=0\text{,}\) then \(f\) has a local maximum or a local minimum at \(x=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. A critical point can be neither. For example, consider \(f(x)=x^3\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    If \(f'(x)\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(x=2\text{,}\) then \(f\) has a local minimum at \(x=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This is the First Derivative Test for a local minimum.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    4.
    If \(f''(x)>0\) on an interval, then the slopes of \(f\) are increasing on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. \(f''(x)>0\) means \(f'(x)\) is increasing.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    If \(f\) is increasing on an interval, then it must be concave up on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. A function can be increasing and concave down.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    6.
    If \(f\) is concave down on an interval, then \(f'(x)\) is decreasing on that interval.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Concave down means \(f''(x)<0\text{,}\) so \(f'(x)\) decreases.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    If \(f''(5)=0\text{,}\) then \((5,f(5))\) must be an inflection point.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. You also need \(f''\) to change sign at \(x=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    8.
    It is possible for \(f\) to have an inflection point at \(x=-1\) even if \(f'(-1)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. Inflection points concern concavity; the slope can be zero there.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    9.
    If \(f''(x)\) is undefined at \(x=4\text{,}\) then \(x=4\) cannot be an inflection point.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. An inflection point can occur where \(f''\) is undefined, as long as concavity changes and \(f\) is defined.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    10.
    Consider the graph of \(f\) below. On this graph \(f'(2) \gt f''(2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. The slope at \(x=2\) is positive (the curve is increasing), so therefore \(f'(2) \gt 0\text{.}\) The concavity is downward (the curve is bending down), so \(f''(2) \lt 0\text{.}\) Therefore, \(f'(2) \gt f''(2)\text{.}\)
