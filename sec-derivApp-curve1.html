    Section 3.6 Curve Sketching Part 1

    Objectives

    • Determine where a function is increasing or decreasing using the sign of \(f'(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Use the First Derivative Test to classify critical points as local maxima, local minima, or stationary points.
      🔗
    • Determine concavity using the sign of \(f''(x)\) and identify inflection points.
      🔗
    • Use the Second Derivative Test to classify a critical point when applicable.
      🔗
    • Combine derivative information with intercepts and asymptotes to sketch a reasonable graph.
      🔗
    🔗
    Derivatives do more than provide slopes at individual points. They also reveal the overall shape of a graph. In this section, we build a toolkit for sketching the graph of \(y=f(x)\) by analyzing \(f'(x)\) and \(f''(x)\text{.}\) The goal is not a perfect drawing, but a reliable picture that captures the key features.
    🔗

    Subsection Increasing and Decreasing

    Definition 3.6.1. Increasing and Decreasing.

    A function \(f\) is increasing on an interval \([a,b]\) if \(f(x_1)<f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1<x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\) A function \(f\) is decreasing on \([a,b]\) if \(f(x_1)>f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1<x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The derivative provides an efficient test: the graph rises where slopes are positive and falls where slopes are negative.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.2. Increasing/Decreasing Test.

    Let \(f\) be continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is increasing on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)<0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is decreasing on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Assume \(f'(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) and choose any \(x_1<x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{.}\) By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(c\in(x_1,x_2)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(c)=\frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(x_2-x_1>0\) and \(f'(c)>0\text{,}\) we have \(f(x_2)-f(x_1)>0\text{,}\) so \(f(x_2)>f(x_1)\text{.}\) Therefore \(f\) is increasing on \([a,b]\text{.}\) The decreasing case is similar.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.3.

    Let \(f(x)=x^5-15x^3\text{.}\) Determine where \(f\) is increasing and decreasing, and use this information to identify local extrema.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Differentiate:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=5x^4-45x^2=5x^2(x^2-9)=5x^2(x-3)(x+3). \end{equation*}
    The critical points occur where \(f'(x)=0\text{,}\) so \(x=-3,0,3\text{.}\) To determine the sign of \(f'(x)\) on the intervals between critical points, we test one value in each interval.
    🔗
    Since \(5x^2\ge 0\text{,}\) the sign of \(f'(x)\) is controlled by \((x-3)(x+3)\) except that \(x=0\) contributes a zero but does not change sign.
    🔗
    A sign chart is summarized below.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|c|c|c} x & \quad & -3 & \quad & 0 & \quad & 3 & \quad \\ \hline f'(x) & + & 0 & - & 0 & - & 0 & + \\ \hline f(x) & \nearrow & & \searrow & & \searrow & & \nearrow \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The function changes from increasing to decreasing at \(x=-3\text{,}\) so there is a local maximum at \(x=-3\text{.}\) The function changes from decreasing to increasing at \(x=3\text{,}\) so there is a local minimum at \(x=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At \(x=0\text{,}\) the derivative is zero but the function is decreasing on both sides, so \(x=0\) is a stationary point rather than a local extremum.
    🔗
    Remark 3.6.4.
    Since \(f(x)=x^5-15x^3\) contains only odd powers, it satisfies \(f(-x)=-f(x)\text{.}\) This means the graph has 180-degree rotational symmetry about the origin.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.5. First Derivative Test.

    Let \(f\) be differentiable on an interval around \(c\) and suppose \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f'(x)>0\) for \(x<c\) and \(f'(x)<0\) for \(x>c\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local maximum.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)<0\) for \(x<c\) and \(f'(x)>0\) for \(x>c\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local minimum.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f'(x)\) has the same sign on both sides of \(c\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a stationary point (not a local extremum).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Concavity and Inflection Points

    Increasing and decreasing behavior describes whether the graph rises or falls. Concavity describes how the graph bends. A graph is concave up when it looks like a smile, and concave down when it looks like a frown. An inflection point is a point where the concavity changes.
    🔗
    A helpful way to connect this to derivatives is to think about slopes. When a function is concave up, its slopes tend to increase as \(x\) increases. When a function is concave down, its slopes tend to decrease.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.6. Concavity Test.

    Let \(f\) be twice differentiable on an interval \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f''(x)>0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is concave up on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(x)<0\) for all \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is concave down on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(c)=0\) and \(f''\) changes sign at \(x=c\text{,}\) then \((c,f(c))\) is an inflection point.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Apply the Increasing/Decreasing Test to \(g(x)=f'(x)\text{.}\) If \(g'(x)>0\text{,}\) then \(g\) is increasing. Since \(g'(x)=(f'(x))'=f''(x)\text{,}\) the condition \(f''(x)>0\) means \(f'(x)\) is increasing, which corresponds to concave up behavior. The concave down case is similar, and the inflection point criterion follows from a sign change in \(f''\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.7.

    Continue with \(f(x)=x^5-15x^3\text{.}\) Find where the graph is concave up or concave down, and identify inflection points.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We already computed \(f'(x)=5x^4-45x^2\text{.}\) Differentiate again:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f''(x)=20x^3-90x=10x(2x^2-9). \end{equation*}
    Candidate inflection points occur where \(f''(x)=0\text{,}\) so \(x=0\) and \(2x^2-9=0\text{,}\) which gives \(x=\pm\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A sign check for \(f''(x)\) shows that concavity changes at each of these points, so they are inflection points. In particular, the graph is concave down for large negative \(x\text{,}\) then concave up between \(-\frac{3}{\sqrt2}\) and \(0\text{,}\) then concave down between \(0\) and \(\frac{3}{\sqrt2}\text{,}\) and concave up for large positive \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.6.8. Second Derivative Test.

    Let \(f\) be twice differentiable near \(c\text{,}\) and suppose \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • If \(f''(c)<0\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local maximum.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(c)>0\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local minimum.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(f''(c)=0\text{,}\) then this test is inconclusive.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.6.9.

    The Second Derivative Test is convenient when it applies, but it does not replace the First Derivative Test. If \(f''(c)=0\text{,}\) the point might still be a maximum, a minimum, or neither.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A Longer Example

    Example 3.6.10.

    Sketch the graph of \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^{2/3}}{(x-1)^2}\text{.}\) Identify intercepts, critical points, intervals of increase/decrease, concavity, inflection points, and any vertical asymptotes.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The function is defined for all real \(x\) except \(x=1\text{.}\) Also note \(f(0)=0\text{,}\) so the graph passes through the origin.
    🔗
    Using derivative rules (and simplifying), we obtain:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x)=-\frac{\tfrac{2}{3}(2x+1)}{(x-1)^3x^{1/3}}, \qquad f''(x)=-\frac{\tfrac{2}{9}(14x^2+14x-1)}{(x-1)^4x^{4/3}}. \end{equation*}
    Critical points occur where \(f'(x)=0\) or where \(f'(x)\) is undefined (while \(f\) is defined). The equation \(f'(x)=0\) is satisfied when \(2x+1=0\text{,}\) so \(x=-\frac12\text{.}\) The derivative is undefined at \(x=0\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) Since \(f(1)\) is undefined, \(x=1\) is a vertical asymptote of the function. Since \(f(0)\) is defined but the derivative is not, \(x=0\) is a critical point.
    🔗
    A sign analysis for \(f'(x)\) shows:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|c|c} x & \quad & -\frac12 & \quad & 0 & \quad & 1 \ \ \ \\ \hline f'(x) & + & 0 & - & \text{undef} & + & \text{undef} \ \ - \\ \hline f(x) & \nearrow & & \searrow & & \nearrow & \text{VA} \ \searrow \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Therefore \(x=-\frac12\) is a local maximum. At \(x=0\text{,}\) the graph has a cusp: the function reaches a local minimum at the origin, but the tangent line is vertical rather than horizontal. At \(x=1\text{,}\) there is a vertical asymptote, and the function grows without bound as \(x\to 1^\pm\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Inflection points occur where \(f''(x)=0\) and concavity changes. Solve \(14x^2+14x-1=0\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x=\frac{-14\pm\sqrt{14^2-4(14)(-1)}}{2(14)} =\frac{-14\pm 6\sqrt7}{28} =\frac{-7\pm 3\sqrt7}{14}. \end{equation*}
    These values (approximately \(-1.07\) and \(0.07\)) are candidates, and checking concavity confirms that the concavity changes at both, so they are inflection points.
    🔗
    Finally, as \(|x|\to\infty\text{,}\) the factors \(x\) and \(x-1\) are nearly the same, so we compare with
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{x^{2/3}}{(x-1)^2}\approx\frac{x^{2/3}}{x^2}=x^{-4/3}. \end{equation*}
    This shows \(f(x)\to 0\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\text{.}\) Combining these features produces a reliable sketch.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A Method for Curve Sketching

    Strategy 3.6.11. Curve Sketching Checklist.

    1. Compute \(f'(x)\) and \(f''(x)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Find critical points from \(f'(x)=0\) and where \(f'(x)\) is undefined.
      🔗
    3. Use the sign of \(f'(x)\) to determine where the function increases or decreases and to classify critical points.
      🔗
    4. Find possible inflection points from \(f''(x)=0\) and where \(f''(x)\) is undefined.
      🔗
    5. Use the sign of \(f''(x)\) to determine concavity and confirm inflection points.
      🔗
    6. Find intercepts and identify any vertical asymptotes from the domain.
      🔗
    7. Describe end behavior as \(x\to\pm\infty\) (we will develop stronger tools for this soon).
      🔗
    8. Check for symmetry when it is helpful (\(f(-x)=f(x)\) or \(f(-x)=-f(x)\)).
      🔗
    9. Combine these features into a clear sketch.
      🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.6.12.

    We will return to end behavior and asymptotic comparisons in more detail in the next part of curve sketching.

    Exercises Practice / Study Problems

    Conceptual Questions.

    1.
    True or False: If \(f'(c)=0\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) must be a local maximum or minimum.
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. The point could be a stationary point where the function does not change direction.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    2.
    True or False: If \(f''(c)>0\) and \(f'(c)=0\text{,}\) then \(x=c\) is a local minimum.
    🔗
    Solution.
    True. This is the Second Derivative Test.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    3.
    True or False: An inflection point must occur where \(f''(c)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    False. \(f''(c)=0\) is a common candidate, but an inflection point can occur where \(f''\) is undefined as well.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Algebraic Practice.

    4.
    Let \(f(x)=x^4-4x^2\text{.}\) Find the critical points, intervals of increase/decrease, concavity, and inflection points. Then sketch a graph.
    🔗
    🔗
    5.
    Let \(f(x)=\dfrac{x}{x^2+1}\text{.}\) Find where \(f\) is increasing or decreasing and where it is concave up or concave down. Then sketch a graph.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Graphical and Interpretation Problems.

    6.
    A differentiable function has critical points at \(x=-2\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) The sign of \(f'(x)\) is positive on \((-\infty,-2)\text{,}\) negative on \((-2,1)\text{,}\) and positive on \((1,\infty)\text{.}\) Describe the behavior of the graph and classify the critical points.
    🔗
    🔗
    7.
    The second derivative satisfies \(f''(x)<0\) for \(x<3\) and \(f''(x)>0\) for \(x>3\text{.}\) What does this say about concavity and inflection points?
